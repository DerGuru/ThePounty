# Chapter 10: The Deliveries

Gerald noticed the first package on a Monday. Small. Brown cardboard. No Amazon logo, no FedEx branding. Plain. The UPS driver left it on Aldric's porch at 10:42 AM. Gerald wrote it down because he wrote everything down, but he didn't think much of it.

The second one came Wednesday. Same size, different shape. Flatter. Padded envelope, not a box. USPS this time. Kevin left it leaning against the door. Gerald was eating lunch at his desk and saw it from the study window. He noted the time: 1:15 PM.

The third one came Friday. UPS again. Heavier, from the way the driver carried it. Gerald could tell weight by how a person shifted their grip walking up the steps.

Three deliveries in five days. That wasn't unusual for most people. Rosa got three a week from Amazon alone. Dave ordered tools. Sandra ordered everything.

But Aldric didn't get packages. In three months on Maple Lane, Gerald had seen exactly four deliveries to number four. He'd noted each one. This was a man who bought groceries in person, picked up his dry cleaning by hand and received almost nothing through the mail except the occasional letter. No catalogs. No subscription boxes. No online shopping patterns whatsoever.

Gerald opened his second notebook. The Aldric notebook. He turned to a fresh page.

*Week of August 26:*
*Mon 10:42 — UPS, small box, ~2lbs*
*Wed 13:15 — USPS, padded envelope, flat*
*Fri 10:08 — UPS, medium box, heavier (~5lbs?)*

He looked at it. Three points. Not a pattern yet. Three points were coincidence. He'd wait.

The next week brought four more.

Monday. Tuesday. Thursday. Saturday. The carriers rotated. UPS, USPS, FedEx and UPS again. None of the packages had visible branding. No return addresses Gerald could read from across the street, though he tried with the binoculars Sandra had given him for birdwatching. The binoculars had never seen a bird.

*Week of September 2:*
*Mon 11:30 — FedEx, box, medium*
*Tue 09:45 — USPS, small padded envelope*
*Thu 14:22 — UPS, long thin package (tube? rod?)*
*Sat 10:55 — UPS, box, heavy, driver used both hands*

Seven packages in twelve days. Aldric brought each one inside within minutes of delivery. No sitting on the porch, no leaving them by the door. He appeared, picked them up and went back in. Efficient. Like he was expecting each one.

That was the other thing. Gerald had been watching Aldric for three months. In that time, the man's evening routine had been fixed: dinner, porch, coffee, book, lamp off at ten-thirty. Every night. Regular as a clock mechanism.

Starting the first week of September, the porch was dark.

Not every night. Monday he was out there. Wednesday he wasn't. Thursday he wasn't. Friday he was, but only until nine, and then he went in and the basement light came on. Gerald could see it, a faint glow from the narrow windows at ground level on the side of number four that faced the Hoopers' house.

Gerald had never seen that light before.

He wrote: *Basement light. First observed Sept 4, 9:17 PM. Visible from study window. Stayed on until at least 11:40 (I went to bed). Duration: minimum 2h23m.*

Sandra noticed it too. She mentioned it at breakfast, Thursday of the second week.

"Aldric's been busy lately."

Gerald lowered the newspaper. "What makes you say that?"

"He hasn't been out on the porch. Haven't seen him at the mailbox either. Usually he waves." She stirred her coffee. "You think he's OK?"

"He's fine," Gerald said. "People get busy."

"You've been watching him."

"I watch the street."

"You watch him." Sandra took a sip. "Should I bring something over? A casserole? People bring casseroles when someone seems off."

"He doesn't seem off. He seems busy. There's a difference."

Sandra looked at him with that expression she used when she thought he was wrong but didn't want to argue before nine AM. She drank her coffee and dropped it.

Gerald went back to the paper. He didn't read it. He was thinking about the basement light.

The packages kept coming. Three more in the third week. Gerald's list now had ten entries across fourteen days. The carriers were mixed, the sizes varied, the timing was irregular. Nothing pointed to a single source. This wasn't a man ordering parts for a project from one supplier. This was multiple shipments from multiple origins, arriving on different schedules.

Gerald had seen something like this once before. His brother-in-law had built a home gym. Ordered pieces from twelve different suppliers over a month. Bench from one place, plates from another, rack from a third. By the end of it, UPS knew his address by heart.

Maybe that was it. Maybe Aldric was building something in the basement.

Gerald didn't believe that.

What Gerald believed was harder to articulate. He sat at his desk on a Thursday afternoon with the notebook open, the pen uncapped and the list of deliveries staring back at him. Outside, the street was quiet. Aldric's porch was empty. The basement light would come on around eight, stay on until Gerald couldn't keep his eyes open.

He wrote: *Observations, week 3.*

*1. Packages continue. 10 in 14 days. Multiple carriers, no branding, no visible returns.*
*2. Evening routine disrupted. Porch dark 4 of 7 nights. Basement light instead.*
*3. Still polite. Still waves. Still takes trash out Tuesday. Still mows. But: fewer conversations. Fewer lingering.*
*4. Sandra noticed. If Sandra noticed, it's real.*

Gerald capped the pen. He looked at point four. He uncapped the pen and added:

*5. He's preparing for something.*

He stared at that line. He didn't know where it came from. It was a feeling, not a fact, and feelings didn't belong in the notebook. The notebook was for observations. Verifiable data. Things he could point to and say: this happened, at this time, and I saw it.

He crossed out point five. Then he wrote it again, smaller, in the margin where it would be harder to see.

*He's preparing for something.*

Below, on the street, Aldric's front door opened. Gerald watched him come out, walk to the mailbox, take out a single envelope, look at it and put it in his back pocket. He stood at the mailbox for a moment. He looked up at the sky. Clear, blue, September afternoon. Then he looked at the Hoopers' house. At Gerald's study window.

Gerald didn't move. The blinds were angled. There was no way Aldric could see him from that distance, at that angle, with the afternoon sun reflecting off the glass.

Aldric raised one hand. A wave. Small, casual, friendly.

Then he went back inside.

Gerald sat very still for a long time. Then he wrote: *Sept 18, 2:47 PM. Venn waved at my window. Blinds were closed.*

He underlined it. He didn't know what it meant. He didn't like not knowing what things meant.

The basement light came on at eight. Gerald watched it until ten. Then he went to bed. He didn't sleep well.
