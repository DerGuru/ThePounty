# Chapter 20: Halloween

*Thursday, the 31st of October 2024*

Sandra had been decorating since breakfast. The fake cobwebs were up on the porch railings, stretched thin between the posts the way the YouTube tutorial had shown her. The plastic jack-o'-lanterns lined the walkway. The motion-activated skeleton by the door worked after Gerald tightened a connection she couldn't reach without the step stool.

"You've got enough candy?" Gerald asked from the living room. He was in his chair with the newspaper, not reading it.

"Three bags of mixed, two bags of Reese's, and the gummy eyeballs for the little ones." Sandra counted on her fingers. "Plus the backup bag in the closet."

"That's a lot of candy."

"It's Halloween, Gerald. It's exactly the right amount of candy."

She went to the front window and checked the street. The Ortegas had put out two carved pumpkins. Marcus's had four eyes, which was apparently a design choice and not a mistake. The Chens had orange string lights around their mailbox. She'd seen on her morning walk that even the Parkers had managed a wreath on their door, which for the Parkers was practically a decorating spree.

Number four had nothing.

No pumpkin. No lights. No cobwebs, fake or otherwise. Aldric's porch looked the same as it did every other day of the year. Two chairs. The small table. The lamp that came on at dusk.

Sandra had half a mind to go over there with a spare jack-o'-lantern. She had three in the garage. She didn't need all of them.

"He's probably not a holiday person," Gerald said without looking up.

"Everyone's a holiday person, Gerald. Some people just haven't been given permission yet."

"Or some people are focused on other things." Gerald turned a page. "Leave the man alone."

Sandra left the man alone. She went back to arranging the candy bowl and told herself that not everyone had to participate. It was fine. Aldric was allowed to sit this one out.

It still felt like a waste.

By five-thirty, the first clusters of kids appeared at the end of Maple Lane. Tiny ghosts, tiny superheroes, and one committed six-year-old in what appeared to be a self-made astronaut suit with a helmet that didn't quite fit. Sandra turned on the porch lights, positioned the candy bowl, and sat in her chair to wait.

Dave came out of his house at quarter to six with Marcus and Lina. Marcus was a Tyrannosaurus rex, naturally. The costume had a tail that dragged on the ground and a headpiece with teeth that kept sliding over his eyes. Lina was something dark and angular that Dave couldn't identify.

"What are you supposed to be?" he asked her, holding Marcus's candy bucket while Marcus wrestled with his tail.

"A raven."

"Like the bird?"

"Like the poem."

Dave decided not to ask follow-up questions. He hiked the bucket onto his arm and followed them down the walkway.

They hit the Hoopers first. Sandra gave them full-size Reese's cups. She told Marcus his costume was magnificent and told Lina her wings were literary. Gerald nodded from behind his newspaper.

Then the Chens. Fun-size Snickers. The Greens had a full haunted setup in their garage, with a fog machine and a speaker playing thunder sounds. Marcus loved it. Lina stood at the edge with her arms crossed and said "Not bad" in a tone that suggested she'd seen better. Then the Parkers at the end of the cul-de-sac, who gave out vegetable sticks every year and every year the kids smiled politely and then made faces once they were back on the sidewalk.

They worked their way back down the other side. The Millers at fourteen had Halloween cotton candy. Maria Alvarez gave them pan de muerto wrapped in wax paper and told Marcus it was ghost bread, which made him hold it at arm's length until Lina took it from him.

Then they were in front of number four.

Marcus was already walking up the path.

"Hey, bud." Dave called out from the sidewalk. "I don't think he's doing—"

The porch light came on. Not the usual warm glow of the reading lamp. A different light, angled to illuminate the doorway. Dave hadn't noticed the change in the fixture. It cast an orange tint over the entrance. Subtle. Almost warm.

Marcus rang the bell.

The door opened. Aldric was wearing a charcoal vest over a white shirt. The same clothes he always wore, or close enough. He was holding a wicker basket in both hands. The basket was lined with dark orange tissue paper, and inside were small packages, each one wrapped in brown paper and tied with string.

"Good evening," Aldric said. He looked at Marcus. "Mr. Ortega. I see you've gone with something from the Late Cretaceous. Excellent posture for a biped."

Marcus pushed his headpiece up. "It keeps falling."

"May I?" Aldric set the basket on the small table, crouched down and adjusted something at the back of the headpiece. A fold, a tuck. Marcus shook his head experimentally. The headpiece stayed.

"How'd you do that?"

"Counterweight principle. The teeth are heavier than the back panel. You needed ballast." He picked up the basket. "Now then."

He selected a package from the basket. Marcus's name was written on a small card attached to the string. Written, in ink, in handwriting so even it looked typeset.

"Thank you for visiting." Aldric placed the package in Marcus's bucket. Then he selected another. This one said LINA.

Lina took it. She turned it over in her hands, examining the wrapping. "What is it?"

"A treat," Aldric said. "Traditionally offered on this date in exchange for not being tricked. I trust you'll uphold your end."

Lina almost smiled. "Nice wings?" she said, testing.

"Quoth the raven," Aldric said. "Very appropriate for the evening. Poe would approve of the wingspan."

Lina took a step back, holding her package against her chest. She was definitely smiling now, even if she was trying not to.

"Thanks, Aldric." Dave raised a hand. "Happy Halloween."

"Happy Halloween, Dave. Enjoy the evening. Marcus, remember: ballast."

Marcus and Lina walked back to the sidewalk. Dave looked in the bucket. The two packages sat on top of the pile of regular candy like artifacts among rubble.

"Can I open it?" Marcus was already pulling at the string.

"Wait till we get home."

"But—"

"Home, buddy."

Dave saw the Hendersons were heading toward number four. Amy Henderson had Chloe by the hand — a fairy, or possibly a butterfly, the wings were ambiguous.

"Is he doing it?" Amy said. "The light's on, but—"

"He's doing it." Dave shifted the bucket to his other arm. "Trust me. Go."

They came back out two minutes later. Amy was holding a package identical to Marcus's. Brown paper, string, handwritten card.

"He gave Chloe a personalized card." Amy held it up. The writing was the same precise script. "It says 'For Chloe. I hope the gymnastics competition went well last month. You looked focused.' " Amy stared at Dave. "How does he know about her gymnastics?"

Dave thought about it. "She practices in the driveway sometimes?"

"Yeah. Maybe once or twice. But mostly in the backyard."

Dave didn't have an answer for that. He shrugged the way he shrugged about most Aldric-related things. The shrug that meant *I know, but what are you going to do.*

By seven-fifteen, the street had developed a pattern. Parents clustered in twos and threes on the sidewalk, comparing cards. The Parkers' son had gotten one that referenced his skateboard. Nadine Solis' twins had each gotten a separate package with separate cards, each one noting something different about them. Mrs. Chen stopped Dave on the sidewalk and told him that Aldric's card to her daughter said "Your piano scales have improved markedly since September. The Chopin piece deserves a larger audience."

Sandra found Dave on his front steps at seven-thirty. She was holding a package herself.

"He gave *you* one?" Dave said.

"He's giving them to everyone who comes to the door. Adults too." Sandra opened her card. It read, in the same impeccable hand: *Dear Sandra. Thank you for making this street feel like a neighborhood. The tensioning on the cobwebs is exceptional. Best regards, A. Venn.*

Sandra pressed the card against her chest. "He noticed my cobwebs."

"He notices everything."

"But the *tensioning*, Dave."

Gerald appeared at Sandra's elbow. He was not holding a package.

"You didn't go?" Sandra said.

"I don't trick-or-treat, Sandra."

"He would have given you one."

"I'm aware." Gerald looked across at number four. The porch light was still on. A fresh group of kids was approaching the door. "He prepared individual cards for every child on this street. How long do you think that took him?"

"I think it's sweet."

"How long does a man spend watching other people's children to write those?"

"Gerald."

"I'm stating a fact."

Sandra swatted his arm with her card. "Go get your package."

"I don't need a package."

"Gerald Robert Hooper."

Gerald sighed. The argument was lost but the surrender would be slow and dignified.

Sandra looked at Dave. "Where's yours?"

"I stayed on the sidewalk. The kids went up."

Sandra looked at him the way she'd looked at Gerald. "Dave Ortega."

"Fine." He handed Marcus the bucket. "Watch your sister. Don't move."

He and Gerald walked across the street together. Neither of them said anything. Gerald had his hands in his pockets. Dave didn't know what to do with his. They reached the porch. Gerald rang the bell.

The door opened. Aldric looked at both of them. Something moved in his expression — not surprise, not quite amusement. Recognition, maybe. Like he'd been waiting.

"Gentlemen," he said. "I was wondering when you'd come."

He selected two packages from the basket. GERALD. DAVE. Same brown paper. Same string. Same precise handwriting.

Gerald took his without looking at it. "Thank you."

"Thank you for coming, Gerald. Good evening."

Dave took his. "Happy Halloween, Aldric."

"Happy Halloween, Dave." A pause. "The ballast is holding, I see." He nodded toward Marcus across the street, headpiece still in place.

They walked back. Gerald said nothing. Dave said nothing. Sandra was smiling from ear to ear.

Dave looked down at Marcus, who had finally been allowed to open his package. Inside the brown paper was a small box of Belgian chocolates and a card. The card read: *Dear Marcus. Happy first Halloween at six. I understand the Tyrannosaurus had excellent vision for moving objects but poor depth perception. This may explain the short arms. Warm regards, A. Venn.*

Marcus looked up. "What's depth perception?"

"How you tell if something's close or far away."

"Is that why they had short arms? Because they couldn't tell?"

"That's not—" Dave stopped. He didn't actually know. "Maybe. We'll look it up."

Lina had opened hers while Dave wasn't watching. Her card was already in her jacket pocket, hidden. The chocolates were in her hand. She was eating one with an expression that suggested they were considerably better than the Reese's cup from the Hoopers.

"What did yours say?" Dave asked.

"None of your business."

"Fair enough."

They walked home. Behind them, the street was still buzzing. Kids in costumes, parents with cards, the orange glow from number four still on.

Dave smiled. He took Marcus's hand, picked up Lina's bucket and walked the rest of the way home.

At eight-thirty, Rosa was in the kitchen sorting the kids' candy haul when Dave came in from locking the door.

"Gerald came over," she said. "While you were doing bedtime."

"What did he want?"

"He wanted to know what our cards said." Rosa picked up her own card from the counter. She'd gone over separately with a plate of cookies, after the kids were inside. Her card read: *Dear Rosa. Your sofrito recipe deserves to be published. The cumin is inspired. Thank you for the plate. — A. Venn.*

"He could smell the cumin?" Dave said.

"He could smell the cumin." Rosa put the card on the fridge, next to Marcus's dinosaur drawing. "What did Gerald's say?"

Dave sat on the counter stool. "He didn't tell me."

"He didn't tell Sandra either." Rosa smiled. "Which means it's either very sweet or very accurate."

Dave picked up one of Marcus's Belgian chocolates. He turned it over. Gold foil, embossed brand name he couldn't pronounce. He bit into it. It was exceptional.

"How much do you think all this cost him?" Dave said.

"I don't think that's the point."

"What's the point?"

Rosa looked at the card on the fridge. The handwriting was so precise it looked like it came from another century. She picked up the rest of the chocolate wrappers and dropped them in the bin.

"He was paying attention," she said. "That's the point."

Outside, the porch light at number four went off at ten-fifteen. Same as always.
