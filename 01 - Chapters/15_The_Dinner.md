# Chapter 15: The Dinner

Rosa had spent the afternoon in the kitchen and most of it had been good. The rice was done. The black beans were simmering with cumin and a bay leaf. The corn was roasted and cooling on a sheet pan, the kernels browning at the edges. The chorizo was cooked and resting in a bowl, sliced thin, releasing orange into a paper towel.

The delicata had taken the longest. Dave had brought two from the market. She'd sliced them into half-moons, tossed them with olive oil and smoked paprika, and roasted them until the edges crisped. They smelled like fall. The house smelled like fall. She'd opened the kitchen window to let the heat out, and the October air came in cold and clean.

Marcus was in the living room building something with blocks that would collapse soon. Lina was at the table, doing homework with the eraser end of her pencil more than the writing end. Dave was upstairs changing his shirt, which meant he was nervous. Dave changed his shirt when company came. It was his only tell.

"What time is he coming?" Lina said without looking up.

"Six."

"Is he bringing dessert?"

"I don't know. Probably not." Rosa checked the clock. Five forty-two. She still needed to chop the cilantro, cut the limes, and set the table. "Lina, can you set four places? The big plates."

"Five," Lina said.

"Right. Five." Rosa smiled. She kept forgetting to count Aldric. He wasn't a regular at their table. He wasn't a regular at anyone's table, which was part of why she'd invited him.

Lina set the table with the kind of precision that came from watching Rosa do it. Forks on the left. Knives on the right. She put out the cloth napkins, the blue ones, and Rosa didn't correct her. Those were for guests.

Dave came down in a flannel shirt that was slightly nicer than his usual flannel. "What can I do?"

"Limes. Cut them in quarters."

He washed his hands, took the cutting board, and started cutting limes with the focus of a man who'd been given a task and intended to complete it. Rosa loved him for that. He never asked why or how many. He just cut.

At five fifty-eight, the doorbell rang.

"Me, me," Marcus yelled from the living room, already running. Rosa heard the door open. She heard Marcus say "Hi" in the voice he used for people he liked but wasn't sure about.

"Good evening, Marcus." Aldric's voice from the hallway. "Thank you for opening the door."

"Mom's cooking."

"I can tell. It smells excellent."

Rosa wiped her hands on the dish towel and came around the corner. Aldric was in the hallway, holding a bottle of wine in one hand and a small paper bag in the other. He wore a knitted cardigan over a collared shirt. No jacket. Like he'd thought about what formality meant for a family dinner and calibrated precisely.

"Rosa." He smiled. "Thank you for having me."

"We're glad you're here." She meant it. "Come in. Can I take anything?"

He held up the bottle. "A Rioja. Viña Ardanza Reserva. I thought it might suit the evening."

She took it. The label was simple, elegant. She didn't know the vineyard. She hadn't told him what she was making, but a dark red wine with fruit and moderate tannins was a safe bet for Mexican food. "This is lovely. Thank you."

"I'm glad." He set the paper bag on the counter. "Pepitas. Toasted. In case you need extra."

She looked at him. He looked back. His expression was open, warm, entirely without apology.

She had not told him about the pepitas either.

"Dave," she said. "Open this?"

Dave took the wine, looked at the label, nodded in the way men nod at wine bottles when they have no opinion, and found the corkscrew. Aldric moved into the kitchen like he moved into every space. He didn't hover. He didn't fill the room. He occupied a corner of it like he'd always been there.

Marcus followed him. "Who cooks for you?"

"I cook for myself."

"Every day?"

"Most days."

"That's sad," Marcus said. 

"Marcus, that's not a nice thing to say." Rosa chided him. 

"It's all right," Aldric said. "He's not entirely wrong." He looked at Marcus. "But the good thing about cooking for yourself is that you always get to eat exactly what you want."

"I want popsicles," Marcus said.

"An excellent choice. What flavor?"

"Grape."

"A classic."

Marcus climbed onto his chair. "Can I have juice?"

Rosa got him juice. Lina had closed her homework and was watching Aldric from the far end of the table. Not staring. Observing. She had Rosa's eyes for people and Dave's patience for keeping what she saw to herself.

"Lina," Aldric said. "How is fifth grade treating you?"

"Fine."

"Who's your teacher this year?"

"Mrs. Hendricks."

"Is she good?"

Lina considered this. "She's fair," Lina said. "She doesn't pick favorites."

"That's high praise."

"It's accurate, though." Lina went back to watching. Aldric accepted it without discomfort. Most adults tried to draw children out. Aldric let them sit where they sat.

Rosa served the bowls. Rice at the bottom, then beans, then the roasted squash and chorizo, corn scattered on top. She set out the toppings in the middle of the table. Cilantro, lime wedges, cotija crumbled into a small bowl, and pepitas. Both hers and his.

Dave poured wine for the adults. Marcus got juice. Lina got water with lemon, which she preferred and which Rosa had only started offering six months ago after Lina asked once.

They ate. The conversation moved like conversations do with children at the table. In pieces. Marcus talked about a boy at daycare who could hang from the monkey bars with one hand. He talked about Jake's older brother who had a Lego fire station "where you can open all the doors and the ladder *actually goes up* and there's a dog inside," and he used both hands to show how big it was. Lina observed quietly. Dave ate steadily and made sounds of approval. Aldric ate slowly, with attention, and complimented the squash — twice.

"The browning is excellent," he said. "You cut them thin enough to crisp without drying."

"Dave helped with those," Rosa said. It wasn't quite true. Dave had carried them in from the market. But it was a generous thing to say.

"The market squash," Aldric said. "They were good ones. Even weight, clean skin."

Dave looked up from his bowl. "You picked them."

"I suggested them. You carried them."

"Same thing," Dave said.

"Debatable." Aldric smiled.

The conversation moved on. Marcus held his bowl up with both hands. "More?"

Rosa took it, spooned in rice and beans, added a few pieces of squash. He pulled it back before she could add cilantro.

"Is there hot sauce?" Lina said.

Rosa pointed. "Cabinet. Second shelf."

Lina got up, found it, sat back down. She shook three drops onto her rice with the precision of someone who'd learned the hard way what four drops meant.

The dinner continued. Aldric helped clear plates. He didn't stack them the way Dave did, in a tower that threatened the counter's edge. He carried two at a time, scraped them into the bin, and loaded them into the dishwasher with the care of someone who'd thought about dishwashers.

"Evenly spaced," he said, setting a plate into the lower rack. "So the water from the spray arm reaches every surface. It took me an embarrassing number of rewashes to figure that out."

Rosa sent Marcus to brush his teeth. He grumbled. Lina went without being asked, which was her way of showing she was better than her brother.

Rosa watched Aldric from the counter. He was doing it the way he did everything. Precisely. The bowls went in the top rack, openings down. The serving spoons in the utensil basket, handles up. He checked the spray arm's rotation path before closing the door.

A few things didn't fit. The cutting board. The cast iron pan. The wooden spoons Rosa never put in the dishwasher. Aldric turned to the sink and ran the water.

"You don't have to do that," Rosa said.

"It's no trouble." He was already soaping the sponge.

"Aldric. You're a guest."

He stopped. Set the sponge down. Turned off the water. "You're right," he said. "I apologize." He stepped away from the sink and dried his hands on the towel that hung from the oven door.

Dave made coffee. Aldric accepted a cup.

They sat in the living room. Rosa on the couch. Dave in the armchair. Aldric in the chair by the window, the one that was slightly too upright for most people. He looked comfortable in it.

"Thank you for this," Aldric said. "It was a lovely evening."

"You're welcome anytime," Rosa said. And she meant it. "The wine," she added. "How did you know it would pair?"

Aldric tilted his head. "Chorizo is Spanish in origin. Rioja is its natural companion. And the lime and cilantro suggest brightness, so a Reserva with fruit rather than too much oak." He paused. "Was that what you meant?"

"I meant how did you know I was making chorizo."

He looked at her. For a moment, something shifted behind his eyes. Not guilt. Not surprise. Recognition. The recognition of someone who'd been asked a question they'd been expecting.

"I didn't," he said. "I guessed."

"You guessed."

"The squash from the market. Chorizo pairs well with them. And you invited me on a week when pepitas are in season." He smiled. "It was an educated guess. I'm glad it landed."

Dave was looking between them. He had the expression of a man who'd missed three steps in a conversation and was trying not to ask.

"That's—" Rosa started.

"Intrusive?" Aldric said.

"Impressive."

"Often the same thing." He drank his coffee. "I apologize if it felt like more than attention. It was just attention."

Rosa shook her head. "Don't apologize. The wine was perfect."

Aldric left at eight thirty. He thanked them both at the door, told Marcus goodnight through the closed bathroom door where Marcus was still not brushing his teeth, and said something to Lina that Rosa didn't catch. Lina smiled. Not the half-smile she gave most adults. An actual smile. She was ten, almost at the age where adults became embarrassing by default. Whatever Aldric had said, he'd earned it. 

Rosa closed the door. 

"That was nice," Dave said.

"It was."

"The wine was good, too."

"He guessed what I was cooking," Rosa said. She leaned against the counter. "From the squash at the market and time of the year."

Dave frowned, looking to his side. "That's a little—"

"Yeah."

"But also—"

"Yeah." Rosa crossed her arms. Not against Aldric. Against the feeling she couldn't name. The feeling of being known in a way she hadn't invited. Not violated. Not exposed. Just... seen. By someone who saw everything. 

Rosa picked up the bottle. There was one glass left in it. She poured it. "He's a good guy."

"You said that last week."

"I'm saying it again." She drank. The Rioja had been perfect all evening. It was still perfect now. "He just pays attention in a way that's different from how other people pay attention."

Dave came over. He leaned beside her, shoulders touching. "Different weird or different good?"

Rosa thought about the pepitas. The way he'd said *in case you need extra*, like he already knew she'd bought some. Like he'd watched her at the market too.

"Both," she said. "Good. Definitely good."

Dave kissed the side of her head. He went upstairs. Rosa finished her wine, wiped the counter and turned off the kitchen light. The house was quiet. Marcus was asleep. Lina's reading light was still on under her door. Tomorrow was Sunday. The delicata was gone. The Rioja was gone. The evening was gone.

There were still some pepitas left. And the wine. How had he known?
