# Chapter 1: The New Neighbor

Dave saw the Volvo before he saw the man.

It was old. Dark green, clean, no bumper sticker, no rust. The kind of old that meant someone took care of it. The trailer behind it was too big. Half empty. Dave had helped three people move in over the years and the trailer was always too small. Mattresses on the roof, garbage bags in the gaps, someone sitting in the passenger seat holding a lamp. This one looked like the guy couldn’t fill it.

Dave watched from the porch. The man had gotten out of the car, stretched once, and walked to the back of the trailer. Then he didn’t unload. He went inside the house first, came back out a few minutes later and carried one thing in. A small metal case. Then he sat in the rocking chair on the porch for a moment, looking at the street. Like he was getting his bearings. Or like he was waiting.

Dave told Rosa he’d be back in a minute and walked across the lawn in his flip-flops, coffee still in one hand.

The man was standing at the trailer again. Tall. Clean-shaven. Wearing a collared shirt tucked into dark jeans, sleeves rolled once at the cuff. His shoes were clean. Not new. Clean. On moving day. Dave looked at his own flip-flops and the grass stain on the left one.

“Hey,” Dave said, raising a hand. “You must be the new guy. Dave Ortega, next door.”

The man turned. Smiled. Not the polite kind people did at the grocery store. The real kind. The kind that used the whole face, starting at the eyes.

“Aldric Venn.” He extended his hand. “It is a genuine pleasure to meet you, Dave. Thank you for coming over. That was very kind of you.”

The handshake was perfect. Not too hard, not too soft. Dry palm. Dave had a thought he couldn’t quite place — that this man had practiced this. Then the thought was gone.

“Just being neighborly,” Dave said.

“Neighborly.” Aldric said it like a gift he was accepting. Like the word itself had weight. “Thank you. Truly.”

Nobody said *truly*. Dave smiled back.

“Well, I’m glad to have you here. You need help with the—” He looked at the trailer. He looked again. “Is that everything?”

“That’s everything.”

“Huh.”

Gerald appeared within ninety seconds. That was Gerald. Something happens on the street, Gerald shows up. He came from across the road with his hands in his pockets. Dave had decoded that years ago: hands in pockets meant he was staying to watch. Hands at his sides meant passing through. Today the hands were deep.

“Gerald Hooper,” he said. No handshake. “Across the street. Number three.”

“Mr. Hooper.” Aldric didn’t look at the un-offered hand. He didn’t hesitate. He just folded his own in front of him like that had been the plan all along. “A pleasure. You have a remarkable front yard, if I may say. The hedge line is extraordinarily even.”

Gerald blinked. Nobody had ever complimented his hedge. Dave had lived across from it for eleven years and never mentioned it once.

“Thanks,” Gerald said.

“Do you trim it yourself?”

“Yeah. Once a month.”

“I admire the discipline. Consistent hedgework requires either great patience or an excellent eye. You appear to have both.”

Dave watched Gerald try to figure out if he was being made fun of. He wasn’t. That was the thing. Aldric was standing in his driveway on moving day, complimenting a hedge with complete sincerity, and it wasn’t a joke. His eyes were steady. His posture was relaxed.

Gerald stood a little straighter. “It’s just a hedge,” he said.

“Nothing is *just* anything,” Aldric said. “Not if someone takes care of it.”

Brief silence. A lawn mower started up two streets over. The sound carried in the heat.

“Right,” Gerald said. “Well. Need help with the boxes?”

There were three boxes, a chair and a small toolbox. That was what was left in the trailer.

Dave carried the one marked *Kitchen*. It had the normal, expected weight. Plates, pots, maybe some mugs. It clinked when he shifted it. Gerald took *Books*, which was heavier than it looked. He grunted but didn’t comment. The third said *Miscellaneous* and had an extra layer of packing tape. The thick kind, applied carefully, no bubbles, no wrinkles. 

Aldric carried that one himself. One-handed, the other hand on the leather chair.

The chair was dark brown, worn at the arms, the leather cracked in a way that meant decades of usage. Expensive once and loved always. It didn’t match anything in the furnished house. Aldric set it by the front window and adjusted its angle. Then again. Then a third time. Not much. Maybe half an inch each time. He stepped back, looked at it, moved it once more. He stopped. Satisfied with something nobody else could see.

The trailer was empty in eight minutes. Most of that was walking.

“Seriously,” Dave said. “That’s it?”

“That’s everything.” Aldric smiled again. Always the whole face. “I prefer to travel light. The house came furnished, which was a considerable advantage. The listing mentioned linens and kitchenware.”

“When we moved in, we had two trips and a fight about the couch.”

“Was the couch worth the fight?”

“The couch was garbage. Rosa was right.” Dave grinned and leaned in conspiratorially. “Don’t tell her I said that.” He stage whispered the last part.

“Your confidence in her judgment is safe with me.” A small bow of the head. It should have been weird. It wasn’t.

Dave looked at the empty trailer. Three boxes. A man moves into a new country — that’s what the plates said, Dave had seen the out-of-state plates — and brings three boxes and a chair.

“You want a beer?” Dave said. “I got some in the fridge.”

“I would enjoy that very much. Perhaps later, if the offer stands. I’d like to settle in first.”

“Sure. Anytime.”
Dave and Gerald walked back together. Gerald's hands were at his sides now. Passing-through hands. He nodded once at Dave at the property line and went home.

Sandra appeared twenty minutes later. She came through the front door — which Aldric had left open to let the air through — carrying an energy that Dave recognized as investigative. She was holding a casserole dish in both hands. The dish was her excuse. The casserole was real. The excuse was also real. Both things could be true with Sandra.

“Sandra Hooper,” she said. “Gerald’s wife. Number three. I brought you something. Let me show you where everything is in the kitchen.”

She was in the kitchen before Aldric could respond. Dave was still in the hallway, about to leave, and he watched as she opened the first cabinet. Then the second. Then the third. Everything already in place. Plates stacked. Glasses grouped by size. The kitchen box had been unpacked, broken down, and was nowhere in sight.

“Oh,” Sandra said, opening the fourth cabinet. “You’ve already found everything.”

“The layout is quite intuitive,” Aldric said. “But I appreciate the thought.”

“You’re so tidy though. No boxes anywhere.” She opened the drawer next to the sink. Silverware. Normal silverware. She looked faintly disappointed. “Some people take weeks to unpack.”

“I prefer to settle quickly. It’s a respect for the space.”

Sandra opened one more cabinet. Spices. Three jars. Labeled by hand, in neat block letters. Salt. Pepper. Paprika. That was all. Sandra stared at them like they were evidence of something.

“You label your spices,” she said.

“It helps me find them in poor light.”

“There are just three of them.”

“Yes, there are.” He smiled at her. The full smile. Sandra smiled back. She didn’t find what she was looking for. She never would. That was Sandra’s particular thing — she could open every drawer in a house and still leave with nothing but her own conclusions.

Dave left Sandra to her investigation and went home. Rosa was in the kitchen, chopping something with the good knife. On the big cutting board.

“You cooking for him?”

“He hasn’t eaten. I can tell.”

“How can you tell?”

Rosa gave him the look. The one that said she didn’t explain how she could tell. She just could tell.

Rosa appeared at Aldric’s door at four. Dave went with her, carrying the rice. She had the enchiladas in the good Pyrex. The clear one with the blue lid. Church potluck Pyrex. That was serious. You didn’t use the church potluck Pyrex for someone you hadn’t decided about yet. Rosa had decided.

“You haven’t eaten,” she said to Aldric. Not a question.

“I have not.” He looked at the dishes. He looked at Rosa. “That smells extraordinary, Mrs. Ortega. Is that enchiladas?”

“And rice. And there’s salsa my mother-in-law made, but it’s spicy.”

“I enjoy spicy food very much. Thank you. This is uncommonly generous of you.” 

Rosa looked at him. Then at the house behind him. The single chair by the window. The three boxes stacked against the wall, already broken down and folded flat. Flattened neatly. Edges aligned.

“It’s just food,” she said. But she was already stepping inside.

“It isn’t,” Aldric said. “But I won’t argue with the cook.”

Rosa almost smiled. That was fast. It usually took her three conversations to almost-smile at someone new. Dave noticed.

He glanced back toward the kitchen. “Between this and Mrs. Hooper’s casserole, I now have more food than one person can reasonably eat in a week. Would you both consider keeping me company? It seems wrong to have this much generosity on a table and no one to share it with.”

They ate on the porch. All five of them — Dave, Rosa, Aldric, Gerald, Sandra — on moving day, which was already unusual. Sandra had never left. Gerald came back over when he saw the plates come out. The evening was warm. Still light at seven. The food was good. Aldric ate slowly, carefully, like each bite was worth noticing.

He asked Rosa about the spices in the salsa. Cumin, yes. Chipotle. But something else. Cinnamon? Rosa told him yes, just a pinch, and he listened the way he’d listened to Gerald’s hedge — completely. Like the answer mattered. Like it would go somewhere. His small notebook appeared briefly. He wrote something and put it back.

He asked Dave about the neighborhood. How long they’d been here. Eleven years, Dave told him. What the winters were like. Mild. One ice storm in 2019 that took out power for two days. Whether the mailman was reliable. Mostly. He skips Saturdays sometimes.

"And children?" Aldric asked. "Are there children on the street?"

Rosa answered that one. "Ours. Three. The oldest is ten, the youngest just started kindergarten. The Hoopers' son is grown. He visits sometimes, brings the grandkids. Maria at number eight has a boy, but he just moved out. The Chens are too young for kids. Or too smart." She almost smiled again. "The Greens have a daughter, fifteen. And the dog. That counts, according to them."

Aldric nodded. He made a note.

Normal questions. All of them normal. And yet Dave couldn’t shake the feeling that Aldric was filing the answers somewhere. Not for small talk. For something else. He didn’t know what. He didn’t think about it too hard. The beer was cold and the evening was nice.

He asked Gerald about the neighborhood watch. Gerald told him there wasn’t one, officially. Just him. Just his eyes. Aldric nodded like that was expected. Like he’d already known.

Then Ms. Yuen walked past on her evening walk and stopped at the edge of the lawn. Nobody had invited her. Nobody needed to. She stood there with her hands behind her back and watched. She was always watching. She’d lived on this street longer than anyone. The last of the original owners.

Aldric noticed her immediately. He set his plate down on the railing.

“Ma’am,” he said, and stood up from his chair. Actually stood up. Like it was 1955 and someone important had entered the room. “Would you like to join us? There’s more than enough.”

Ms. Yuen looked at him for a long time. Long enough that it would have been uncomfortable with anyone else. Her eyes were dark and still. She was eighty-three years old and she was measuring him. Dave knew that look. She gave it to everyone eventually. Most people looked away. Aldric didn’t.

“You’re very polite,” she said.

“I try.”

“You succeed.” She took the offered chair. Aldric remained standing until she was settled, then sat back down on the porch step instead. He gave her his seat. Dave and Gerald exchanged a look. Gerald’s said *what is happening*. Dave’s said *I don’t know but there’s casserole*.

Ms. Yuen accepted a small plate. She ate with small, unhurried bites and said very little. She watched everything, though.

The questions came naturally after that. They always did on move-in days. Sandra led, which was also natural.

“So where are you from?”

“The north. The quieter part.”

“Which part is that?”

“The part without much to report.” Smile. “I enjoy the weather here already. Less grey.”

“Family?”

“None to speak of.” That same smile. No sadness in it. No self-pity. Just a fact.

“What brought you down here?”

“A desire for neighbors. I’ve lived alone for some time. I thought it might be good to hear other people’s lawnmowers on Saturday mornings.”

Everyone laughed. Aldric looked pleased that they laughed. Like their laughter was a small gift he hadn’t expected.

“And what do you do? For work, I mean?”

Aldric set his beer down. His smile did not falter. “I solve problems. Think of me as a consultant of sorts.”

That was it. The smile stayed. Nothing else came. Everyone waited. Dave waited. A full five seconds of silence. Nobody pushed. Dave didn’t know why. It just felt like pushing would be rude, and Aldric had been so aggressively un-rude all afternoon that rudeness had stopped being an option in his radius. Like he’d built a zone around himself where people behaved slightly better than they normally would. A field.

Gerald opened his mouth. He closed it and drank his beer instead.

“What kind of problems?” Sandra said.

“The kind people struggle with alone.” He picked up his beer again. “I find that most problems look larger from the inside than they do from a slight distance.”

That was more than he’d given before. Also not really anything. Dave let it go.

Before they left, Aldric thanked each person individually. He stood at the bottom of his porch steps and shook hands.

“Dave.” Direct eye contact. “Thank you for carrying the kitchen box. And for coming over before anyone else. First impressions of a new place are set by the first person you meet. You made this one very good.”

Dave didn’t know what to say. “Anytime, man.”

“Rosa. The enchiladas were exceptional. The cinnamon is your innovation, isn’t it? Not your mother-in-law’s. I wonder if you’d be willing to share the complete recipe sometime. Only if it’s not a family secret.” Rosa just nodded. The almost-smile again.

“Gerald. Thank you for showing me where the fuse box is. I would have spent an hour looking.”

That last one was interesting. Gerald hadn’t shown him anything. Sandra had opened cabinets. Gerald had just stood in the hallway and looked at things. But in Aldric’s version, Gerald had been helpful. And Gerald accepted it. Dave watched it happen. Gerald’s face did something it rarely did. The jaw unclenched. The forehead smoothed. He nodded once, like *of course I showed you where the fuse box is, that’s what I do*.

“Sandra. Thank you for the casserole. The fact that you made it on such short notice is genuinely impressive. You are clearly a person of considerable skill under pressure.”

Sandra beamed. Her whole face changed. Gerald, for the first time all evening, put his hands at his sides. Passing-through hands. Relaxed hands.

“Ms. Yuen. Thank you for your company. You were under no obligation to stay, and the fact that you did made the evening better. I hope you’ll allow me to walk with you sometime.”

Ms. Yuen nodded once. She said nothing. She turned and walked away down the sidewalk. Her back straight. Her steps even.

By nine, everyone was gone. The street was quiet. Lightning bugs starting up in the yards. The heat loosening. Dave’s was the last to go and Aldric shook his hand again. The same perfect handshake. He said he hoped they’d allow him to return the hospitality soon. Dave believed him. He didn’t know why he believed him, but he did.

He walked back across the lawn with Rosa. The grass was damp already. The kids were asleep. Or faking it. Close enough.

“Nice guy,” Dave said.

“Mm,” Rosa said. 

“What?”

“Nothing. He’s very polite.”

“That’s bad?”

“No.” She paused at the front door. She put her key in the lock but didn’t turn it. “He’s already done the dishes.”

“What?”

“I saw him through the window. He was at the sink before Gerald finished his beer. Every bowl. Every plate. The Pyrex is on the porch already. Stacked. With little notes.”

“Notes?”

“Handwritten. Which dish belongs to who. In case we get them mixed up.”

Dave thought about that.

“Huh,” Dave said.

Rosa turned the key. “He’ll fit in,” she said. Then, quieter: “Or we’ll fit around him. One of those.”

Dave laughed. Rosa didn’t. He stopped laughing.

Inside, the house was dark and cool. The dishwasher was running from earlier. The cat was on the couch. Normal house. Normal night. Dave locked the door behind them.

In bed, he stared at the ceiling. The fan turned slowly. Nice guy, but who moves eleven hundred miles for the sound of lawnmowers?

He turned over. Rosa was already asleep. Breathing slow and even.
