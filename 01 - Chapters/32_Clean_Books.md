# Chapter 32: Clean Books

*Friday, the 10th of January 2025*

The blue notebook was on its fourth page now. Gerald had already filled three of them in the dark.

Not the dark of the room. The dark of the season. January came on at four-thirty in the afternoon, took the color out of the street, and left him a window that showed mostly his own reflection by five. He had learned to work around it. The desk lamp threw a circle on the page and nothing on the glass, so a man could sit at his own window after dinner and not announce himself to the street. He had tested it from the sidewalk in December, in his coat, pretending to check the mailbox. From outside, the study was a dim square. The lamp didn't reach the blinds. Good.

He had been right about that, like he had been right about most of the small things.

It was the large thing he couldn't close.

He opened the notebook to the working page and read down the left column, which was the column of things he knew. The house: four hundred twelve thousand dollars, paid in full, no mortgage, bought in May from the Blanchard Family Trust. The car: registered in June, no plate before it. The name: four results on a search engine, none of them older than last spring. He had written all of it in November, the night Sandra's laptop froze. He had gone looking and found a man who started existing eight months ago.

Two months later the column on the right was still mostly white. The right column was the column of things he didn't know. At the top of it, underlined once, in his own block capitals: WHO WAS HE BEFORE.

Gerald capped the pen. He took off his glasses and pressed the bridge of his nose between two fingers.

Thirty-one years of audit work had taught him one thing he trusted above all the others. People left a trail. Not because they were careless. Because living made a trail, walking through life left footprints. A man rented an apartment at twenty-two and the lease survived him by decades in a county file. A man got a parking ticket in 1994 and it sat in a database forever, harmless, dumb, true. A man coached a Little League team and showed up in an archived newspaper photo, third row, squinting. None of it mattered. All of it was there. The trail was never the evidence. The trail was just the proof that a life had been lived in the open, the way every honest life was.

Aldric Venn had no trail. And a man with no trail was not a man who had hidden it. He had read enough bad books to recognize that fantasy. You could not scrub a life that thoroughly. The records sat in too many hands.

A man with no trail was a man who had been issued one. Recently. By someone with the authority to issue it.

Gerald had not written that sentence down. He knew better than to write down the sentence that would make Sandra call his brother.

He turned to the December pages instead, because he liked to remind himself how thin the take had been. The car photograph was taped there, a four-by-six he had printed at the drugstore kiosk, feeling like a fool the whole time. He had taken it on a gray afternoon before Christmas, from his own porch, telephoto, the back of the green Volvo at the curb. He had thought he might catch a parking sticker, an inspection tag, a dealer frame, or a bumper decal from a town a thousand miles away. Something with a place name on it.

The photograph showed the back of a clean car. No sticker. No frame. No decal. Nothing at all. The man didn't even let his car carry a trail.

Gerald looked at the photograph a while. Then he did the thing he had been procrastinating for three weeks.

He pulled the strip-mall flyer out from under the desk blotter and dialed the number on it.

The man's name was Dale Pruitt. A former county sheriff's deputy, twenty-six years, now running skip-traces and background checks out of an office between a vape store and a nail salon on Route 9. The flyer said FIRST CHECK $400, NO HIT NO FEE. Gerald had picked it off the community board at the library in December and carried it in his coat for a month like a cigarette he wasn't going to smoke.

Pruitt answered on the fourth ring, around a mouthful of something.

"Pruitt."

"I need a background check on an individual. A neighbor. I have a name, an address, and a date of birth I'm not certain about."

"Tenant screening? You a landlord?"

"Private. Personal."

A pause, the sound of a chair taking weight. "I can run him. Four hundred, half now. I'll need you to sign that you got a permissible purpose. You suspecting fraud, threat, anything I should know going in?"

Gerald looked across the street. The porch light at number four was on. The chair was empty. The reading lamp glowed behind the front curtain, steady and warm.

"I suspect," Gerald said, "that he isn't who he says he is."

"That's most people," Pruitt said. "Give me the name."

Gerald gave him the name.

The four hundred dollars was the problem.

Not the money itself, but the four hundred dollars would become a line on a credit card statement. The statement came to the house. Sandra read it the way Gerald read the street. She would see a payment to a name she didn't know, about four hundred dollars. She would ask, because asking was what Sandra did and not-telling was what Gerald did. They had been doing that particular dance for forty-two years.

He paid the deposit. Two hundred dollars to D. PRUITT INVESTIGATIONS. Then he sat with the knowledge of it for two days, which was how long it took the charge to post and Sandra to find it.

She brought the statement into the study on Thursday evening and set it on the desk beside the lamp. She didn't sit. She had her reading glasses pushed up into her hair, which meant she had already read it and decided how to come in.

"Who's D. Pruitt."

Gerald considered the several available lies and discarded them. Sandra knew the shape of him too well for the small ones, and the large one would take more energy than he had after a day at the window.

"He runs background checks."

Sandra was quiet for a moment. Her face did a thing it didn't often do. It got tired.

"Gerald."

"I know what you're going to say."

"You told me you stopped."

"I told you I'd run out of things to look at. That was true. This is a new thing to look at."

"It's not a thing, Gerald. It's a man. A nice man who fixed my garden. Who gave me a lemon tree and shovels Ms. Yuen's walk before the sun's up." She put one hand flat on the statement. "Four hundred dollars to find out what, exactly? That he's polite? We know that. The whole street knows that."

"The whole street thinks he's polite. That's not the same as knowing who he is."

"Nobody knows who anybody is. I've been married to you for forty-two years and you still surprise me." She said it without warmth, which was worse than if she'd been angry. "I don't like this. I didn't like it in September and I don't like it now. It's not the watching. It's what the watching is doing to you. You're at that window every night. You used to read."

"I still read."

"You read the street." She straightened up. "I'm going to bed. Cancel the man, or don't. But I'm not going to pretend I didn't see it, and I'm not going to ask about it again. I'm too old for that."

She went out. The bedroom door didn't slam, which would have been easier to live with than the quiet click it made instead.

Gerald sat with the statement in the lamplight. He did not cancel the man.

Pruitt called eleven days later, while Sandra was out and the house was his.

"You want this on paper or you want the short version on the phone."

"The short version."

"Short version is I don't have much, which is also the interesting part." Pruitt's chair creaked. "Your guy's real. Aldric Jonathan Venn checks out clean across the board. Social's valid, issued in-state, no flags. Credit file opened May of last year, thin but spotless, every account current. Property's his, free and clear. No liens, no judgments, no bankruptcies. No criminal, not even a sealed juvenile. No civil suits. No marriage on record, no divorce, no kids. No prior addresses I can pull, no prior employers, no utilities older than last June. Clean."

"That's what I expected."

"Yeah, well." A pause. "I've been doing this thirty years between the badge and the office. You want to know what I tell people about clean?"

"Yes."

"Clean isn't innocent. Clean is rare. Real people are a mess. A real forty-three-year-old has a repo from his twenties, an old phone bill in collections, a fender bender, a forgotten credit card from 2009, and an address from a city he doesn't talk about anymore. Real people leak. I run more than a hundred of these a year and the honest ones are always the ugliest files." The chair creaked again. "Your guy's file isn't ugly. It starts last spring, and it's perfect from the first line. A file that starts last spring is a man who came from somewhere else. That much is ordinary. Most of the time somewhere else is two states over, and the paper's just slow to catch up."

"And this time?"

"This time the paper doesn't catch up, because it isn't where I look. So I ran it wider. Old habit. I've still got a fella I worked border files with, from back when I carried a badge." The chair creaked. "Your guy didn't come from two states over, Mr. Hooper. The trail runs north. Over the line."

Gerald's pen stopped on the page.

"Canada," he said.

"Canada. And before you make it into more than it is, that doesn't tell me what he is. Came from there, lived there, I can't say which, and it changes nothing for you either way. I am not telling you he works for anybody up north. I'm telling you where the paper's been. The paper's been north, and the paper's old."

"How old."

"Twenty years, give or take. That's why you're getting a phone call and not an envelope." The ease had gone out of his voice. "It's clean up there too. Twenty years of it, a border through the middle, and not one ugly line on either side. Nothing at the crossing, which is the one place there's always something."

"And that means what."

"Fresh clean I can explain. Witness protection takes a real person and builds him a tidy new life over a weekend. If you pulled those files and run a few years deep, the old dirt's still under the new boards if you know where to pry." The chair was still now. "This is not a few years, and there are no boards. Twenty years, two countries, and no dirt anywhere in any of it. A protected man is a messy man somebody cleaned up. Your guy was never messy. In thirty years I have seen that exactly once."

Gerald did not say anything.

"So here is the part I don't bill for," Pruitt said. "Whatever this is, it isn't yours to open. A file that clean, that old, and that quiet has people behind it. Those people don't take kindly to prying eyes. I'll shred my copy tonight. Think hard about yours." A beat. "Good luck, Mr. Hooper. Don't call me again on this one."

He hung up.

Gerald sat for a long time. He wrote it down. All of it. In his blue notebook. In the small even hand he used when his pulse was up and he didn't want the writing to show it. Clean credit. Clean record. No prior anything in this country. *North. Twenty years. Both sides of the line. No mess on either.*

He had searched the wrong country. That was the plain, humbling size of it. In November he had sat at this desk past midnight and walked every database the state gave him, and he had found the month a man began. He had been standing at a wall, calling it the edge of the world. The man had not begun in May. He had crossed a line in May. Gerald had missed the difference because it had never occurred to him to look in another country's filing cabinets.

The worst of it was that the man had told him. The first night, on this street, with a plate of Rosa's food on his knee and the whole porch listening, Sandra had asked him where he was from. *The north,* he had said. *The quieter part.* He had said he liked the weather here, less gray. Everyone had pictured Vermont, or Maine, or some cold county with nothing to report, and they had gone back to the casserole. Gerald had dismissed it as an evasions. But it had not been an evasion. It had been the truth, set down in front of the whole porch, wrapped so neatly in small talk that the one man who wrote everything down had filed it as a lie.

Three data points were coincidence. Five were a reasonable suspicion. Seven were a trend. He had been counting points since June. The count had passed seven a long time ago, and he had still been telling himself a careful man could be wrong.

He was not wrong. He had the audit in front of him now, run by a stranger with no reason to flatter him, and the audit said the same thing his gut had said since a man moved in with three boxes and a handshake he'd practiced. The books were too clean. And in thirty-one years, Gerald had never once seen books that clean that someone hadn't kept that way on purpose.

He should have felt vindicated. He felt the way he'd felt the morning he'd signed the papers to retire. Correct, and smaller than the moment.

The water book had been on the corner of the desk since Christmas. Aldric's Christmas gift, a 1962 survey of the township water system, dry as chalk. Gerald had read an hour of it on the day and resented every interesting page. He had looked at the marked note once and set the book aside as one more of the man's gentle eccentricities. He reached for it again now, with Pruitt's voice still in the room.

Page eighty-four. The note was square and yellow, the hand so even it looked set in type.

*Page 84. Your study sits over the original well, capped in '61, not filled. The head of it lies under the northeast corner, beneath your window. I thought a man who values his ground would want to know what is under it. — A.V.*

Gerald read it again. Then he looked at the page.

A survey map. The parcels as they had been before the street, the well-heads inked as small open circles, and the old water rights drawn in fine hatching. He found his own lot without trouble. One of the circles sat beneath its northeast corner.

The northeast corner was the corner he was sitting in. The desk. The lamp. The chair he had dragged here in June and angled so a man could watch the street without the street watching back. The window in front of it looked straight across at number four.

Gerald had owned the house for thirty-one years and had not known the well was there. The man across the street had found it on a sixty-year-old map, then set the note on the exact corner where Gerald sat each night to watch him.

Something turned over at the back of his memory. September. A closed blind, the afternoon sun making a mirror of the glass, no possible way for anyone on the street to see him behind it. And Aldric on his own lawn, raising a hand to the window. Gerald had logged it that day and then taught himself not to believe it. *Venn waved at my window. Blinds were closed.* He had decided it was the sun. He had needed it to be the sun.

Gerald set the book down very carefully, the way you set down a thing you have just understood is heavier than it looks.

The note was a gift. That was the worst of it. Read one way, it was exactly what it said, a kind and curious thing handed to a man who liked his ground. True, useful, and worth money in a dry year. Sandra would read it that way and be charmed. Anyone would.

Read the other way, it was a man pointing, politely, at the chair Gerald watched him from. *I know which window you sit behind. I have known since September.*

He could not make the two readings cancel. He could not fold them into one, either. That was the thing about everything the man did. Each act had a clean surface. Under it sat something Gerald could feel and never quite see. A lemon tree for a casserole. A path cleared before dawn. A book about water, left open on a well no one had known was under the floor, beneath the one corner of the house Gerald had told no one he used.

Gerald turned off the desk lamp.

In the dark, the window stopped being a mirror and became a window again. Across the street, number four glowed warm behind its curtain. One lamp. One chair. One man he could not see but knew was there, reading, or appearing to read, the way Gerald sat at his own window appearing to do anything but what he did.

If he carried the note to Sandra, she would see the gift. If he carried it to anyone, they would see the gift. He had nothing else to show them but a well, a kindness, and the cold space behind his sternum where the certainty should have been.

He left the lamp off. He sat in the chair the note had pointed to and watched the warm square of number four until it went dark. For once he could not have said which of the two men at their windows was the watcher and which the watched.
