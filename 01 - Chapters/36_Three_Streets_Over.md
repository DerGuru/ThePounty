# Chapter 36: Three Streets Over

## Tuesday

The bruise was on the wrist this time.

Rosa saw it when Nora handed Lily her water bottle at four-forty-five and the sleeve rode up an inch on the right arm. A band of yellow-green at the heel of the hand, fading at the edges, a few days old. The kind of bruise that came from being held, not from bumping a doorframe.

Nora pulled the sleeve down. She did it without looking at Rosa and without looking at the sleeve. The movement was automatic. It was the movement of a person who had been pulling that sleeve down for a long time.

"Lily, do you have your hat?"

"It's in my backpack."

"Get it out before we go. It's colder than this morning."

Lily knelt on the floor and unzipped her backpack. Nora stood above her with both arms now crossed, both sleeves pulled down past both wrists, and looked at the bulletin board on the wall as if she had not seen it twelve weeks in a row.

Rosa stayed at the table. She did not get up. She did not cross the room. She had learned, in eleven weeks, that crossing the room was the wrong move. Nora's eyes went to the door whenever the room got smaller.

"Thursday?" Rosa said, lightly, from where she was.

Nora's head turned. "Probably. I'll call if not."

"Okay."

Lily found her hat. They left.

Rosa watched them through the window to the parking lot. Nora's car was the gray Corolla in the second row. She held Lily's hand on the curb, which was good. She opened the passenger door for her before her own, which was also good. She looked at the parking lot before she got in, which was the part Rosa had been watching for.

She looked at the parking lot like a person about to walk into a room.

---

Rosa drove home on Route 9 with the heat at the highest setting and the radio off. She did not turn onto Maple Lane the first time she passed it. She drove a quarter mile past, sat at a red light for a cycle, turned around in the parking lot of the closed garden center, and came back.

She did not know why she had done that.

Yes, she did.

She wanted another minute before she had to be back in her own kitchen, where the question would still be there but the room would be too warm and too full of dinner for the question to fit.

She pulled into the driveway. Dave's truck was there. The kitchen light was on. Across the street, Aldric's porch lamp was lit and the cardigan was on the railing. He was not in the chair. He was inside.

She sat in the car for a moment with the engine off and her hands on the wheel. The cold was already coming through the windshield. She thought about the yellow-green band. She thought about how a person learned to pull a sleeve down without looking at the sleeve. She thought about Lily, seven years old, who had unzipped the backpack without being asked twice, which was not how seven-year-olds normally worked.

She went inside.

Dave was at the stove. She kissed his shoulder. She took the wooden spoon from him and stirred. She did not tell him about the wrist. She told him about DeShawn's spelling test and about Keisha drawing something that looked like a city. She did not tell him about the wrist.

She told him about the wrist at nine-twenty, when they were on the couch and the television was on but neither of them was watching it.

"Three weeks ago it was the upper arm. Today it was the wrist."

Dave did not say anything for a moment. He muted the television.

"You sure?"

"I'm sure."

"Have you said anything to her?"

"No."

"Have you said anything to anyone?"

"I'm saying it to you now."

He thought about that. He was not a man who jumped at things. He thought, and then he spoke.

"There's a hotline."

"I know there's a hotline."

"You could call it. They tell you what to do."

"I know."

"But she hasn't said anything to you."

"No. She hasn't."

Dave looked at the muted television. A car commercial. Bright snow, a road, a family.

"Then it's hard," he said. "Right? That's the hard part. You can't make her ask."

"I know."

"And if you do it wrong, she stops bringing the girl."

"I know."

They sat with that for a minute.

"Rosa."

"Yeah."

"You're going to figure out what to do. You always do. But not tonight."

She leaned her head against his shoulder. He put the volume back on, low, so the room was not silent. She fell asleep there at ten-fifteen and woke up at eleven with her neck stiff and went upstairs and brushed her teeth and slept the rest of the night without dreaming.

## Wednesday

She came down at six-forty to make coffee. Aldric was at his mailbox.

He should not have been at his mailbox at six-forty. The mail did not come until two in the afternoon. He was at the mailbox sometimes in the morning anyway, because he walked, and the mailbox was a place to stop on the walk. But he was usually back inside by six-forty. The cardigan was on. The mug was in his right hand. He was looking at her front door.

She put her coffee in a travel mug. She stepped out onto the porch in her slippers, with her coat thrown over her pajamas. The cold caught her at the collar.

"Morning," she said across the lawn.

"Good morning, Rosa." He raised the mug. "I'm sorry to ambush you. May I ask you something?"

"Of course."

He came across the street at his own pace and stopped at the bottom of her steps. He did not come up. He stayed on the walk with the mug in both hands now.

"The mother of the girl," he said. "Lily, I think you said her name was. Does she pull at her sleeves often, or only when she's tired?"

Rosa stood with her coffee at her chest.

She had told Dave on Tuesday night, on the couch, with the television on low. She had not told anyone else. Aldric had not been at his porch when she had pulled in. The cardigan had been on the railing, undisturbed, since the morning.

"Often," Rosa said.

Aldric nodded. He did not say anything else for a beat. He looked at her front step instead of at her, which she had learned, with him, was a thing he did when he was not asking the next question yet.

"Does the girl flinch when her mother's voice gets sharp?"

Rosa thought.

"Yes."

He nodded once more.

"Thank you." He raised the mug. "Have a good day, Rosa."

He went back across the street, up his own walk, and inside.

Rosa stood on her porch with her coffee getting cold in the travel mug and the cold finding its way past her collar. She did not understand what she had just answered. She understood that she had answered something.

She went back inside and made breakfast and got Marcus on the bus and got Lina to school and did not think about it again until she was at the sink at eleven and realized she had been rinsing the same plate for a minute.

She had told him one fragment. *The mother of the girl.* He had built the rest.

She dried her hands.

She picked up her phone and put it down. She picked it up again. She did not call the hotline. She did not call Diane. She did not call Dave at work.

She put the phone face down on the counter and went outside to bring in the empty trash bins.

Aldric's car was gone from the driveway.

It was a Wednesday morning and his car was sometimes gone on Wednesday mornings. He had errands. He had the library. He had the post office. There was no reason for her to notice that the car was gone.

She noticed that the car was gone.

## Wednesday evening

The candle on his porch was not lit.

Rosa noticed at six-fifteen, pulling the curtains in the living room. The lamp was on, the yellow one. The candle in the hurricane lantern beside the chair was not. He lit the candle in the evening when he sat outside. He did not sit outside when it was below twenty, but he lit it some nights anyway and let it burn while he was inside. It had been burning every night this week.

It was not burning tonight.

She closed the curtain.

## Thursday

The candle was not lit Thursday either. The cardigan was on the railing where it had been Tuesday night. The car was in the driveway by Thursday afternoon, parked as always, perfectly between the lines.

Rosa did not go to the community center Thursday. She told herself it was because Marcus had a fever, which was true. The fever was small. She could have asked Sandra to sit with him for the hour. She did not ask Sandra to sit with him for the hour.

She did not want to be at the center if Nora was not there.

## Friday

Officer Kim got the call at seven-fifty in the morning.

Aggravated assault, hospital admission Thursday night, victim refusing to make a statement. The address on Birch Street had a flag in the system: two domestic disturbance calls in eighteen months, no charges filed, no order of protection. The wife had declined to press both times. The neighbors had called both times. This time it was not the neighbors. It was the wife, who had driven him to the emergency room at eleven-thirty Thursday night and dropped him at the door.

Kim drove to the hospital. She took the long way, because the long way put her past Maple Lane.

She did not look at Aldric Venn's porch as she went by. She looked at the rest of the street. The porch was a thing she noticed without turning her head.

The candle was lit.

She filed the observation under *driving past* and did not think about it again until later.

---

The hospital was the regional one off the parkway. She found Brian Lasko in a single room on the third floor, bandaged from both elbows down. Both wrists in casts. Three fingers on the right hand splinted. One on the left. The left thumb was wrapped separately. His face was untouched.

Kim read the chart before she went in. The orthopedic notes were detailed.

*Bilateral fractures, both scaphoid bones. Bilateral hamate fractures. Right hand: third, fourth, fifth metacarpals at the neck. Left hand: fifth metacarpal at the neck. Multiple distal phalanges. No fractures proximal to the wrist. No skull, facial, rib, or long-bone trauma. No defensive wounds on forearms.*

Kim read it twice.

A man in a bar fight broke his face, his ribs, and his knuckles. A man who fell from a height broke his wrists trying to catch himself, and the breaks were dirty, comminuted, with the rest of the arm taking the rest of the energy. A man who had been hit by a car broke long bones.

A man whose hands had been broken, only his hands, every bone in both hands that mattered for grip, and nothing else anywhere on his body, had had his hands broken on purpose, one by one, by someone who knew which bones were which.

Kim closed the chart and went into the room.

"Mr. Lasko. I'm Officer Kim, Greenfield Police. I'm here to take a statement about what happened last night."

He looked at her from the bed. He was forty-one, blond, the kind of build that meant he had played football in high school and never really stopped eating like it. His eyes were wet but not from pain. The pain was managed. The eyes were something else.

"I don't want to make a statement."

"All right." Kim sat in the chair. She did not open her notebook. "Can you tell me anything about what happened?"

"No."

"Did you see who did it?"

"No."

"Were you home when it happened?"

A pause. He looked at the ceiling. "Yes."

"Was anyone else home?"

"No."

That was a lie. The neighbors three doors down had reported a woman and a child leaving the Lasko residence at approximately eight p.m. Thursday in the gray Corolla, which had not returned until eleven-fifteen. Kim let the lie sit.

"Mr. Lasko." She kept her voice flat. "I'm not going to push. You don't have to talk to me. But I'm going to tell you what I see, because what I see is going to be in my report whether you talk to me or not."

He looked at the ceiling.

"What I see is a person who was held down by one person while a second person did the breaking. There aren't any defensive marks on your forearms, so you weren't fighting back when it happened. The breaks are on both hands in the same places, which means whoever did it knew how to do it. Your face is fine, your ribs are fine, your legs are fine. Whoever did this had a very specific reason to leave the rest of you alone."

She let that sit.

"You're going to heal," she said. "The orthopedic surgeon says nine to twelve weeks for full function. You will not be able to grip anything for that time. You will not be able to make a fist."

He did not look at her.

"If you want to tell me anything, even off the record, I'll listen. If you don't, that's your right. I'll come back next week."

She stood up. She was at the door when he said it.

"He asked me."

She turned.

"What?"

"The one who did it. Before. He asked me." His voice was small. "If I wanted to stop. He said it like he meant it."

"Stop what?"

He did not answer.

"Mr. Lasko. Stop what?"

He closed his eyes. A tear went sideways into his hair.

Kim waited. He did not open his eyes again. After a minute she stepped out of the room and closed the door behind her.

She stood in the hallway for a long time.

---

She did not drive past Maple Lane on the way back to the station. She drove the direct route.

She wrote her report at her desk. *Victim refused to provide statement regarding assailant. Victim made one statement of note, off the record, to the effect that he had been offered an opportunity to stop a behavior prior to the assault. Victim did not specify the behavior. No suspect identified. Wife (Nora Lasko, 32) declined to provide statement, exercising spousal privilege regarding events in the residence. Two prior domestic disturbance calls at this address, both withdrawn. Recommend follow-up contact with both parties in seven to ten days.*

She read it back. It was a clean report. It said exactly what had happened, and exactly nothing about what she thought.

She filed it.

At the end of her shift she signed out the cruiser and drove home. The route home went past Maple Lane, because the route home had gone past Maple Lane for four months now.

It was six-ten and the sky was already going. Aldric Venn was on his porch in the cardigan with the candle lit. He had a mug. He looked at the street.

He saw the cruiser. He raised his hand.

Kim raised hers back.

She drove to the end of the block, pulled to the curb, and put the cruiser in park. The radio was quiet. The street was quiet. Somebody three houses down had a wind chime going.

A man had come home with hands that would not grip for three months. The hands of a man who had used his hands to hurt his wife for at least a year and a half, probably longer. The wife had not pressed charges twice. The neighbors had given up calling. The system, as Kim had to use the system, had no entry point.

And now the man had hands that would not grip for three months, and his wife had three months to do whatever she was going to do, and the only person on the street who had asked the question that had to be asked to know there was a question at all had been a retired consultant in a slate-blue cardigan who had never met Nora Lasko in his life.

A man on a porch raised his hand. A police officer raised hers back.

She put the cruiser in drive and went home.
