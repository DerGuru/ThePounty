# Prologue: The Market

*Saturday, the 5th of July 2025*

The market filled up early on a good Saturday, and this was a good Saturday, so by nine the green was thick with strollers, dogs on long leads, and people who had nowhere to be but wanted company while they got there. Miranda liked it full. A thin market left you standing out in it. A full one let you be furniture, one more body turning fruit over with no place to be.

She'd been coming three Saturdays for a reason that had nothing to do with fruit.

The first Saturday she'd told herself it was the tomatoes. The second she'd quit bothering with the story. She came to look for the guy from church, and three weeks running he'd been here, which told her something on its own. Nobody came to a farmers' market three weeks straight for the bread. You came because the market was where the people were, and you wanted the people without having to talk to them. She knew the want. She had it herself.

She found him like she always did now, by not looking. She worked the stalls with her eyes down and her attention everywhere else. Somewhere around the cut flowers she felt the shape of him at the edge of things. Gray hair. A canvas bag. A man in no hurry, reading the crowd like other people read a paper, mild and unbothered, taking it all in.

Then she felt the other thing, and the morning shifted half an inch.

He was looking at her.

Not a glance. Not the loose, sliding look the market handed around for free. This one had weight on it. It sat on her like his stillness sat on everything, patient and decided, in no rush to move off. Three weeks she'd been the one doing the looking, careful as a cat, sure she had the jump on him. And here he was with his eyes resting on her like he'd known where she stood the whole time and had only now bothered to let her see it.

Her first thought was that she'd gotten sloppy.

Her second, slower and colder, was that she hadn't. She didn't get sloppy. Not at this. This was the one thing she'd never once been sloppy at in her life.

Which left the other reading, and she didn't much like it. He'd let her catch him. A man that still, that careful, didn't hand you his attention across a crowded green by accident. He'd set it down where she'd find it, the same as the vendor a few weeks back had set his thumb on the scale, except she couldn't yet see what he was weighing or what it would cost her.

So she did what she did. She straightened the crooked thing. The itch came up off her like heat off a stovetop, there and physical and wanting something done. What it wanted was to know where he went when he finished being nobody at the market. If he'd opened the door, she'd walk through it. Let him think he'd shown her something. She'd take more than he meant to give.

He bought his bread. He didn't hurry it. She drifted two stalls down, bought a pint of something she didn't want so her hands had a reason, and watched him cross to the lot at the far side without once looking back at her. That bothered her more than the looking had. A man checked his tail. He didn't. Either he was careless, which he wasn't, or he didn't think he needed to.

She let it go for now. She had a car to get to.

His was a Volvo, old, dark green, one of those square solid things built back when a car was meant to outlast its owner, then sent off to do exactly that. She'd clocked it the second Saturday and filed it. It pulled out slow. Everything about him was slow, an unhurriedness she was starting to read as its own kind of armor. She gave him a gap, slid into traffic three cars back, and followed.

He didn't make it hard. That stuck with her the whole way. He signaled every turn well ahead, held the speed limit like it was a personal value, and slowed for a yellow that most people would've taken. A careful man going home. He led her maybe a mile: past the school, over the little bridge, and into a development with a wooden sign and trees for street names. Oak. Birch. Then a left, onto a street so tidy it looked drawn with a ruler, white fences all of a height, mailboxes spaced out like teeth.

Maple Lane.

A few streets over from her own rented place. That landed wrong. All this time she'd pegged him as a man from somewhere else, somewhere she'd have to work to reach, and he'd been parking that green tank a six-minute drive from her bed.

She didn't turn in. She slowed at the mouth of the street and let him roll on without her, like you let a fish run before setting the hook. He pulled into a driveway partway down. Number four. A beige house with a porch that wrapped halfway around. He got out with his bread and his bag, unhurried as ever. Two doors down, a man lifted a hand off a lawnmower and called something friendly across the grass.

She knew that man.

Not his name. His face. He'd been a few pews up at the church a handful of Sundays this spring, him, a wife, and two kids, the kind of family that took up a whole row and didn't mind. The wife came out onto the porch now with a dish towel over her shoulder, said something, laughed at whatever came back. Church people. The friendly kind. The kind who'd have a new face at coffee hour pegged and fed inside a month.

Miranda sat at the end of the street with the engine running and a pint of berries she didn't want sweating on the seat. She did the math without trying to, like she always did. The careful man lived at number four. The warm family lived at number two, close enough to borrow sugar. And the warm family went to her church.

There it was. The door, standing open. She hadn't even had to find it. It had been handed to her, gift-wrapped, two doors down from the man she'd come to take apart.

She'd be in that pew tomorrow. Front half, this time. She'd let the warm wife find her, because warm people liked nothing better than finding someone, and a stranger who let herself be found was halfway to belonging before she said a word.

She put the car in gear. She didn't look at number four as she rolled past it, didn't give it the satisfaction, though she marked the dark-green Volvo in the drive, the empty flower box, and the chair angled at the front window, set just back from the glass where a man could sit and watch a street without the street watching him.

She knew that trick. She had a window like it herself.

She drove the six minutes home to her own half-empty place, already three moves into a game she was sure, right to the edge of certain, that she was the one playing.
