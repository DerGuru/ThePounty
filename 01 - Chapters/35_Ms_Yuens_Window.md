# Chapter 35: Ms. Yuen's Window

*Saturday, the 8th of February 2025*

The kettle clicked off at five-forty. Ms. Yuen warmed the pot, measured out the oolong and poured the water over the leaves. The tin had been Aldric's gift at Christmas. A loose-leaf oolong with a small card that said only *for the morning cup*. Other people would have kept a tin like that for a Sunday, or for company. She had opened it the day after Christmas. Tea did not improve by sitting in a closed tin. She carried the cup to the front window. Snow sat on the lawns in flat panels, with wind-edges visible at the curbs. The streetlamps were on. Saturday morning had not yet begun, which was the version of Saturday morning she preferred.

She stood at the window.

The Ortegas' porch light was off. The Hoopers' newspaper sat on their driveway in its plastic sleeve. Number four was dark, except for a thin line of kitchen light. He was up. He was always up by a quarter to six. She had been keeping a record without setting out to.

Eleven years on this street had given her a slow library of habits. Mrs. Chen's curtains. The cat at number nine that no one would claim. The order in which the Ortega children appeared at the bus stop. The library kept itself. She added to it without effort.

The Aldric entry had grown.

She drank her tea.

She finished the cup standing there. At six-twenty the kitchen light at number four went off, the porch light came on, and she saw him for a moment on his porch in his coat with a snow shovel in his hand. Then he was off the porch, but she could not see where he went from her angle. She turned away from the window. She washed the cup, dried it and put it back in the cupboard.

At eight-fifteen she opened her front door to bring in yesterday's mail from the box.

Her walk had been cleared.

She stood in the doorway in her slippers and looked at it. From porch to curb, four feet wide, the snow lifted in clean rectangular bites and piled neatly at the edge of the lawn. Not a path. The whole walk. Salt had been laid down where the concrete dipped at the third slab, the place where ice always formed because the slab had settled. 

She brought in the mail.

She did not call to thank him. She did not write a note. He had not knocked. He had not left the shovel leaning against her railing as a sign. He had cleared the walk and gone. To thank him would have been to insist that the walk had been a favor, when it had been something quieter.

She put the mail on the kitchen table and comed through it.

She took her walk at nine, when the light was good and the ice had begun to grain under the sun. Her knee was tolerable today. She went down to the end of the block, around the loop, through the park and back. The street had woken up. A delivery van was reversing at the Hoopers'. Marcus Ortega was building something out of the cleared snow in his front yard that looked more and more like a fortress with every day passed and every inch of snow, that fell.

The cleared walks led down her side of the street like a thread. The Alvarez house. The Chens', who were young enough to do it themselves and would not have minded the help. Then his own. Then nothing past that, because past that was the Bukoviches', who had a service.

Aldric was on his porch with a mug.

He saw her before she saw him. By the time she had lifted her head, his hand was already raised. *Good morning, Ms. Yuen. How is the knee?* Same words, same warmth, every time. She had stopped finding it formulaic. Some people repeated themselves because they had nothing else. He repeated himself because the question still mattered to him on the seventh asking as it had on the first.

"It is tolerable, thank you. The salt on the slab is appreciated."

"It looked like it wanted attention."

"It has wanted attention since the second year I lived here. No one had noticed before."

"It was visible in the snow. The other slabs held a flatter coat."

"You read concrete now."

He almost smiled. "I read what is in front of me."

She nodded and walked on.

She had reached the corner before she registered the other thing. His back had been to the side railing. Not the lawn, not the street. The railing. From there he could see the lawn, the street, the gap between the Chens' and the Hoopers', and a corner of his own back yard. She did not know which of those he had been watching. She suspected the answer was *all of them at once*. She suspected he was not aware that he was doing it. She suspected he had stopped being aware of it a long time ago.

She finished her loop.

At eleven the doorbell rang. It was Maria Alvarez, with a plate.

"For you," Maria said. "Bizcocho. I made too much. Aldric got one too."

"Thank you, Maria. That's good. He cleared my walk."

"He did mine, too. Before I was even awake."

"He works early."

Maria looked at her for a small extra moment. The two women understood the look. They had not exchanged it before, but they had each held it on their own at different windows on different mornings, and to recognize it on another face was a relief, even if neither said so. Maria left. Ms. Yuen put the plate on the kitchen counter.

She drank a second cup of tea at the table.

She thought about the morning.

He was a strange man. She had decided that some time ago. She thought instead about the *kind* of strange. There were many kinds. Sandra was a strange woman in one direction. Gerald was a strange man in another. None of those strangenesses required explanation. They sat in the street, were absorbed by it, and the street kept moving.

Aldric's strangeness had a different shape.

He was a man who had organized himself. Not as a person organized a closet, or a calendar. As a person organized a *position*. The shovel went out at five-fifty because that was the right time for snow to be lifted before it set. The salt went on the third slab because that was the one, which needed salt. His smile arrived a fraction late because it was being chosen. When he sat on his porch and read, the book was open but he was also reading the street. When he made her tea that one afternoon, he had made it the way she preferred without asking, because he had filed the preference on the day she had mentioned it, possibly without intending to.

A man did not organize himself like that without having needed to.

Her husband had needed to, once. Long ago. Before. He had stopped needing to in the early years of their marriage and had been a comfortable man for the rest of his life. The shape of the needing had stayed in his hands, though, and she had recognized it the first time she had seen Aldric Venn carry a casserole dish across the street.

She poured a third cup. She did not usually have three.

She walked past his house again on her second loop, when the light was beginning to fade.

He was on the porch with the phone to his ear. He was not talking. He was listening.

She did not slow. She did not turn her head. The walk past his lawn was twenty-four strides at her pace, and in the ninth one his voice came across the cold air, low and short.

"I understand. Thank you. No, that is sufficient."

That was all. He lowered his phone and was looking at the screen and put it in his pocket. When she looked at him out of the side of her eye, the small careful arrangement of his face was already gone, the porch face back in its place. She would not have known a phone call had happened if she had arrived three seconds later.

She walked by the ortegas and crossed the street.

When she arrived at the Hoopers' house, the porch was empty. The hallway window beside his front door was lit and she could see him inside.

He was on the stairs in the slate-blue cardigan, coming down. His coat lay folded over the banister at the bottom. In both hands he carried a small flat metal box, painted matte gray, no larger than a hardcover book. The lid was closed. He was not looking at the stairs. He was looking at the box.

She watched for the second it took her to pass the gap of the window, then she was past it and walking on.

She did not look back. People who looked back were either confused or rude, and she was neither.

She did not wonder what was in the box, but it was the carrying that stayed with her on the walk home. He had gone upstairs to get it. He had carried it down with both hands when one hand would have been enough. He had not opened it. A man who needed to open a box opened it where it was. A man who needed to *consider* opening it brought it somewhere else first, where the decision belonged to a different room than the storage did.

Her husband had done the same thing. Twice, in thirty years of marriage. Both times he had brought it down, set it on the kitchen table, sat with it for an hour, then put it back upstairs without opening it. She had never asked.

She made dinner. She ate at the table by the window with the small lamp on. After dinner she washed the plate, dried it and put it away. She refilled the kettle for the evening tea but did not turn it on yet. She sat in the armchair by the window with the lamp off. The street settled into its evening quiet. The Ortegas' kitchen window glowed yellow. The Hoopers' television flickered blue through the blinds, illuminating the snow in strobes of color. Number four's porch light was on, low and warm, the same setting it had held since the first weekend he had moved in.

She thought about her husband.

She did not think about him every day anymore. She had decided years ago that thinking about him every day was a thing other widows did, who had not had what she had had. Hers had been a good marriage, and a good marriage did not need to be carried around like an injury. He came when he was needed. Today he had been needed.

He had moved like Aldric Venn moved, before he was old. Not all the time. Only in certain seasons of their life, the ones she had not asked about. The shape of the movement was the same. The economy. The box. The small late smile.

He had been a good man, but not a soft one. There was a difference.

A car went past on the wet street, slow. Not one she recognized. It did not stop. It turned left at the end of the block.

She had decided about him months ago. The morning had not changed the decision. It had only filled in around it. He was a dangerous man who had chosen to be kind, and that was a thing she knew how to live next to. Her husband had taught her how.

She turned the kettle on.
