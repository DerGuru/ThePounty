# Chapter 17: The School Problem

The thing about Aiden Parker was that he wasn't stupid. He was smart enough to only say things when no teachers were close. Smart enough to smile after, like it was a joke. Smart enough to pick the moments when Lina was alone or when the only people around were kids who'd laugh.

It had started in September. First week back. Lina had been eating lunch at her usual table, the one near the window where Emma and Sofía sat, and Aiden had walked past and said, "What *is* that?" He was looking at her food. Mom had made rice and beans with leftover chicken and a lime wedge. Normal food. Home food.

"Smells weird," Aiden said. Two of his friends laughed. He walked on.

It was nothing. Lina told herself it was nothing. Kids said dumb things. She ate her rice.

But it kept happening.

"Ortega." Not Lina. Never Lina. Just the last name, like a label. "Hey, Ortega, is that *dog food*?" The other kids laughed, or they didn't, but Aiden didn't care either way. He said it for himself. He said it to see her face.

Then, six weeks in, the one that stuck: "Why don't you go back to where you came from?"

Lina was from here. She was born at Mercy General, twelve minutes away. She'd lived on Maple Lane her whole life. She knew every crack in the sidewalk between her house and the park. She was *from here*.

She didn't say that. She didn't say anything. She picked up her tray and walked away, and her face felt hot and her chest felt tight and she thought: *I'm not going to cry about this. I'm not.*

She didn't cry. Not then. Not after. She just stopped talking about school.

Mom noticed. Of course Mom noticed. Mom noticed everything. She had that look for a week, the one where her eyes followed Lina around the house like she was reading something invisible on her back. And eventually Lina told her. On the stairs, on a Tuesday night, because she was tired of carrying it and because Mom was folding laundry and the house was quiet and it just came out.

Mom didn't freak out. She didn't hug too hard or say too much. She just sat down and said, "How long?" and when Lina said "since September," something in Mom's face went still. Not angry. Worse than angry. The kind of still that meant she was holding something back.

Mom called the school. Lina knew because she heard her on the phone the next morning, using her polite-but-firm voice. The voice that meant she wasn't asking.

Nothing happened.

Thursday. Friday. Nothing changed. Aiden still said things. Not every day. But enough that the space around him felt sharp, like broken glass she had to step around.

On Friday, Lina saw Mom talking to Mr. Venn at the fence. She was watching from her bedroom window. They talked for a while. Mr. Venn was standing with his hands in his pockets, listening. Mom was doing the thing with the hose where she coiled it around her arm when she was thinking. Lina didn't know what they were talking about. Could have been anything. Could have been chrysanthemums.

The weekend passed. Sunday dinner was okay. Marcus made her laugh by accident, which was the only way Marcus ever made anyone laugh.

Monday was a normal day until recess after second class.

Lina was crossing the hallway toward the water fountain when she saw Mr. Venn outside the teachers' lounge. He was talking to Mrs. Hendricks. They were standing by the door to the teachers' lounge, and Mr. Venn had that posture he always had. Straight, relaxed, hands at his sides. Like he belonged wherever he was.

He didn't belong in her school. He didn't have kids here. He didn't have any reason to be in that hallway that Lina could think of.

Mrs. Hendricks was nodding. Mr. Venn said something, and she nodded again, and then she went back inside. Mr. Venn turned toward the exit.

He saw Lina.

He smiled. The regular smile, the one she knew from the dinner at their house. He raised his hand in a small wave.

Lina waved back. Then she went to class.

After school, she came out the main doors with Emma, and Mr. Venn was on the sidewalk across the street. Not at the school. Just on the sidewalk, walking in the direction of Maple Lane. The same direction Lina walked when Mom didn't pick her up.

"I'll catch up," Lina told Emma, and crossed at the light.

"Hello, Lina," Mr. Venn said when she fell into step beside him. He didn't slow down or speed up. He just adjusted, the way tall people sometimes do when they walk with someone shorter. "How was your day?"

"Fine." She looked up at him. "Why were you at my school?"

He didn't hesitate. "I had a conversation with your teacher."

"About what?"

"About the environment in Mrs. Hendricks' class." He looked down at her. His face was calm. No worry, no fake cheerfulness. Just there. "I wanted to understand the situation better."

Lina walked for a few steps. "Mom told you."

"She did."

"She's worried."

"She has reason to be. You shouldn't have to deal with what you're dealing with." He said it like a fact.

Lina didn't know what to say to that. So she said, "Are you going to fix it?"

Mr. Venn was quiet for a moment. Three steps, four. "I'm going to talk to some people," he said. "I can't promise you a timeline. But I can promise you that I take it seriously."

"Okay." 

They walked the rest of the way without talking much. Mr. Venn asked about the volcano project and seemed genuinely interested in the eruption-height problem her group was having. He suggested food colors for a more realistic effect and Lina filed that away.

They arrived at the corner of Maple Lane, when he said, "Have a good evening, Lina," and walked to his house. Lina walked to hers.

Mom was in the kitchen. Marcus was on the floor with blocks.

"Hey, baby. How was school?"

"Fine." Lina dropped her backpack. "Mr. Venn was at school today."

Mom's hands stopped moving. Just for a second. Then they started again. "Was he?"

"He was talking to Mrs. Hendricks. And then I walked home with him."

"You walked home with him?"

"We were just going the same way." Lina opened the fridge. "He said he's going to talk to some people."

Mom didn't say anything. When Lina looked up with the juice in her hand, Mom was looking out the window. Toward Mr. Venn's house.

"Okay," Mom said. "That's good."

The next morning, Mom drove her. That was normal now. Lina didn't mind. She had her earbuds in, and Mom didn't try to talk, which was the best kind of car ride.

Mom parked in the lot and waved her goodbye. Lina was about to go in when she saw him again.

Mr. Venn was standing near the flagpole. He was talking to two people. A man and a woman. The man was tall, bigger than Mr. Venn, wearing a NASCAR t-shirt even though it was October and cold. The woman had her arms crossed.

Lina knew who they were. She'd seen the man at pickup. That was Aiden's dad.

Mr. Venn was smiling. The same smile as always. He was saying something Lina couldn't hear. Aiden's mom was looking at the ground, and his dad was leaning forward.

Then he got loud.

"—the hell do you think you are?" His voice carried across the lot. Other parents looked. A mom near the bus lane turned her head. "You come to my kid's school and—"

"—my son is not—"

Lina stood still. She was maybe twenty feet away. Close enough to hear the volume, not close enough for all the words. But she could see Mr. Venn's face.

His smile left.

Just gone. Like a door closing. What was underneath wasn't angry or mean. It was just... nothing. Flat. Still. Mr. Venn stood there with his hands at his sides, looking up at Aiden's dad, and his face was like a wall with no windows.

Aiden's dad was still talking. His voice was getting louder. He took a step forward.

Mr. Venn said something. Lina heard the sound of his voice but not the words. It was quiet. Short. One sentence.

Aiden's dad stopped talking.

It was like someone had turned off the sound. One second he was big and loud and leaning in, and the next he was just standing there. His shoulders came down. He looked at Mr. Venn the way Lina's cat looked at the neighbor's dog. Very still.

Mr. Venn said something else. The man nodded.

Then Mr. Venn smiled again. A smaller smile. He put out his hand. Aiden's dad took it. They shook. The woman said something quiet and they walked toward the school entrance. Aiden's dad didn't look back.

Mr. Venn turned and walked toward the street. He saw Lina. This time he didn't wave. He just smiled at her and nodded once, the way adults nod when they know you saw something.

Lina went inside.

She didn't know what Mr. Venn had said. She didn't know what made Aiden's dad go from loud to quiet in one sentence. She knew what she'd seen, and she knew what she'd felt watching it. Not scared exactly. It was more like standing very close to a high fence and realizing for the first time how tall it actually was.

At lunch, Aiden came to her table. He stood there for a second, looking at the floor. Then he said, "Sorry. For the stuff I said."

Lina looked at him. He wasn't looking at her. He was looking at his shoes. His ears were red.

"Okay," she said.

He walked away. Emma looked at Lina with wide eyes. "What was *that*?"

"I don't know," Lina said. But she kind of did.

That afternoon, she got in Mom's car and the world felt lighter. Not fixed. But lighter, like someone had taken a book out of her backpack.

"How was school?" Mom said.

"Good." Lina buckled her seatbelt. "Aiden said sorry."

Mom's hands tightened on the steering wheel. Just a little. "He did?"

"Yeah. At lunch. Just came up and said it." She looked out the window. "Also, Mr. Venn was at school again this morning. He was talking to Aiden's parents."

"He was?"

"Aiden's dad got really loud. Like, yelling." Lina picked at a thread on her backpack strap. "Then Mr. Venn said something and he just... stopped."

Mom didn't say anything for a moment. She was driving, eyes on the road, but Lina could tell she was thinking. That was the thing about Mom. She could think and drive at the same time, but you could always tell which one she was doing more of.

"What did Mr. Venn say?" Mom asked.

"I don't know. I couldn't hear it. But it was short." Lina tried to find the right words. "He stopped smiling first. And then whatever he said made Aiden's dad get small."

Mom looked at her in the rearview mirror. "Get small?"

"Like... less loud. Less big." Lina shrugged. "I don't know how to explain it."

"You're explaining it fine," Mom said quietly.

At home, Dad was already back. He was in the kitchen, cutting something. Mom went in and Lina heard them talking. Low voices. She caught pieces. "...at the school..." and "...said something..." and Dad: "Jesus."

She went to her room and did her homework. Not because she wanted to. Because she could. For the first time in six weeks, she could sit at her desk and think about math without thinking about what tomorrow would be like.

Wednesday was better. Aiden didn't talk to her, but he didn't say anything either. He just existed in the same room without being sharp. 

Thursday, he asked to borrow a pencil. She gave him one. He said thanks.

Friday, Mrs. Hendricks put them in the same group for the volcano presentation. Lina almost said no. But Aiden just nodded and said, "Cool," and then asked what they needed to buy for materials. Like a normal kid asking a normal question.

On Saturday, Lina was on the porch reading when Mr. Venn came out of his house across the street. He saw her and raised his hand.

"Good morning, Lina."

"Good morning, Mr. Venn."

He walked down his steps. "How are things?"

"Better," she said. "A lot better."

He smiled. The regular one. The warm one. "I'm glad to hear that."

Lina looked at him across the street. Same Mr. Venn as always. Cardigan, straight posture, polite voice. She thought about his face at the flagpole. The flat one. The one without the smile. She thought about Aiden's dad, getting quiet.

"Mr. Venn?"

"Yes?"

"What did you say to Aiden's dad?"

He tilted his head. "I explained the situation."

"That's it?"

"That's it." He paused. "Sometimes people need to hear clearly what they already know."

Lina turned that over. It didn't quite make sense. But it did not *not* make sense either.

It was more than the grown-ups got, she thought. When they asked him things, they got the nice voice and no answer. He had actually told her something true.

"Okay," she said.

"Tell your mother I said hello."

"I will."

Mr. Venn walked on. Lina went back to her book. The sun was cold but the porch was out of the wind and the story was getting good. She didn't think about Aiden. She didn't think about lunch or the words or going back. She just read.

Inside, she heard Mom start the coffee machine. Heard Dad say something and Mom laugh. Normal sounds. Saturday sounds.

She thought, once more, about Mr. Venn's face going flat. About how a voice that quiet could make a loud man stop.

Then she turned the page.
