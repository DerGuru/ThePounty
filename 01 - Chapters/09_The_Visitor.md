# Chapter 9: The Visitor

Dave was pressure-washing the driveway when the car pulled up across the street. Silver sedan, current model year, rental-clean. No bumper stickers, no dents, no parking permits. The kind of car that looked like it had never been anywhere before today.

He killed the pressure washer. The silence rushed back in. Sprinkler at the Hoopers, a lawnmower two streets over, Marcus shouting something at Lina through the den window. Normal Saturday afternoon sounds. Late August heat pressing down on everything.

The car sat at the curb in front of Aldric's house for a full minute before the door opened. A man got out. Forty-five, maybe fifty. Gray suit, no tie, white shirt open at the collar. Sunglasses, which he took off and folded into his breast pocket. He straightened his jacket. He looked at Aldric's house the way someone might look at a restaurant they'd booked a table at. Expectantly.

Dave wiped his hands on his shorts. He wasn't staring. He was taking a break from the driveway, hydrating. He happened to face that direction.

The man walked up Aldric's path. Good shoes. Dave noticed that because they were too good for a Saturday afternoon in a neighborhood where the fanciest footwear was Gerald's church loafers. These were the kind of shoes you saw in airport lounges. The man's posture was straight but not stiff, his pace measured. Everything about him was calibrated to look unremarkable, and the fact that Dave noticed that meant the calibration wasn't quite right.

He knocked. Three knocks. Even spacing. Dave's brain offered categories: insurance adjuster? Financial advisor? Lawyer? None of them fit. Insurance guys had folders. Financial advisors had smiles. Lawyers had that particular tiredness around the eyes. This man had nothing. His face was clean of anything that could be read.

Aldric opened the door. Dave couldn't hear what was said, but he saw the posture. Aldric stood in the doorway for a beat longer than he stood for anyone else. Not surprised. Not pleased. He stepped aside. The man went in. The door closed.

Dave picked up the pressure washer wand. He didn't turn it on. He stood there looking at the driveway, at the fan of water still pooling in the low spot near the garage, at the moss stain he'd been working on for the last ten minutes.

Something about the man's walk. Something about the way Aldric hadn't smiled.

Aldric smiled at everyone. At Gerald, at Kevin the mailman, at the Parkers' kid who refused to make eye contact. He smiled at people knocking on his door the way other people breathed. It was automatic. It was always there. Dave had never seen it not be there.

He turned the pressure washer back on. The noise filled the street. He worked the moss stain until it was gone and then worked the concrete around it until the whole section matched. Twenty minutes passed. Twenty-five.

The door opened. Dave killed the washer again.

The man came out first. He was putting his sunglasses back on. Aldric followed him to the porch. They stood there for a moment. Aldric said something. The man nodded once. No handshake. The man turned, walked back to the car, got in, started it and pulled away. No wave. No backward look.

Aldric stood on the porch. His hands were at his sides. He was looking at the street, but not at anything on it. Dave had seen that look once before, on his college roommate the morning after his father called about the diagnosis. Looking at everything, seeing something else.

Then Aldric turned his head. He saw Dave. The smile came back like a light switching on.

"Dave. Good afternoon. The driveway looks excellent."

"Thanks." Dave coiled the hose. "Everything OK?"

"Perfectly fine. Old acquaintance." Aldric lifted one hand. A wave, a dismissal, a period at the end of a sentence. "Enjoy your Saturday."

He went inside. The door closed. The porch was empty.

Dave finished the driveway. He put the pressure washer back in the garage. He went inside, showered and came downstairs. Rosa was in the kitchen cutting tomatoes for dinner. Marcus was drawing at the table. Lina was nowhere to be seen, which meant she was upstairs with her headphones on.

"Aldric had a visitor," Dave said.

Rosa looked up. "Oh? Who?"

"No idea. Guy in a suit. Silver car." He opened the fridge, took out a beer, closed it. "Something felt off."

"Off how?"

Dave twisted the cap. He took a sip. He leaned against the counter next to her and tried to find the word.

"Smooth," he said. "Too smooth. Like everything about him was on purpose."

Rosa's knife paused on the cutting board. "Isn't everything about Aldric on purpose?"

"Yeah, but—" Dave took another sip. "Aldric does it warm. This guy did it cold. Same thing, different temperature." He looked at the beer label. Peeled at the corner. "Like someone who practices looking normal. But hasn't gotten to the part where it stops looking like practice."

Rosa went back to cutting. Three slow slices. Then she said: "Aldric didn't come out tonight?"

Dave turned and looked through the kitchen window. Aldric's porch. The chair was empty. The lamp was off. At seven-thirty on a Saturday evening in August, when the heat finally let up and the street smelled like barbecue and freshly watered lawns, Aldric's porch was dark.

In three months, Dave had never seen that porch dark on a warm evening. Not once.

"No," he said. "He didn't."

Rosa picked up another tomato. She didn't say anything else. Dave drank his beer. Marcus was drawing a dinosaur with too many legs.

Dave looked at the dark porch one more time. Then he looked at his son's drawing, at his wife's hands on the cutting board, at the beer in his hand.

He decided not to think about it.
