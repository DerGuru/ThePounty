# Chapter 14: The Market Man

The Saturday market on Greenfield took up half the parking lot behind the Methodist church. Dave went most weeks, not because he needed anything, but because Rosa gave him a list and the list gave him a reason to leave the house before the kids woke up.

Late September had turned the air sharp. He could smell woodsmoke from somewhere, faint and sweet under the diesel of the food trucks. The stalls were half-set when he arrived, vendors hauling crates of late-season squash, jars of honey, bread wrapped in wax paper. A woman was arranging flowers in metal buckets, big ones in reds and oranges. A man in a flannel shirt was stacking apple crates three high, the apples sorted by color in a way that looked accidental.

Dave parked the truck and checked Rosa's list. Tomatoes, basil, eggs. Something called "delicata squash" which he'd have to look up. And at the bottom, in her handwriting: *if they have the sourdough bread, get two.*

He was reading the list a second time when Aldric appeared beside his truck. He'd walked over from somewhere in the lot, but Dave hadn't seen him coming. That was the thing about Aldric. He moved very quietly for a man his size.

"Good morning, Dave." He was wearing a linen jacket over a dark shirt. His hands were empty. He looked like someone who'd come to browse with no intention of buying, which was exactly what rich people did at farmers' markets.

"Hey. Didn't know you came to these."

"Most Saturdays, if the weather permits. The eggs here are considerably better than what the grocery store offers."

"Rosa says the same thing."

"Rosa is correct." He smiled. "Shall we?"

They walked the market together. It wasn't planned. You started going somewhere and Aldric was next to you, matching your pace without effort. Dave got the tomatoes from the first stall, picked through them like Rosa had taught him. Firm but not hard. No cracks near the stem.

Aldric bought eggs. A full dozen from a woman with a red tent. He examined the carton with what Dave could only describe as attention and he looked at eggs like other people looked at contracts.

"You checking for cracks?" Dave said.

"Shell integrity and uniformity of color. Both indicate consistent calcium in the feed. These are excellent." He closed the carton gently. "The basil is three stalls east, if you're looking."

Dave was looking. He hadn't said so. But Aldric must have read the list over his shoulder at some point, or he'd remembered Rosa mentioning basil last week, or he simply knew. Dave had stopped trying to figure out which one it was.

They worked their way through the vendors. Dave found everything on Rosa's list except the squash. Aldric helped him identify it. Long, cream-colored with green stripes, ribbed like a decorative gourd.

"These are not decorative," Aldric said, reading Dave's face. "They're quite good roasted with brown butter."

"If you say so."

"I do." He picked up two. "Take these two. The weight is even."

Dave paid and they moved on.

At the far end of the parking lot, past the prepared food trucks, there was a stall Dave always skipped. Hand-lettered sign: PRESERVES. Jars of jams, pickles, and canned tomatoes. The man behind the table was heavy and ruddy, wearing a canvas apron that had seen better decades. Dave had bought from him once, two years ago. The jam had been fine. The man had been less fine. Something about the way he'd counted change that made Dave check his wallet afterward.

Aldric stopped.

"Do you know this vendor?" he said.

"Sort of. His jam's okay."

A woman was at the stall. Older. She wore a cardigan despite the mild weather not really warranting one yet. She had three jars in her hands, two strawberry, one something darker. She set them on the table. The man punched numbers into a calculator. His lips moved with the numbers.

"Eighteen fifty-three," he said.

The woman opened her purse. She took out a twenty and handed it over. The man opened his cash box, made change. He held the coins out, and the woman took them without counting. She put the jars in her tote bag and left.

Dave wouldn't have noticed. He was looking at the squash in his bag, double-checking that delicata wasn't just a decorative gourd after all. Aldric was watching. He stood still with his eggs under one arm, his head tilted slightly to the left, watching the transaction like a bird watching a seed.

Then he walked to the stall.

"Good morning," Aldric said. "Your preserves look excellent. May I ask the price?"

The man looked up. Sized him up. The linen jacket, the posture, the accent that wasn't quite anything.

"What do you want?" he said.

"One jar of the strawberry, please."

"Six dollars."

"Thank you." Aldric picked up a jar. Turned it in his hand. Read the label. Set it on the table. "And the blackberry?"

"Same."

Aldric nodded. He pulled a ten-dollar bill from his wallet and placed it flat on the table. "One strawberry, please. Six dollars. That should be four in change."

The man took the ten. Opened the cash box. Handed back three ones and three quarters.

"I believe there's a discrepancy," Aldric said. His tone hadn't changed. Same warmth, same politeness. "I gave you ten. The jar is six. The correct change would be four dollars."

The man looked at the money in Aldric's palm.

"Three dollars and seventy-five cents," Aldric said. "The jar is six dollars. That totals nine seventy-five. I gave you ten."

"Tax."

"The sign says prices include tax." Aldric pointed to the small laminated card taped to the front of the table. PRICES INCLUDE TAX. "Which would make the correct change four dollars even."

His jaw shifted. Something behind his eyes calculated. Not math. Cost. The cost of arguing with a man who stood perfectly straight and spoke with the calm of someone who had all day.

"Must've miscounted," the man said.

Aldric held out his hand with the three-seventy-five, palm open. Waiting.

The man reached into the box. He put a quarter on the counter.

"Thank you." Aldric picked it up. He looked at the jars, the stall, the hand-lettered sign. "You have a fine operation here. Good product, loyal customers. I see the same faces every week." He smiled. "I'm here most Saturdays myself. I enjoy the routine. And I tend to notice things — patterns, numbers. It's a habit I've never been able to break." He said it like an apology. Like he was confessing a minor flaw. "I'm sure today was just an off morning."

The man's hands had gone still on his cash box.

"These things happen. I believe you were simply distracted. Perhaps the child didn't sleep well last night?" Aldric said.

"Yeah. Something like that."

Aldric placed the three-seventy-five on the table, plus the quarter, plus another two bills from his wallet and smiled. "Actually, I'd like two jars. The strawberry and the blackberry. Twelve dollars exactly."

He gathered his jars, nodded at the vendor, and walked back to where Dave was standing.

Dave had watched the whole thing. He was still holding his squash bag.

"That was twenty-five cents," Dave said.

"It was."

"You just— for a quarter, you didn't even keep?"

Aldric looked at him. The morning light caught the lines around his eyes. He looked the same as he always did.

"The woman before me paid eighteen dollars and fifty-three cents for three jars at six each. Three times six is eighteen. He charged her fifty-three cents over. She didn't notice."

Dave looked at him. "You were watching her transaction?"

"It's not difficult to notice, Dave. The issue isn't the amount. The issue is that he's doing it deliberately. Small amounts. Amounts people don't notice or don't contest because it's not worth the discomfort." He tucked the jars into his bag. "He's been doing it all morning. I watched three transactions before you arrived." He smiled. "Would you like coffee? There's a truck near the entrance."

They got coffee. Dave paid because Aldric had paid last time, which was something Dave kept track of. Aldric probably didn't, but it always ended up at exactly even. The coffee was too hot and the cups were too thin. They walked back toward the trucks.

"What if he'd said no?" Dave asked. He didn't know why the question mattered.

"To the quarter?"

"Yeah. What if he just said tough luck."

Aldric drank his coffee. He considered the question with full attention, as if Dave had asked something profound instead of hypothetical.

"Then I would have informed the market organizer that one of their vendors is systematically overcharging customers by small, irregular amounts designed to avoid detection." He said it without heat. "Mr. Kowalski's stall would lose its permit. He knows that. I know that. The quarter is cheaper."

Dave looked at him. "You know his name?"

"There's a sticker on his cash box. R. Kowalski."

"Right. And you just— read that. From across the aisle."

"I'm occasionally wrong about things, Dave. But not often about names on stickers."

Dave laughed. He couldn't help it. The coffee was cooling in his hand. The morning was sharp and bright. A kid was running between the stalls, chasing a dog that had gotten loose from somewhere, and a woman was yelling the dog's name in a voice that suggested this happened weekly.

"How'd you know about the kid?" Dave said. "His kid."

"I didn't."

Dave looked at him. "You said—"

"I offered a plausible reason. Most men his age have children. If I'd been wrong and he would have corrected me, then I would have offered another." Aldric took a sip of his coffee. "The point isn't whether it's true. The point is that he needed a reason that wasn't the real one."

"You carry that around all the time?" Dave said.

"Carry what?"

"That. The— noticing. The math. The keeping track."

Aldric took a sip. "I notice what I notice. I don't find it burdensome."

Dave nodded. He didn't know what else to say. If it wasn't burdensome for Aldric, then it wasn't burdensome. Dave believed that. But there was something, he couldn't quite point his fingers at. A man who noticed cents on a stranger's transaction, who waited, who then corrected it with perfect manners and walked away like it was nothing. That man was paying attention in a way that Dave couldn't quite name. It was impressive. It was also, in some corner of his brain he didn't want to examine, a little unsettling.

They finished their coffee by Dave's truck. Aldric said goodbye, thanked Dave for the company, and walked to his Volvo two rows over. The morning continued. The market continued. Somewhere, Kowalski was making change, and Dave was fairly sure it was correct now.

He drove home. Rosa was in the kitchen with Marcus on her hip, Lina at the table eating cereal with a book propped against the milk carton.

"Did you get everything?" Rosa said.

"Everything. Including the decorative squash."

"Delicata."

"Right." He set the bags on the counter. "Ran into Aldric."

"Oh yeah?"

"He goes to the market too. Most Saturdays."

"That's nice." Rosa put Marcus down. He immediately went for the bags. She intercepted him with one hand, still looking at Dave. "How was it?"

Dave thought about the quarter. The polite voice. The plausible reason. Aldric saying *the issue isn't the amount* like it was obvious, like everyone should care about twenty-five cents.

"He's a good guy," Dave said. And he meant it in a way he couldn't fully explain.

Rosa smiled. She was unpacking the tomatoes, turning each one in her hand. "We should have him over for dinner."

"Yeah?"

"Just us. Not a neighborhood thing. An actual dinner. He's been here since June. He's cooked for everyone, helped everyone, and he's never sat at anyone's table."

Dave leaned against the counter. She was right. Aldric had been there for three months. He'd grilled for the whole street at the housewarming, helped Rosa with her flowers in the garden, stayed to clean up after Dave's birthday, and caught the dog when it got loose. Nobody had invited him inside for a meal. Not once that Dave knew of.

"Okay," Dave said. "Let's do it."

Rosa picked up the basil and smelled it. "I'll ask him tomorrow."

Marcus had gotten into the squash bag. He was holding a delicata in both hands, looking at it with suspicion. Dave took it from him gently.

"It's food," Dave said. "Apparently."

Marcus didn't look convinced and Dave couldn't blame him.
