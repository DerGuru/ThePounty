# Prologue

The last forty miles had been the best. Flat land opening up. Houses growing smaller, farther apart, then clustering again into neighborhoods with names. Oak Ridge. Birchwood Estates. Maple Lane was between those two, which amused him. An entire taxonomy of trees within a three-mile radius. Someone had lacked imagination. Or someone had loved trees very much and named accordingly. He preferred the second interpretation. He always preferred the generous interpretation. That was a rule.

Eleven hundred miles had taken fourteen hours. He could have done it in twelve, but speed limits existed for a reason, and the reason was that other people were on the road and deserved to arrive safely. He had not exceeded the posted limit once. He considered that a minor accomplishment. Not because it was difficult — the Volvo didn’t encourage speed — but because patience, exercised consistently, was a form of respect for the system one operated within.

He had eaten nothing since a gas station outside of Erie. A granola bar, unremarkable, consumed standing beside the car while the pump ran. He had cleaned his hands afterward. Twice. Once with the napkin from the wrapper. Once with the small bottle of hand sanitizer he kept in the glove compartment. Not because of germs. Because the texture of processed sugar on fingertips was distracting, and distraction behind the wheel was discourteous to everyone sharing the road.

Aldric Venn turned the Volvo onto Maple Lane at fourteen minutes past two on a Tuesday afternoon in late June. The first thing he noticed was that someone had planted marigolds in a tire rim. Bright orange, almost aggressive against the white fence behind them. Deliberate. He approved. He wanted to know who. He wouldn’t ask today.

The street had symmetry. White fences, all the same height. Driveways, all the same width. Mailboxes evenly spaced, upright, freshly painted. The lawns were green in the specific way that required commitment. Someone on this street had standards. Several someones, probably, enforcing them without a committee. He respected that. A neighborhood that maintained itself through mutual expectation rather than rules on paper was, in his experience, the strongest kind.

He wondered who enforced it, and whether they did it on purpose or had simply been doing it so long now, that it felt like weather.

The trailer behind him was rented, standard size and conspicuously underfilled. It rattled once over a speed bump and then settled. He’d driven the distance without stopping for anything longer than fuel and a bathroom. The Volvo hadn’t complained. The Volvo never complained. Forty years old and still running like a promise. He appreciated that about the car. Consistency deserved acknowledgment, even from machines.

Number four. That was his.

He pulled to the curb in a single motion and turned off the engine. The silence that followed was full of sprinklers, distant lawnmowers and a dog barking somewhere with the steady rhythm of an animal that barked at everything and had long stopped expecting results. He liked the dog already. He hoped it was large and poorly trained and belonged to someone who’d tell him its whole life story without being asked. He would introduce himself to the dog first. That was protocol. You always greeted the animal before the owner. It showed respect for the household.

He sat for a moment. Hands on the wheel. The leather warm from the drive.

The house next door had a flag. Something with a bird on it. A personal choice, displayed publicly. The one beyond that, a wind chime made of spoons. Someone had made it and hung it where the whole street could hear it. That took confidence. He respected that enormously.

His house was modest, beige, with a porch that wrapped halfway around the front. The previous tenants had left a rocking chair. That was a kind thing to do, leaving something behind for a stranger. He would find out who they were. He would write them a note. A proper one, on paper, with his name and a mention of the rocking chair so they’d know it wasn’t a generic pleasantry. People deserved to know when they’d done something kind. Generic gratitude was worse than none at all.

The flower box was empty, soil still dark and ready. The windows were clean. Someone had prepared this for whoever came next. He would thank that person too, once he identified them. There was always someone responsible. Finding them was a courtesy.

He was whoever came next and he intended to be adequate.

He had given his word. That was why he was here. A word, once given, was permanent. Another Rule.

But he hadn't expected to like it.

He looked at the street again. Fences the same height. Hedges trimmed to a line you could set a level against. The quiet agreement between houses that this was how things were done here.

*Community through uniformity,* he thought. *Identity through exception.*

He would buy a flower box. Something with color, not just green. He had opinions about flower boxes now. That was recent. He looked forward to implementing them.

The rearview mirror showed the trailer. Three boxes. A chair. A toolbox. A flat metal case he’d packed last and would unpack first. Plenty of room left over. He traveled light. He’d always traveled light.

He opened the door. The air smelled like cut grass, warm asphalt, and something baking. Someone on this street was making something in an oven at two in the afternoon on a Tuesday. That suggested a household with time, or one that made time. Either was admirable. He wanted to know what it was, and who it was for, and whether they’d made it from scratch or from a box. If they offered him some, he would accept. Declining food was rude. If they didn’t offer, he would compliment the smell the next time they met. People should know when their home smelled wonderful. He would not knock to ask. But not today. Today was for arriving.

He stepped out. Stretched once, the way a man stretches after eleven hundred miles. 

Across the street, a curtain moved.

Next door, a screen door opened.

He did not look at the curtain. He did not wave at the screen door. They would come to him, or they wouldn’t. He hoped they would. He had already decided to learn their names quickly, all of them, and to use them every time. That mattered. Names mattered. A name used correctly was the smallest possible gift you could give someone, and it cost nothing, and most people forgot to give it.

He had read the mailboxes on the way in. Ortega. Hooper. Chen. Green. Yuen. Alvarez. Six names, already filed. He would match them to faces today. That was the first task.

But not yet. First, the house.

He unlocked the front door and stepped inside. The listing had said furnished. The listing had been accurate. He approved of people who wrote accurate listings. It suggested a respect for the incoming stranger’s need to plan.

Kitchen first, because kitchens told you about a house’s character. This one was modest and well-maintained. Laminate countertops made to look like granite. He did not judge that. He cataloged it. Three plates, stacked neatly in the first cabinet. Glasses in the second. A box of Earl Grey on the shelf above the sink, left by the previous tenants or the landlord. Another kindness for the note.

The back door had a deadbolt and a chain. The chain was decorative at best — three screws into soft pine, maybe fifteen pounds of force to failure. He would replace it tomorrow. Today was for settling. Tomorrow was for improvements.

Living room. Front window, south-facing, good light. The chair would go there. Angled toward the street but not directly facing it. Visible from outside but not ostentatious. A man in a chair by a window, reading. That was the image. That was the correct image.

The mantelpiece above the fireplace was bare, dusted, centered. The frame would go there. He could see it already.

Upstairs. Two bedrooms, one bathroom. The master bedroom window faced the backyard. The bed was against the far wall. He moved it. Left side, flush against the exterior wall, headboard touching plaster. He straightened the sheets. Better. 

He checked the closet. Top shelf, deep enough. Behind the spare blanket that smelled faintly of lavender. That was where the case would go, once he brought it in.

He stood at the upstairs window for a moment. The backyard was small, fenced, with a single tree he couldn’t identify from this distance. He would identify it tomorrow. Knowing what grew in your yard was a basic responsibility of residence.

From this window he could see the back yard of number two and number six. Fences low enough to wave over. Gardens in various states of commitment. One immaculate, one waiting. Number six had a sign in the front he’d noticed on the way in. The garden didn’t know yet. It kept growing anyway. He respected that.

He came back downstairs. Through the kitchen again, out the front door. The porch. The rocking chair. He sat in it briefly. It creaked once, then settled under him. Good craftsmanship. Solid joints. The previous tenants had taste in chairs, at least.

He looked at the street from the rocking chair. This would be a good vantage point. Mornings with tea. Evenings with a book. At the far end, where the street opened, was a small playground. Two swings, a slide, a sandbox, a seesaw, a climbing frame. The street ended with an invitation. Or began with one, depending on which way you drove in. The kind of life that looked simple from the outside because the architecture was invisible.

He stood. Enough. Time to unload.

He walked back to the trailer. Unlatched the back. The metal case was closest to the door, positioned for first retrieval. He lifted it — light, despite its contents — and carried it inside, upstairs, into the closet, behind the blanket. He closed the closet door. He did not look back at it.

When he came out of the house again, a man was crossing the lawn next door. Flip-flops. Coffee mug still in one hand. The kind of walk that said *I’m being casual about this but I’ve been watching for ten minutes.* Ortega. From the mailbox. Mid-thirties, maybe. Working hands. Open face.

Good.

From across the street, another one approached. Slower. Hands in pockets. Reading the scene before entering it. Hooper. Sharper posture, watchful eyes. The kind of man who noticed things and took notes. Aldric could tell. It was in the shoulders — the slight forward lean of someone cataloging, not just looking.

Two already within the first fifteen minutes. That was better than he’d hoped.

He closed the trailer latch. He turned to face the man in the flip-flops. He let himself smile — not the efficient one, not the professional one. The real one. The one he had built from scratch over twenty years until it used the whole face, started at the eyes, and meant exactly what it appeared to mean.

He waited for the name he already knew, because hearing it offered freely was better than having it in advance. That was a rule too.

Aldric Venn was here. He was going to be good at being here.
