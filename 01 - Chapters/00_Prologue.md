# Prologue

Aldric Venn turned the Volvo onto Maple Lane at fourteen minutes past two on a Tuesday afternoon. The first thing he noticed was the symmetry. White fences, all the same height. Driveways, all the same width. Mailboxes standing at attention like soldiers who'd been told to relax but didn't quite believe it. The lawns were green in the specific way that required commitment. Not nature, but schedule. Someone mowed on Saturdays. Someone else mowed on Sundays to make a point.

He liked that.

The trailer behind him, rented, standard size, conspicuously underfilled, rattled once over a speed bump and then settled. The engine hummed the way it always had: patient, reliable, unbothered by distance. He'd driven eleven hundred miles in this car without stopping for anything longer than fuel and a bathroom. The Volvo hadn't complained. The Volvo never complained.

Number four. That was his.

He pulled to the curb in a single motion, no adjustment, no second try, and turned off the engine. The silence was immediate and specific. Not empty. Full. Full of sprinklers and distant lawnmowers and a dog barking somewhere to the left with the steady rhythm of an animal that barked at everything and had long stopped expecting results.

Aldric sat for a moment. Hands on the wheel. The leather was warm from the drive.

The house was modest, white, with a porch that wrapped halfway around the front. The previous tenants had left a rocking chair. The flower box was empty. Soil still dark, ready. The windows were clean. Someone had prepared this. He appreciated that.

He looked at the street again. At the evenness of it. The quiet agreement between houses that this was how things were done. Fences the same height. Hedges trimmed to a line you could set a level against.

*Community through uniformity,* he thought. *Identity through exception.*

He would buy a flower box. Something appropriate. He wanted to be a good neighbor.

The rearview mirror showed the trailer, its interior barely visible through the small window. Three boxes. A chair. A toolbox. A flat metal case he'd packed last and would unpack first. The empty space, the gaps where a life's accumulation should have been, he'd long stopped noticing.

This was enough. This was everything he needed.

He opened the door. The air smelled like cut grass and warm asphalt and, faintly, something baking. Someone, somewhere on this street, was making something in an oven at two in the afternoon on a Tuesday. He found that remarkable.

He stepped out, stretched once, efficiently, without theater, and walked to the back of the trailer to begin.

Across the street, a curtain moved.

Two houses down, a screen door opened.

Aldric smiled. Not because he'd noticed. Because the sun was warm, the street was quiet, and he was here.

He was here.
