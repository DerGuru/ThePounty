# Prologue

Aldric Venn turned the Volvo onto Maple Lane at fourteen minutes past two on a Tuesday afternoon, and the first thing he noticed was that someone had planted marigolds in a tire rim three houses down. Bright orange, almost aggressive against the white fence behind them. He wanted to know who. He wouldn't ask today.

The street had symmetry. White fences, all the same height. Driveways, all the same width. Mailboxes standing at attention like soldiers who'd been told to relax but didn't quite believe it. The lawns were green in the specific way that required commitment. Not nature. Schedule. Someone mowed on Saturdays. Someone else mowed on Sundays to make a point.

He liked that. He wondered who enforced it, and whether they knew they were doing it, or whether it had become invisible to them the way rules always became invisible to the people who followed them best.

The trailer behind him, rented, standard size, conspicuously underfilled, rattled once over a speed bump and then settled. He'd driven eleven hundred miles without stopping for anything longer than fuel and a bathroom. The Volvo hadn't complained. The Volvo never complained. It was the most reliable relationship he'd ever maintained, and he was aware of what that said about him.

Number four. That was his.

He pulled to the curb in a single motion, and turned off the engine. The silence that followed was not empty. It was full of sprinklers and distant lawnmowers and a dog barking somewhere to the left with the steady rhythm of an animal that barked at everything and had long stopped expecting results. He liked the dog already. He hoped it was large and poorly trained and belonged to someone who'd tell him its whole life story without being asked.

He sat for a moment. Hands on the wheel. The leather was warm from the drive.

The house next door had a flag. Not a country flag. Something with a bird on it. The one beyond that, a wind chime made of spoons. He cataloged them the way he cataloged everything, automatically, precisely, and then made himself stop. Not everything needed to be assessed. Some things could just be looked at.

His house was modest, white, with a porch that wrapped halfway around the front. The previous tenants had left a rocking chair. That was a kind thing to do, leaving something behind for a stranger. The flower box was empty, soil still dark and ready. The windows were clean. Someone had prepared this for whoever came next.

He was whoever came next.

He looked at the street again. At the evenness of it. The quiet agreement between houses that this was how things were done here. Fences the same height. Hedges trimmed to a line you could set a level against. He thought: *Community through uniformity. Identity through exception.*

He would buy a flower box. Something with color, not just green. He wanted to be a good neighbor. He wanted, if he was being honest with himself, to be the kind of person who bought flower boxes. That was a newer want. He was still learning the shape of it.

The rearview mirror showed the trailer. Three boxes. A chair. A toolbox. A flat metal case he'd packed last and would unpack first. The gaps where a life's accumulation should have been didn't bother him anymore. They used to. That was progress, probably. Or practice. He'd stopped trying to tell the difference.

He opened the door. The air hit him like a greeting. Cut grass, warm asphalt, and something baking. Someone on this street was making something in an oven at two in the afternoon on a Tuesday. He found that remarkable. He wanted to know what it was, and who it was for, and whether they'd made it from scratch or from a box. He would not knock to ask. Not today. Today was for arriving. There were rules about today.

He stepped out. Stretched once, the way a man stretches after eleven hundred miles. Then he walked to the back of the trailer to begin.

Across the street, a curtain moved.

Two houses down, a screen door opened.

He did not look at the curtain. He did not wave at the screen door. They would come to him, or they wouldn't. He found that he hoped they would. That was newer too.

Aldric Venn opened the trailer, and the street watched, and the afternoon continued as Tuesday afternoons do. Ordinary. Unremarkable. Full of small things about to shift.

He was here. He was going to be good at being here.
