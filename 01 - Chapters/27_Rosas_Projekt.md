# Chapter 27: Rosa's Project

*Tuesday, the 10th of December 2024*

The community center smelled like floor wax and somebody's microwaved soup. Rosa stood in the entrance hall with her tote bag over one shoulder, the one with the broken zipper she kept meaning to replace, and read the bulletin board without seeing any of it. A flyer for a blood drive. A lost cat named Dusty. A sign-up sheet for a spring garden swap that still had November's date crossed out on top.

She was fifteen minutes early. But this mattered to her.

The Greenfield Community Center was a single-story building three blocks south of Maple Lane, brick on the outside, linoleum on the inside. It had been a library annex in the eighties. Now it housed a food pantry on Mondays, a seniors' exercise class on Wednesdays, and on Tuesday and Thursday afternoons, the homework help program for kids whose parents worked past three-thirty.

Rosa had called last week. The coordinator, a woman named Diane Osei, had said yes before Rosa finished her sentence. "We're down two volunteers since September. Can you start Tuesday?"

She could start Tuesday.

The hallway opened into a large room with folding tables arranged in a U-shape. Plastic chairs in three colors. A whiteboard on wheels with Tuesday's schedule written in blue marker: 3:00 arrivals, 3:15 homework time, 4:00 snack, 4:15 reading circle, 4:45 pickup. Clean and organized. Simple.

Rosa set her bag on the nearest table and unpacked. Extra pencils, a pack of erasers, and two boxes of animal crackers she'd bought at the store yesterday. Her planner, open to this week. She'd written "CC - first day" in the Tuesday square, small letters, blue ink.

"You must be Rosa."

Diane came through the side door carrying a stack of folders. She was tall, mid-fifties, natural hair cut close to her scalp. She wore reading glasses on a chain and moved like someone who'd been running this program long enough that her body knew the room without looking.

"That's me." Rosa shook her hand. "Thank you for having me."

"Thank me in a month when you've met all twelve of them at once." Diane set the folders down and smiled. "We'll start with six today. The Tuesday group is the smaller one. Mostly third and fourth graders. One fifth grader who pretends she doesn't need help."

"Sounds like my daughter."

"How old?"

"Ten. Going on thirty."

Diane laughed. It was a good laugh. Genuine. "You'll be fine. Come on, I'll show you the supply room."

The supply room was barely a room. Shelves from floor to ceiling, labeled bins, construction paper in every color. Rosa noticed the organization immediately. Pencils sorted by hardness. Glue sticks in a separate container from bottled glue. A bin of scissors with orange handles. Everything had a place.

She followed Diane back to the main room and took a seat near the whiteboard.

The kids arrived in twos and threes after three o'clock. Backpacks dragging on the floor, coats half-zipped, the specific energy of elementary schoolers released from six hours of sitting still. Rosa watched them find their seats. Two boys argued over the blue chair. A girl with braids took the corner spot and opened her math workbook without being asked.

Rosa sat beside a boy named DeShawn who was stuck on fractions. She showed him how she'd shown Lina two years ago. Pieces of paper torn into halves, then quarters, then eighths. He got it. His face when it clicked was worth every minute of the fifteen she'd been early.

The fifth grader sat at the far end of the table. Keisha. She had a spiral notebook open and a mechanical pencil moving in small, steady strokes. Drawing. She didn't look up when Rosa introduced herself to the group. She didn't look up when DeShawn cheered about the fractions. She didn't look up at all.

Rosa tried once. "Keisha, do you need help with anything?"

"No." Eyes on the notebook. Pencil still moving.

Rosa let it go. She'd try again next time.

At three-forty, the door opened. A woman came in with a girl of about seven behind her. The girl had a purple backpack and went straight to the reading corner. She knew where things were. A regular.

The woman stayed in the doorway. She was maybe thirty, dark hair pulled back tight, wearing a gray sweater that was a size too large. Her hands were in her pockets. She scanned the room like Rosa scanned a kitchen: checking what was wrong before registering what was right.

"Hi," Rosa said from the table. "I'm Rosa. I'm new here."

The woman looked at her. A pause, barely there, like she was deciding whether to commit to the conversation. Then she stepped forward. "Nora. That's my daughter, Lily."

"She seems to know the place."

"She's been coming since October. It's good for her." Nora's eyes moved to the reading corner where Lily was already sitting cross-legged on a beanbag. "She likes the routine."

"Routine's good," Rosa said.

Nora nodded. Her sleeves were pulled down past her wrists, her bunched fists inside the pockets. It was cold outside, mid-thirties, but the center was warm. Rosa noticed. 

"Do you live nearby?" Rosa asked.

"Three streets over. Birch." Nora took her hands out of her pockets, crossed her arms instead. "I should go. I'll be back at quarter to five."

"She'll be fine."

"I know." Nora smiled. It was a quick smile, practiced, the kind that answered a question nobody asked. She left through the front door without looking back.

Rosa watched her cross the parking lot through the window. Quick steps. Shoulders pulled in. Not from the cold.

She turned back to DeShawn, who had moved on to word problems.

The hour passed. She helped three kids with math, read a chapter of Charlotte's Web to the younger group while Diane handled pickup logistics, and cut sixteen squares of construction paper for a girl named Amara who was building something she called "a house for my hamster but bigger." Through all of it, Keisha drew. Never looked up. Never asked for help. Never joined in.

Nora came back at four-forty-five. She stood in the doorway again, same posture, same scan. Lily ran to her with a construction paper house in both hands. Nora bent down to look at it. For a moment, her face was unguarded. Open. Soft in a way that made Rosa understand something about the difference between who this woman was when no one was watching and who she was when she walked through a door.

Then it closed again. Quick and practiced.

"Say thank you to the volunteers, Lily."

"Thank you!" Lily waved at Rosa with the hand that wasn't holding the house.

Rosa waved back. Nora nodded at her, the smallest acknowledgment, and they left.

Rosa packed up her bag. She said goodbye, said she'd be back Thursday, and said it was really good, all of it. Diane said she was glad. She meant it. Rosa could tell.

Outside, the cold hit her face and she pulled her coat tighter. December dark was coming early, the sky already turning that deep blue-gray that meant winter had settled in properly.

She drove home on streets she knew with her eyes closed.

When she pulled into the driveway, Dave's truck was there. The kitchen light was on. Through the window she could see him at the counter, probably starting dinner because she'd told him she'd be late today.

She went inside. Dave had pasta water going and was chopping garlic with the wrong knife. She kissed his shoulder and took the right knife from the block.

"I got it," she said. "Go take out the trash before we forget."

"How was it?"

"Good." She stirred the sauce he'd started. "Really good. There's this boy, DeShawn. Fractions. Took him some time, but then his whole face lit up."

"Nice."

"And a girl, Amara, who's building a hamster mansion out of construction paper. She's committed."

Dave grinned. "Sounds like Marcus with the Lego."

"Exactly like Marcus with the Lego." He grabbed the trash bag and headed for the door, opening it. Rosa called after him: "There's one kid I'm not sure about, though. Fifth grader. She just draws in her notebook the whole time. Won't look up."

"Maybe she doesn't want to be there." He turned back to look at her.

"Maybe." Rosa set the spoon down. "I don't know. It bugged me."

A knock on the door frame. Aldric stood behind Dave, one hand raised, the other holding his mug. The cardigan. The evening cold behind him.

"I saw the light," he said. "I wanted to ask — how was the first day?"

"Come in," Dave said. "We are about to eat, there's pasta."

"Thank you, but I've eaten." He stayed in the doorway. "Rosa?"

"It was good." She leaned against the counter. "Really good. There's a boy who finally got fractions today. And a girl building a hamster mansion."

Aldric smiled. "That sounds like a productive afternoon." He nodded. He didn't say anything else about it. He asked Dave about the truck's oil light, said good night, and walked back across the street.

Rosa watched him go. The porch light came on at his house. Normal evening. Normal neighbor.

Wednesday morning. Rosa was in the front yard pulling dead mums from the border bed when Aldric came down his driveway to check the mail. He found it empty, turned back, and stopped at the edge of his lawn.

"Good morning, Rosa."

"Morning."

"May I ask you a question?" Aldric asked, his voice calm and even, like he was asking for directions to the nearest grocery store.

"Of course."

"The girl who draws — is she drawing the same thing over and over, or different things each time?"

Rosa looked at him. She hadn't told him about Keisha. She'd told Dave. Last night, at the counter, half a sentence before Aldric knocked. He'd been standing in the doorway when she said it. She hadn't even been talking to him.

"Different things," Rosa said. "I think."

Aldric nodded. "Then she's listening. Some people can't hold still and hold attention at the same time. The drawing is how she holds attention."

He said it simply. Like it was obvious. Like anyone would know that.

"Give her a question without asking her to look up," he said. "She'll answer."

He went back inside.

Rosa knelt in the flower bed for a long time after that. The dead mums came out one by one. She put them in the paper bag and tied it shut and set it by the curb. She went inside, washed her hands and stood at the kitchen window looking at nothing.

One sentence. She'd said one sentence about a girl who draws, and from that he'd built the whole picture. Not what the girl was doing. What the girl *needed*. The mechanism behind the behavior. The solution.

Rosa's own instinct had been to try harder, to engage, to make contact. Obvious, but wrong. What a normal person would think.

Aldric had asked one question — *same thing or different things* — and from the answer, he knew.

Thursday. Rosa came back to the center. DeShawn was already at his spot. Amara had brought more construction paper from home. Keisha was in the corner, notebook open, pencil moving.

Rosa sat down two chairs away. She didn't look at Keisha directly. She helped a third grader with spelling for ten minutes. Then, still facing the other boy's worksheet, she said: "Keisha, what's twelve times eight?"

She didn't look up. She didn't ask Keisha to look up.

"Ninety-six," Keisha said. The pencil didn't stop.

"What about seven times nine?"

"Sixty-three."

Rosa moved to the next question. Then the next. Keisha answered every one. Quick and correct and without lifting her eyes from whatever she was drawing. After the fourth question, the corner of her mouth moved. Not quite a smile. But close.

Rosa kept going. By four o'clock, Keisha had answered eleven questions, corrected DeShawn once from across the table without looking up, and turned to a fresh page in her notebook.

She was listening. She'd always been listening.

Rosa drove home with the heater pushing warm air and the radio off. She pulled into the driveway. Dave's truck. Kitchen light. Everything the same as Tuesday.

Across the street, Aldric's study light was on upstairs.

She sat in the car for a moment. Hands on the wheel.

*How does he know?*

She'd told Dave last month that Aldric was good at knowing what people needed. Dave had said yeah, it's nice. And it was nice. It was nice, and it was something else too. Something she couldn't name without making it sound like a complaint when it wasn't one.

He read people. He read them like she read her children. Like she read a kitchen. Like she knew which neighbor hadn't waved back this week. But she did it because she loved them. She did it because caring was the same thing as paying attention.

She didn't know why he did it. She just knew he was better at it than anyone she'd ever met. That whatever she'd said — one sentence, standing in her kitchen, not even thinking about it — he'd taken the fragment home and built the whole shape from it overnight. The girl who draws. The mechanism. The solution. All from *she won't look up.*

Rosa went inside. Dave asked how it went. She said good. She said really good. She told him about Keisha answering questions without lifting her pencil, about DeShawn correcting his own worksheet this time, about Amara's hamster mansion gaining a second floor.

She didn't tell him about Wednesday morning. About the question Aldric asked. About how a man with no children, who'd never taught anyone anything, had known what she hadn't seen.

She thought about Nora, too. About the sleeves. About how she'd stood in the doorway like someone measuring the distance to an exit. She'd see her again on Tuesday. She'd keep noticing.

Some things needed more time before they were ready to be said out loud.
