# Chapter 28: The Snowstorm

The forecast said four inches. Dave checked it Monday morning on his phone while the coffee brewed, scrolled past the winter storm warning, and thought: fine. Four inches meant shoveling once, maybe twice. Marcus would want to build something. Lina would refuse to go outside. It was December and winter had finally arrived.

By noon, the sky was a flat gray lid pressed down over the rooftops. The first flakes came at one-thirty. By three, when Rosa picked up the kids from school, the driveway was already covered. Dave stood at the kitchen window and watched the plows go past on the main road. They wouldn't come down Maple Lane until late evening. They never did.

He texted Rosa: *how bad is it out there?*

*Bad. Took twenty minutes to get to the school.*

She came in at three-twenty with both kids, snow on her coat and her boots leaving puddles on the mat. Marcus ran to the window. "Dad, can we go outside?"

"After you eat something."

"Can we build a fort?"

"After you eat something."

Lina dropped her backpack by the stairs and disappeared into her room without taking off her coat. Ten, going on thirty.

Marcus ate his apple slices standing at the counter, bouncing on his toes, eyes on the window the whole time. He left two slices on the plate. "Done."

"All of them."

He ate the last two in a hurry, looking like they offended him personally. Then he was at the door, pulling on boots, hat crooked, zipper only halfway up. Dave fixed the zipper. Marcus was already reaching for the handle.

"Stay in the front yard. Where I can see you."

The door opened and Marcus was gone, swallowed by the white. Dave watched from the window. The kid threw himself backward into the snow on the front lawn and made an angel that looked more like a starfish. Then he stood, assessed the depth, and started packing snow with both hands. The fort. Of course. Dave gave it ten minutes before he'd want help.

It lasted eight.

"Dad! I need you!"

Dave put on his coat and grabbed the shovel from the garage on his way out. The snow was heavier now, coming at an angle. He cleared the front walk while Marcus explained the fort's architecture from three feet away, hands waving, snow on his hat. Then Dave helped him stack a wall that came up to the kid's waist. It wasn't straight. It didn't matter. Marcus patted it like it was alive.

Dave went back to the driveway. Four inches since Rosa had come home. He pushed it to the side in long strokes, the scrape of the blade loud in the muffled street. Marcus supervised from behind his wall. "You're putting it in the wrong place."

"Where should it go?"

"Over here. For the fort."

Dave changed direction and shoveled the driveway snow onto Marcus's construction site. The kid packed it into the second wall with both hands, delighted by the supply chain.

They stayed out until Marcus's cheeks were red and his gloves were soaked through. The fort had one wall, half a second wall, and a pile that was supposed to be a tower. The driveway was clear, for now. Marcus declared the fort finished. For now.

Inside, Dave peeled off Marcus's wet layers and draped them over the radiator. Marcus stood in his socks on the kitchen tile, shivering and grinning. "Can we go back out after dinner?"

"We'll see, buddy."

They wouldn't. By dinner the snow would be something else entirely.

By five o'clock, it was dark and the snow was still coming. Dave could hear the wind now, pressing against the house in gusts that rattled the screen door. He went to the front window. The streetlights were on, dim halos of white in the falling snow. He couldn't see the Hoopers' house across the street. The snow was that dense.

By six the wind had dropped and the snow had thinned with it, fat flakes drifting down through the streetlight cones. The Hoopers' porch was visible again. Lights on in most of the windows down the block.

And then it happened. The power went out at six-fourteen.

Dave was reheating chili when the microwave stopped mid-cycle, the kitchen lights cut to black, and the hum of the house went silent. The fridge, the radio, the low drone of things running that you never noticed until they stopped. All of it gone.

"Dave?" Rosa called from upstairs.

"Yeah. Power's out."

Marcus appeared in the kitchen doorway. "Everything's dark."

"I know, buddy. It's okay. Go find the flashlight in the junk drawer."

Dave pulled out his phone. One bar where there should have been four. The tower must have taken a hit too. But one bar was still one bar. He pulled up the outage map. It loaded slow, but it loaded. A red blob covering their side of town. Estimated restoration: unknown. He checked the temperature. Twenty-two degrees outside. The house would hold heat for a while. The insulation was decent. But the furnace ran on gas with an electric igniter. No power, no heat.

He found the candles Rosa kept in the dining room hutch and lit three. The kitchen looked warm in the candlelight, which was a lie, because in two hours it would be fifty degrees in here.

Rosa came downstairs with the kids' extra blankets. She was already thinking three steps ahead. She always was. "How long?"

"Don't know. Map says unknown."

"Wonderful."

A knock on the front door.

Dave opened it to a wall of cold air and Aldric Venn standing on the porch in a dark jacket and a wool hat, holding a camping lantern in one hand and a propane heater in the other. Snow on his shoulders. His boots were dry.

"Come in. Quick. We're losing heat." Dave stepped back from the door.

Aldric stepped in. Dave shut the door behind him and felt the cold pocket settle in the hallway. Every second that door had been open was a degree they wouldn't get back tonight.

"Good evening, Dave. I'm sorry to disturb you. I have a generator running and more capacity than I need. I also have two propane heaters and enough fuel for twelve hours. Would you like one?"

Dave looked at the propane heater. It was a good one. Not the cheap kind from the hardware store. The kind that ran clean, ran quiet, and didn't smell.

"You said two heaters. Who else needs one? Maria's alone over there. Ms. Yuen."

"Ms. Yuen gets the generator line. Maria has a fireplace and dry wood. I checked yesterday afternoon when the forecast updated. She'll be warmer than any of us."

Dave processed that. *Checked yesterday afternoon when the forecast updated.* Filed it away with the rest.

"Yeah," Dave said. "Thanks, man."

"I'll set this up for you and then I need to check on Ms. Yuen." Aldric set the heater in the living room with a clearance of three feet from every surface, connected the propane canister, and lit it. The flame caught blue and steady. He adjusted the output without looking at the dial, like he'd done it a thousand times.

"You have one of these just lying around?" Dave said.

"I keep a few supplies for situations like this." Aldric straightened up. "The generator is powering my house and can extend to one more connection. Ms. Yuen is on supplemental oxygen at night. I'd like to run a line to her house first. Would that be all right with you? You have the heater."

"Of course. Yeah. Go."

Aldric nodded. "I'll come back shortly. The forecast holds twelve to fourteen inches. The furnace won't restart without power, and the heater here will need watching all night. The Hendersons have a fireplace, too. If they or Maria have room, you and the children might be warmer there. With your permission, I'll ask."

Dave looked at Rosa. Rosa looked back. She'd already nodded before he could.

"Yeah. Sure."

"Thank you. I'll return within the hour."

He was out the door before Dave could say anything else. Through the window, Dave watched him cross the street toward number nine, lantern swinging, steps steady in the snow that was now past his ankles. He moved like the weather wasn't there.

Rosa came up beside him. "Did he just—"

"Yeah."

"Maria?"

"Or Hendersons."

"He thought about it before we did."

"Yeah."

The heater was warming the room. Marcus sat in front of it with his hands out, grinning. "It's like camping."

"Exactly like camping," Dave said.

Rosa was already in motion. She went upstairs and came back with two duffels. Pajamas, toothbrushes, the kids' books. Marcus's plush dog. Lina's headphones. She'd packed for an overnight in less than four minutes. Dave watched her work and felt the same feeling he always felt watching her in a small emergency. She was the actual adult in the house. He was a tall helpful child.

Lina came downstairs without being called. "Where are we going?"

"Probably Maria's," Rosa said.

"Okay." She picked up one of the duffels. No questions. She'd already figured it out from the cold and the candles.

Aldric was back at six-fifty. The knock was lighter this time. Dave opened it fast.

"Maria has the room. She'd like the company. She specifically said *please bring the children.*"

Dave heard the *specifically*. Aldric was quoting.

"Mrs. Henderson has agreed to take Nadine Solis and the twins. I'll move them after you. Ms. Yuen will take Dolores Marsh. They're old friends, and both keep an early evening routine. That accounts for everyone on this end of the street who lives alone or with children."

Dave processed the list. Eight people redistributed.

"What about the heater?"

"Put it on the porch, please. I'll take it back when I come for the others. I'd like to bring it to the Hoopers, their bedrooms will be cold by midnight."

"Yeah. Take it."

They got their boots on. Aldric carried both duffels. Dave carried Marcus. Rosa held Lina's hand. The snow was past Dave's mid-calf. The cold had teeth. The snow was still coming down, fat slow flakes pushed by the howling wind. The street was a silent white tunnel between dark houses, with an orange glow at number eight where Maria had put a candle in the front window.

Maria opened the door before they knocked. Warm air. The smell of something cooking. Beans, maybe, with cumin and bay. Dave glanced past her into the kitchen and saw a small green camping stove on the counter, a pot simmering on the single burner. New. The box still folded on the floor beside it. The fireplace in the living room throwing soft heat in a wide arc, the screen pushed aside, fresh wood stacked beside the hearth. She hugged Rosa first. Then she crouched down to Marcus's height and said, "I have hot chocolate. Do you want to help me make it?"

Marcus had forgotten the cold already.

Aldric set the duffels by the door. "Lock the door behind me. I have stops to make."

Maria put a hand on his arm. Just briefly. "Thank you, Aldric."

He nodded. He didn't say *you're welcome.* He stepped back into the snow.

Dave stood in Maria's living room with his shoes off and his coat still on. Marcus was already at the kitchen counter, asking about marshmallows. The fire crackled in the hearth. Lina sat on the couch and pulled out her book without ceremony. Rosa was already in the kitchen with Maria.

It was a strange feeling. Safer than his own house. Warmer. Borrowed.

Across the street, Gerald Hooper's house was dark. Every house on the block was dark except Aldric's, where the upstairs study window glowed warm and steady, powered by whatever he had running in the garage.

The snow kept falling.

At nine o'clock, the snow slowed even more. By ten, it had almost stopped. The street was buried under eleven inches of silence. No plows. No cars. No sound except the wind settling and the occasional crack of a branch under the weight.

Dave stood on Maria's front porch with a blanket around his shoulders. The kids were asleep upstairs in Maria's guest room, sharing the queen bed. Rosa was at the kitchen table with Maria, both of them with hands around mugs, talking softly in Spanish. The fireplace was still throwing heat through the open archway. The house smelled like cumin and cinnamon and old books.

Across the street, Aldric's garage light was still on. Dave could see him through the open door, moving between shelves. Restocking something. Checking gauges. He moved with the economy of someone who'd done field maintenance in conditions where waste meant danger. No wasted motion. No second trips.

Dave watched for longer than he meant to. Then he went back inside and locked the door behind him.

He woke at five-forty to the sound of a shovel on concrete. The guest room was warm. Marcus was curled against Lina's back. Dave got up without waking them and went to the window. It was still dark.

Aldric was shoveling the sidewalk in front of Maria's house. He'd already done the Ortegas' front walk three doors down. Dave could see the cleared rectangle from up here. Now Aldric was working on Maria's path. His breath came in even clouds. Steady rhythm. Methodical. He'd been out here for a while. Dave could tell by how much was already cleared. At least thirty minutes. Maybe more.

The power came back at around six. Dave saw his own house down the block: the porch light blinked on. He felt the relief in his chest before he understood why.

He went downstairs. Maria was already up, making coffee on the camping stove. She handed him a mug without asking. "Power's back at your place."

"I saw."

"Bring him one too." She nodded at the window.

Dave got dressed in yesterday's clothes and went outside with two mugs. The cold hit him hard. The snow was past his shins. Everything was white and still. The sky was clear for the first time in sixteen hours.

Aldric was at the cul-de-sac already, working on Mrs. Henderson's walkway. His jacket was open. He wasn't wearing gloves.

"Hey," Dave called. "You've been out here since before six?"

Aldric looked up. "Five-twenty. Good morning, Dave. How are the children?"

"Sleeping. Unlike some people." Dave handed him one of the mugs. "From Maria."

Aldric took it with both hands and said thank you with the kind of sincerity that made Dave feel like he'd done something significant instead of just carrying a mug across the street.

"I'll finish this. You should wait a bit, before you bring your family home. The heat is on, but it'll take some time, before the house is warm again."

"I'll help you finish first."

Aldric considered. Then nodded.

They shoveled together for an hour. Gerald came out at seven. He had his own shovel. He didn't say anything, just started on his side of the street. The three of them worked in the cold morning until every walk on the block was clear.

Aldric leaned the shovel against his garage wall and looked down the street at the work they'd done. Clean paths. Every house accessible.

"Thank you both," he said. "Gerald, keep the heater as long as you need it. If you don't need the batteries, I'd like them back for next time."

"Next time," Gerald said.

Aldric smiled. "It's December. There might be a next time."

He went inside.

Dave stood in the street with Gerald for a moment. Both breathing hard. Both looking at the closed door.

"The soup was good," Gerald said.

"Yeah."

"He had everything ready."

"Yeah."

Gerald was quiet for a long time. Then he said: "I don't know anyone who keeps propane heaters and a generator and prepares soup, before the storm."

Dave looked at him. Gerald's face was red from the cold and something else. Something working behind his eyes.

"He's just prepared," Dave said. "Some people are prepared for everything."

Gerald nodded. He didn't look like he believed it, but he went back inside without saying another word.

Dave stood alone in the clean street. The sun was coming up over the rooftops, pale and winter-thin. Mrs. Henderson's lights were on at the cul-de-sac. Smoke from her chimney. Ms. Yuen's living room light was steady across the way. Maria's front door opened three doors down and Marcus stepped out in his coat and boots, looking for him.

It was a good street. He lived on a good street with good people, and one of them happened to have a generator and soup and the instinct to put eight neighbors in the right three houses on a Monday night.

Dave walked to Maria's to collect his family. Rosa was already at the door with the duffels packed. Maria pressed a foil-wrapped loaf of something into Rosa's hands and would not take it back. Marcus wanted to know if they could come back tomorrow. Lina said her bed was probably cold.

They walked home together across the cleared street. The house was warm. Rosa made pancakes. Marcus wanted to go outside. Lina said it was too cold.

Normal Tuesday morning. Except that all the sidewalks were already done, and no one could quite explain how one man had done the math that fast.
