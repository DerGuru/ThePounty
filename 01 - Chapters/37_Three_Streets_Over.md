# Chapter 37: Three Streets Over

Tuesday afternoon. The community center was warm to the point of stuffiness. The radiators clanking under the windows, the air thick with crayon wax and the last of the afternoon's microwaved soup. Rosa was packing up at the far table when Nora came in for pickup. Lily ran to her with her water bottle. Nora bent to take it, and the sleeve of her gray sweater rode up an inch on her right arm.

A band of yellow-green at the heel of the hand, fading at the edges, a few days old. The kind of bruise that came from being held forcefully, not from bumping a doorframe.

Nora pulled the sleeve down without looking. The movement was automatic. It was the movement of a person who had been pulling that sleeve down time and time again.

"Lily, do you have your hat?"

"It's in my backpack."

"Get it out before we go. It's colder than this morning."

Lily knelt on the floor and unzipped her backpack. Nora stood above her with both arms now crossed, both sleeves pulled down past both wrists, and looked at the bulletin board on the wall as if she had not seen it twelve weeks in a row.

Rosa stayed at the table. She did not get up. She did not cross the room. She had learned, in eleven weeks, that crossing the room was the wrong move. Nora's eyes went to the door whenever the room got smaller.

"Thursday?" Rosa said, lightly, from where she was.

Nora's head turned. "Probably. I'll call if not."

"Okay."

Lily found her hat and they left.

Rosa watched them through the window to the parking lot. Nora's car was the gray Corolla in the second row. She held Lily's hand on the curb, which was good. She opened the passenger door for her before her own, which was also good. She looked at the parking lot before she got in, which was the part Rosa had been watching for.

Rosa drove home on Route 9 with the heat at the highest setting and the radio off. She did not turn onto Maple Lane the first time she passed it. She drove a quarter mile past, sat at a red light for a cycle, turned around in the parking lot of the closed garden center, and came back.

She did not know why she had done that.

Yes, she did.

She wanted another minute before she had to be back in her own kitchen, where the question would still be there but the room would be too warm and too full of dinner and people and talk for the question to fit.

She pulled into the driveway. Dave's truck was there. The kitchen light was on. Next door, Aldric's porch lamp was lit. He was not in the chair. He was inside.

She sat in the car with the engine off and her hands on the wheel. The cold was already coming through the windshield. She thought about the yellow-green band. She thought about how long it took a person to pull down a sleeve without consciously recognizing that it went up first. How many times a person had to pull down a sleeve before it became automatic.

After a while she got out of the car, closed the door, and went inside.

Dave was at the stove. She kissed his shoulder. She took the wooden spoon from him and stirred. She would not tell him about the wrist. She told him about DeShawn's spelling test and about Keisha drawing something that looked like a city. She did not tell him about the wrist.

She told him about the wrist at nine-twenty, when they were on the couch and the television was on but neither of them was watching it.

"Three weeks ago it was the upper arm. She said she bumped it on the door frame. Today it was the wrist and that is no place you bump."

Dave did not say anything for a moment. He muted the television.

"You sure?"

"I'm sure."

"Have you said anything to her?"

"No."

"Have you said anything to anyone?"

"I'm telling you now."

He thought about that. He was not a man who jumped at things. He thought, and then he spoke.

"There's a hotline."

"I know there's a hotline."

"You could call it. They tell you what to do."

"I know."

"But she hasn't said anything to you."

"No. She hasn't."

Dave looked at the muted television. A car commercial. Bright snow, a road, a family.

"Then it's hard," he said. "Right? That's the hard part. You can't make her ask."

"I know."

"And if you do it wrong, she stops bringing the girl."

"I know."

They sat with that for a minute.

"Rosa."

"Yeah."

"We'll figure out what to do. We always do. But not tonight."

She leaned her head against his shoulder. He put the volume back on, low, just so that the room was not silent. She fell asleep at his shoulder and woke up thirty minutes later with a stiff neck. She went upstairs and brushed her teeth and slept the rest of the night without dreaming.

She came down Wednesday in the early morning to make coffee. Aldric was at his mailbox.

He should not have been at his mailbox at six-forty. The mail did not come until two in the afternoon. He was at the mailbox sometimes in the morning anyway, because he walked, and the mailbox was a place to stop on the walk. The cardigan was on. The mug was in his right hand. He was looking directly at her.

She put her coffee in a travel mug. She stepped out onto the porch in her slippers, with her coat thrown over her pajamas. The cold caught her at the collar.

"Morning," she said across the lawn.

"Good morning, Rosa." He smiled at her, the small steady smile she knew. He raised the mug. "I'm sorry to ambush you. May I ask you something?"

"Of course."

He came over at his own pace, around the end of the fence between their driveways, and stopped at the bottom of her steps. He did not come up. He stayed at her porch with his mug in both hands now.

"You were slow to come in last night."

It was not a question. Rosa did not answer it. But her hand tightened on the travel mug.

"There is something at the center," he said. "Or someone. I think you went to bed last night with it and got up with it this morning."

She waited. She did not know that her eyes had flicked, very briefly, toward Route 9.

"I'm not asking what it is. I'm asking one thing only, because I'd like to know whether to leave it alone." He looked at her front step, not at her. "Is the person you're worried about a child, or someone close to a child?"

Rosa stood with her coffee at her chest. Her thumb had found the cuff of her sleeve at her wrist. She did not remember putting it there.

She had told Dave on Tuesday night, on the couch, with the television on low. She had not told anyone else. Aldric had not been at his porch when she had pulled in. 

"Close to a child," she said.

Aldric nodded. The smile was still on his face. It was smaller now. He did not say anything else for a beat. He kept looking at her front step, which she had learned, with him, was a thing he did when he was not asking the next question yet.

"One more, if you can. Does the child flinch when the adult's voice gets sharp?"

Rosa thought.

"Yes."

The smile was gone.

He nodded once more. The smile came back, the same small steady one he had arrived with.

"Thank you." He raised the mug. "Have a good day, Rosa."

He went back home.

Rosa stood on her porch with her coffee getting cold in the travel mug and the cold finding its way past her collar. She thought about what she had just answered and what she had seen. She had not given him a name. She had not given him a street. She had given him two facts and one direction, *close to a child*, and he had asked her nothing else.

She went back inside and made breakfast. Then she drove Marcus to kindergarten and Lina to school. She did not think about it again until she was at the sink at eleven and realized she had been rinsing the same plate for a minute.

She dried her hands.

She picked up her phone and put it down. She picked it up again. She did not call the hotline. She did not call Diane. She did not call Dave at work.

She put the phone face down on the counter and went outside to bring in the empty trash bins.

Aldric's car was gone from the driveway.

That evening Rosa was at the front window at six-fifteen, pulling the curtains shut against the cold that came through the glass even when the glass was dry. Next door, the porch lamp at number four was off. The driveway was empty.

She closed the curtain.

Thursday afternoon. The driveway was still empty.

Rosa went to the community center. Marcus had a small fever but Sandra came to sit with him for the hour.

Keisha was at the long table with a new drawing — a city again, this time with a river through it. DeShawn was reading aloud to two of the younger ones. Nora came in at the usual time with Lily, hung up Lily's coat, and crossed to the snack table where Rosa was setting out paper cups.

She looked the same like on Tuesday. Her sleeves were past her wrists. Her shoulders were carried higher than they should be. The small tight smile was the same one.

"Afternoon," Rosa said.

"Afternoon. I'll be back at four-thirty." She bent and said something to Lily, kissed the top of her head, and left.

Rosa watched her cross the parking lot. The gray Corolla pulled out at three-oh-five.

They served snack. Lily ate two graham crackers and asked for a third. Rosa let her have it.

Nora came back at four-thirty.

The first thing Rosa noticed was that Nora did not come all the way in. She stopped in the doorway and looked across the room for Lily before she crossed it. Her coat was undone. Her hair on one side was flat the way hair was flat when a head had been against something. Her hands when she reached for Lily's backpack were the wrong color. Not the pink of cold-from-outside. The white of something else.

"Hi," Nora said.

"Hi."

Nora helped Lily into her coat and started the zipper. It caught at the bottom. She did not try it again. She left it caught.

At the door Lily looked up at her. "Mommy, whats wrong?"

"I'm just tired, sweetie. Let's go."

Rosa walked them out as far as the curb. The cold caught her at the collar. Nora held Lily's hand on the curb. She opened the passenger door for her before her own. She did not look at the parking lot before she got in.

When Rosa came home, Aldric's car was back, parked as always, perfectly between the lines.
