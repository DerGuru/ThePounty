# Chapter 41: The Watcher

*Tuesday, the 8th of April 2025*

Gerald had been up since five. He had not meant to be. He had gone to bed at the usual time, read three pages of a book about the Erie Canal and turned off the light at ten thirty-five. Then he had lain there with his eyes open until the clock read two forty-one. He got up to drink some water, went back and lay down again.

By five he had given up. He went down, made coffee and took it to the study without turning on more light than he had to. He raised the blinds one notch. Fifteen degrees. The street was empty.

He had stood at this window yesterday too. Monday before dawn he had been here, blinds at the same fifteen degrees, watching the same stretch of curb. He had not slept much that night. He had not slept much the night before either.

It had begun on Saturday afternoon. He had been on the porch with the paper when a gray Hyundai had come up Maple Lane from the east, slow, under the speed limit, and rolled past the house. He had logged the plate without thinking, the way a man with thirty-one years of auditing logs anything that does not fit a template. A car going under twenty on a quiet street was not a template. He had written it on the back of the crossword. Still, one car, one slow pass, was nothing.

The same car had come past again on Sunday morning. From the west this time. Same speed. Same plate.

Gerald had opened the blue notebook on Sunday at eleven oh five.

Monday morning at six oh four, the Hyundai had pulled to the curb by the playground at the east end of the street and stayed there. Engine off. Driver in the seat, taking out the newspaper. Gerald had been at the kitchen window with his coffee and had seen it the moment it stopped. He had stood very still for half a minute. Then he had taken the coffee to the study.

The Hyundai had been there until ten fourteen. Gerald had logged each end of that window. The middle of the window he had filled in by checking from the window every twelve to fifteen minutes, which was the maximum he could go before the not-checking became its own kind of strain. He had logged the driver: white male, perhaps forty, dark cap, no glasses, no sign on the dashboard that would mark him as a contractor or a delivery driver waiting between runs. 

A guest's car parked in someone's drive, or at the curb in front of the house they were visiting, or directly opposite it. A guest's car did not park at the playground end of the street with the engine off and the driver's seat reclined two clicks.

This morning it was back, and he had seen it the moment he raised the blinds. His hand had the pen moving before he had decided to write anything. He opened the blue notebook and added the morning's line under the others.

*April 8. Gray Hyundai, MD plate ending 47B. Parked yesterday by the playground. This morning in front of the Chens'. Same car. Same man.*

Gerald drank his coffee and watched the sky over the playground go from black to gray to the color milk got when it had been left out.

At seven oh two he saw movement on Aldric's porch.

Aldric came out at the usual time with the usual mug and the usual book. He sat in the right-hand chair. He set the mug on the table. He opened the book.

Gerald watched.

There was nothing to watch. That was the point. The book opened to a place where the bookmark had been. The mug went down and came up at intervals that had been the same for nine months, except when it was too cold for outside reading. Everything as usual.

Gerald wrote, very small, on the back of yesterday's page: *7:02 Venn enters the porch. As usual.*

He had thought, when he started keeping the blue notebook in November, that the hard part of watching a man across the street would be catching him doing something. He had been wrong about that. The hard part was catching him doing *exactly* what he always did when there were reasons for him to do something else.

A car had parked on the street that did not belong on the street. A man Gerald had never seen before was sitting in that car with a coffee. Aldric Venn, who had logged every new face on Maple Lane in nine months and asked Sandra for the name of the woman who had repainted her shutters in October, was on his porch reading a book and did not look at the Hyundai.

Why did he not look?

Gerald had stood at this window for nine months. He had seen Aldric look at every car that turned onto the street. Every single one. Some of the looks were so small you would miss them if you were not also a man who had been at a window for nine months. A flick of the eyes. The smallest pause in a hand bringing a cup up. A glance that traveled across the top of a book.

He did not look at the Hyundai.

That was the first thing.

The second thing was the hedge.

At nine o'clock Aldric came out of the garage with the hand shears and a tarp folded under one arm. Gerald wrote the time. Then he stopped writing and watched, because Aldric had cut the hedge eighteen days ago. Gerald had logged it.

Aldric did not cut a hedge that did not need cutting. Gerald had a separate page for hedge maintenance. He had nine months of dates. Aldric trimmed the front hedge every five to six weeks from April through September, every eight weeks in autumn, not at all in winter, the first cut of the year had been in the second half of March. He had cut it on March twenty-first. It was April eighth.

Aldric trimmed.

He spread the tarp along the foot of the hedge and worked from the porch end outward, the shears moving in a steady rhythm Gerald could almost hear through the closed window. He trimmed the front face square and level. Then the east section. Then the west section. He had cut the west section to the same square line on March twenty-first. It did not need cutting today. And to take it one step further, he worked along it twice. The second pass was even slower than the first. The shears closed on almost nothing.

The west side faced the Chens', across the empty lot at number six.

Gerald wrote: *9:00 — hedge. Not due. West section ×2. Sightline.*

He put the pen down.

Something behind the sternum had gone tight. He recognized it in his body before he could name it. The name, when it came, was the same name he had given that feeling on the morning of September nineteenth. He had stood at this window with a newspaper he was not reading, watching Aldric pull out of the drive with a small canvas bag.

He had felt it on the nineteenth, at this same window. He felt it now.

He went and got the blue notebook from behind the envelopes.

He sat down at the desk and opened it past the columns and the dates and the pasted clippings and the photograph he had taken of the back of Aldric's car last December which had turned out to show nothing at all. Past all of it to a fresh page.

He wrote the date at the top. *April 8, 2025.*

He wrote: *He knows.*

He stared at the two words. Then he wrote, underneath:

*The car has been there two days. He has not looked at it. He has never not looked at a car.*

*He cut a hedge that did not need cutting. The section he repeated was on the side that faces the watcher's car.*

*He is doing what he did in September. Before the trip. The week before.*

He stopped. He went back. He crossed out *the week before* and wrote *the days before*. He was not sure about the week, and the blue notebook was for things he was sure about. Then he sat for a long time without writing.

At nine eleven a Greenfield Township cruiser came up Maple Lane from the east at the speed limit with the window down.

Gerald had seen this cruiser on Monday afternoon. He had seen it on Sunday evening. He had seen it almost every day in the last five weeks. He had logged it in the page he kept for *Officer Kim, drive-bys* since November.

Aldric was at the foot of the hedge folding the tarp.

He looked up.

He raised a hand.

The cruiser slowed. Gerald could not see the officer's face from this angle, but he saw the hand come up inside the cab and stay up a beat longer than a wave required. The hand came down. The cruiser drove on.

Aldric finished folding the tarp.

Gerald wrote, in the blue notebook, on the same page: *9:11 Kim. He waves. She waves. She waits the extra beat.*

He looked at the line. He underlined *waits the extra beat*. Then he sat back.

A man in a car was watching number four from across the street. A police officer was driving past number four every day for five weeks. Aldric Venn was on his porch reading a book, cutting a hedge that did not need cutting and waving at the police officer. Kim had come by exactly once in the months before. One morning after the thing with the contractor in October. And on the morning after the night the man from three streets over had ended up in the hospital her daily drive bys had started. Gerald had circled both dates in the black notebook without yet being able to say why.

He picked up the pen again. He wrote, slowly, neatly, in his auditor's hand: *Something is happening on this street. Aldric is in the middle of it.*

He read it back.

He could not have said which frightened him more, the watcher at the curb or how little the watched man seemed to mind. It created a feeling of standing at the end of a long row of numbers that finally added up. The sum was correct. The correct sum was bad.

He had been right.

He had been right in June when Aldric had moved in with three boxes and a perfect handshake. He had been right in September when the deliveries had come and gone and Aldric had spent his evenings in the basement. He had been right in November when the name had returned four results on Google. He had been right in January, when the man he had paid came back with a file too clean to be true and told him to leave it alone. He had been *wrong* once, in the library on a November afternoon. The wrongness had lasted two weeks and ended in the assessor's database at midnight.

He had been right.

He did not feel right. He felt like a man who had been waiting in a chair for a doctor to come in with a piece of paper, and the doctor had come in, and now there was the piece of paper.

Pruitt's voice came back to him from January, flat over the bad phone line. *A file that clean has people behind it. They don't take kindly to prying eyes.* Gerald had thanked him and kept the notebook anyway. Now there was a man at the curb with a cup of coffee, and the file had a new face in it.

Sandra moved in the kitchen. He heard the kettle, the cupboard. He heard her say something to herself about the toaster, which had been on the verge of giving up since New Year's eve, and which Sandra had been speaking to since then in the conviction that civility would keep it going a little longer.

Gerald closed the blue notebook. He slid it into the drawer behind the envelopes. He put the black notebook on top of it, page open to a column of weather observations from March that he had kept as cover for any morning Sandra wandered in. He capped the pen and laid it parallel to the notebook.

He stood up. He stretched, slowly, because his back had set in the chair.

He looked out the window one more time before going down to breakfast.

The Hyundai was gone.

The street was empty. Aldric was inside. The shears were in the garage. The porch chair was in its place and the book was on the table beside the empty mug and the morning had gone on the way mornings went on.

Gerald stood there for a long moment with his hand on the window frame.

Then he went downstairs. Sandra was at the table with the paper. She looked up.

"You were up early."

"Couldn't sleep."

"You should have come down. I'd have made you something."

"It's fine."

She looked at him a beat longer and went back to the paper.

Gerald poured himself a fresh cup. He sat opposite her, picked up the section she had finished with and held it up. He read none of it.

After a while Sandra said, without looking up, "Everything all right?"

"Fine."

"You sure?"

"Sure."

She turned a page. She did not ask again.

Gerald drank his coffee. Across the street, a man he had been watching for nine months was changing his pattern again.

He had changed it once before. In September he had gone quiet, spending his evenings in the basement, then driving off for two days that ended somewhere Gerald never got to see.

He set the cup down very carefully and did not let it make a sound on the saucer.
