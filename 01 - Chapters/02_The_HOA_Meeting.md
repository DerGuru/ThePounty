# Chapter 2: The HOA Meeting

Sandra had been looking forward to this meeting for eleven days. Not because she enjoyed HOA meetings. Nobody enjoyed HOA meetings. They were two hours of someone reading minutes nobody remembered approving, followed by forty minutes of Diane Kessler complaining about the park benches, followed by Gerald falling asleep in his chair and pretending he hadn't.

But Aldric Venn had said he would attend.

He'd said it on Thursday, when she'd dropped off a lemon loaf. He'd opened the door in a pressed shirt and slacks, at eight in the morning, on a day he presumably had nothing to do, and said, "Mrs. Hooper. What a lovely surprise. Is that lemon? You shouldn't have, but I'm profoundly glad you did."

Then she'd mentioned the meeting, casually, the way you mention things casually when you've been rehearsing the casual mention for ten minutes on the walk over.

"Oh," he'd said. "A homeowners' association. Do you have monthly gatherings?"

"Second Tuesday. Seven sharp. At the community center on Birch."

"I wouldn't miss it. Thank you for telling me." He'd made a note in that leather notebook of his. Actually written it down. With a pen. Like someone had invited him to a state dinner.

So here she was, at 6:47, in the folding chair closest to the refreshments table, where she could see the door and also reach the cream cheese squares without getting up. Gerald was next to her, arms crossed, already composing his nap posture.

"He's not going to come," Gerald said.

"He said he'd come."

"People say things."

"He wrote it down."

Gerald didn't respond to that. He pulled out his phone and opened the weather app, which was what Gerald did when he wanted to signal that a conversation was beneath him.

Diane Kessler was already at the front, shuffling papers. Bob Muñoz was setting up the projector, which hadn't worked correctly since 2019 but which Bob insisted on using because he'd made slides. Last month's slides had included a pie chart about irrigation compliance that nobody understood, including Bob.

At 6:58, Aldric Venn walked through the door.

He was wearing a sport coat. A sport coat. To an HOA meeting in a community center that smelled like floor polish and old coffee. It wasn't a fancy sport coat. It was a perfectly normal navy blazer that fit him like it had been made for him personally, which it probably had, because nothing about this man was accidental.

He scanned the room. Not quickly, not slowly. The way someone reads a menu they already know. Then he walked directly to Diane Kessler, extended his hand, and said something Sandra couldn't hear. Diane's expression went from harried to flattered in under a second. She laughed. Diane Kessler laughed. Sandra had never seen Diane Kessler laugh at anything that wasn't someone else's code violation.

Then he turned, spotted Sandra, and smiled. He crossed the room.

"Mrs. Hooper. Thank you again for the invitation. Gerald." He nodded at Gerald. Gerald nodded back, which was generous by Gerald's standards.

"I saved you a seat," Sandra said. She had. The chair to her left had her purse on it. She moved the purse.

"That's very thoughtful of you." He sat down. Straightened his coat. Looked at the room the way you look at a theater before the show starts. Attentive. Interested. Ready.

"It's usually pretty boring," Sandra said. "Fair warning."

"I doubt that very much. Any gathering where people discuss shared space is inherently interesting. You learn a great deal about a community from how it negotiates its boundaries."

Sandra didn't know what to do with that sentence. It sounded like something from a documentary. She reached for a cream cheese square.

The meeting started at 7:04. Bob's projector worked on the third attempt. Diane read the minutes from last month, which included a lengthy discussion about whether the communal compost bin constituted an "attractive nuisance." Sandra had forgotten that argument entirely. It came back now like a bad dream.

Aldric listened.

That was the thing. He *listened*. Not the way Gerald listened, which was with visible skepticism and occasional sighs. Not the way Sandra listened, which was with half an ear while scanning the room for information. Aldric listened the way a student listens to a lecture. Upright. Focused. Occasionally making a small note in his notebook.

When Diane asked if anyone had corrections to the minutes, Aldric raised his hand.

Everyone turned.

"I wasn't present last month, so I may be misunderstanding," he said. "But the minutes reference a vote on hedge heights in section four. Was that a binding vote or advisory?"

Silence. Nobody had ever asked that question. Sandra wasn't sure anyone had ever *thought* that question.

Diane blinked. "It was... binding. I believe."

"Wonderful. Thank you. I want to make sure I understand the governance structure correctly. It's admirably organized."

Diane straightened her papers. She was standing a little taller. "Thank you, Mr. Venn."

"Please. Aldric."

The meeting continued. Irrigation compliance. The park bench situation. Someone's fence was the wrong shade of white, which Sandra thought was insane, because white was white, but apparently there was *warm* white and *cool* white and the HOA charter specified warm, and this was worth twenty minutes of everyone's life.

Aldric raised his hand during the fence discussion.

"If I may," he said. "Would it be possible to obtain a copy of the approved color palette? I'd like to ensure my own maintenance decisions are compliant from the beginning. I'd rather ask beforehand than apologize after."

Bob Muñoz looked like someone had told him he'd won an award. "We have a binder," he said. "I can get you a copy."

"That would be very generous of you, Bob. Thank you."

Bob beamed. Sandra had never seen Bob beam. Bob's default expression was somewhere between mild constipation and disappointment.

They moved on to new business. Diane asked if there were any items from the floor.

Aldric raised his hand again.

"I have a question, if the group doesn't mind. It's not urgent, merely curious."

"Go ahead," Diane said. She was speaking to him differently now. Softer. Like he was a visiting dignitary.

"I'm new to the neighborhood, as you know. I've been reading the HOA newsletter archive online — very informative, by the way, well-written — and I noticed that some families have been here for decades. The Muñozes, the Yuen family, the Kesslers." He nodded to each as he named them. "I'm curious about the turnover. How often do families move in and out? Is this a neighborhood where people tend to stay?"

It was a strange question. Sandra felt it. Not wrong. Not inappropriate. Just... specific. Like he already knew the answer and was checking something else.

"Oh, most people stay," Diane said. "We've had very little turnover in the last ten years. The Hendersons moved in five years ago. The Parkers before that. A few rentals on the north end that rotate."

"And families that have left? In the last, say, fifteen years?"

"Why do you ask?" That was Gerald. Quiet. Direct.

Aldric turned to him. Smiled. "I find the history of a place fascinating. A street is the sum of everyone who's ever lived there. I like knowing whose footsteps I'm walking in." He looked back at Diane. "But if it's a complicated question, I don't want to take up the group's time."

"No, no," Diane said. "Let me think. The Bowmans left in... 2012? 2013? Moved to Florida. The Reinhardts before them. Oh, and the young woman who rented the Garcias' house for a year or two — what was her name? Quiet girl. Kept to herself."

"I remember her," Bob said. "Brown hair. Worked at the library, I think. Or was it the county office?"

"Something like that," Diane said. "She moved away. 2018? 2019?"

Aldric wrote something in his notebook. A single line. Sandra craned her neck but couldn't see it.

"Thank you," he said. "I appreciate the context. It helps me feel connected to the community."

The meeting ended at 8:43. Seventeen minutes shorter than average, which Sandra attributed to Diane's elevated mood. Normally Diane circled back to old business like a dog returning to a buried bone. Tonight she let things go. Aldric had complimented her organizational skills twice, and that was apparently enough to alter the fabric of spacetime.

Sandra watched Aldric make the rounds afterward. He shook every hand. He remembered every name. He asked Bob about the irrigation slides with the kind of genuine interest that should have been impossible to fake and yet felt engineered. He complimented Diane's minutes a third time. He told Mrs. Alvarez that her cookies were exceptional and asked what kind of vanilla she used. He somehow knew Mrs. Alvarez had brought the cookies even though no one had said so.

He was standing by the door when Sandra approached him.

"So? Bored?"

He looked surprised. "Not for a moment. This is exactly what I hoped it would be."

"An HOA meeting."

"A community. People who care about where they live, negotiating shared space with — largely — good intentions and mutual respect. That's not boring. That's civilized."

Sandra laughed. "You're the only person who's ever used the word 'civilized' about Diane Kessler's minutes."

"Diane Kessler has a thankless job and does it with dedication. That deserves recognition." He paused. "Also, her minutes are genuinely well-organized. I read municipal reports for years that were far worse."

Sandra's ears perked. "Municipal reports? For work?"

"In a former life." The smile stayed. Nothing else came.

She waited. He didn't add anything. The pause stretched. It wasn't awkward. It was just... finished. Like a sentence with a period and no sequel.

"Well," Sandra said. "Same time next month."

"I wouldn't miss it." He shook her hand. Held it just long enough to feel intentional. "Thank you again, Sandra. For the invitation, for the seat, and for the lemon loaf last Thursday. It was exceptional. Meyer lemons?"

"How did you—"

"The flavor is rounder. Less sharp. It's a subtle difference but it matters."

Sandra didn't know what Meyer lemons were. She'd used the ones from Costco. They were yellow. She nodded anyway.

She walked to the car with Gerald. He was already pulling his keys out, which meant he wanted to leave fast, which meant he'd noticed something. Or decided something. Gerald operated in two modes: bored and suspicious. There was no middle.

"Well?" she said. "What did you think?"

"He read the newsletter archive."

"So?"

"Who reads the newsletter archive? Going back how far?"

"He said he wanted to understand the community."

Gerald unlocked the car. Didn't respond until they were both inside and the doors were closed.

"He asked about people who moved away."

"He asked about *history*."

"He asked about a specific timeframe. Fifteen years. That's not curiosity. That's a search parameter."

Sandra rolled her eyes. "Gerald. He's a polite man who asked a polite question at a community meeting. That's not a crime."

"I didn't say it was a crime."

"You're doing the voice."

"What voice?"

"The one where you sound like a podcast about unsolved disappearances."

Gerald started the car. He looked at the rearview mirror for a moment. Aldric was still at the door, talking to Diane. Shaking her hand one more time. Diane was laughing again.

"He knew about the lemons," Gerald said.

"What?"

"Meyer lemons. He identified the type by taste. From a loaf. Eleven days ago."

Sandra put on her seatbelt. "Some people know about baking, Gerald."

"Some people know about baking." He pulled out of the lot. "And some people pay attention in a way that isn't normal."

Sandra looked out the window. The community center shrank in the mirror. Aldric was walking to his car now. The Volvo. Even in the parking lot, it was the cleanest car there.

"He was nice," she said.

"He was perfect," Gerald said. "That's not the same thing."

Sandra didn't answer. She thought about Meyer lemons. She'd look them up later. She didn't tell Gerald that.

At home, she put the kettle on and checked her phone. There was already a text from Diane Kessler in the neighborhood group chat: *What a lovely addition to our meetings! So refreshing to have someone who actually CARES about community governance.*

Sandra smiled. Gerald was in his study with the door closed. She could hear the desk drawer open. The one where he kept the notebook.

She made tea. Thought about asking Gerald if he wanted some. Decided not to. He'd come out when he was ready. Or when he'd finished writing down whatever it was he was writing down.

The kettle clicked. She poured. She drank.

Meyer lemons. The ones from Costco were just regular lemons. They had a yellow sticker on them. She'd never checked what kind.

He'd known anyway.

Huh.
