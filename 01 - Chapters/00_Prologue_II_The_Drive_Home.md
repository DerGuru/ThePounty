# Prologue: The Drive Home

*Saturday, the 5th of July 2025*

Aldric bought a loaf he did not need and took his time about paying for it, because a man who hurried at a market was a man with somewhere else to be, and he wished to look like a man with nowhere else to be. The appearance was mostly true. He had one task left today, and it was the woman three stalls away, pretending to choose berries she would not eat.

Her name was Miranda. He had carried the name for more than a year, since a man he would not name had set it down in front of him, along with a face and a small, plain instruction. He had said the words back. A word given was permanent. That was a rule, and the rule was why he was here, in this town, at this market, buying bread on a Saturday with the patience of a man who had all the time there was.

She believed she had found him. That was the point. He had spent three Saturdays making himself findable.

It had taken some care to be seen without seeming to wish it. In May he had gone to her church, which cost him nothing, since he believed in none of it and said so to no one. He had knelt a beat behind the room and risen a beat behind it, not because he could not learn the order but because a newcomer who fumbled the order was easy to place and easier to forgive. He had wanted to be placed. He had let her watch a man pretending, because she, of all of them, would know a pretense when she saw one. It took one to know one. He had counted on precisely that.

Then he had stopped going, before nearness to her could harden into a habit she could predict. A pattern was a leash you handed to whoever noticed it. He gave her three weeks of absence. Then he appeared at the one market she kept and let her find the rest.

Today he had done the thing he had been saving. He had let her catch him looking.

It was a small gift and an expensive one. A watcher who believed herself unseen kept her habits, and her habits kept showing him their shape. There was a great deal in them still to learn. Letting her know he saw her would cost him that. But she was careful to the edge of vanishing, and a woman that careful would never drift close enough to be read from across a town. She had to be drawn in. So he traded the cheap advantage of being a ghost for the dearer one of her company. She would follow him now. People followed a thing that had noticed them. It was the noticing they could not leave alone.

He paid for the bread. He did not look back at her as he crossed to the lot. He knew to the step where she stood. He knew she would mark the not-looking and read it as carelessness, and be wrong. He was not careless. He simply did not check a tail he had invited.

The Volvo started on the first turn of the key, as it always did. He pulled out slowly and drove as he always drove, which today happened to be how a man drives when he wishes to be followed without seeming to. He signaled early. He held the limit. He caught a yellow he could have made and stopped for it instead, which gave the small gray hatchback three cars back the room to close the gap she had let open. He had identified her car on the second Saturday. He noted now that she still trusted three cars of distance, that she signaled when no one required it, and that she had washed the car this week. All of it went into the file he kept on her, the one with no paper in it, the only kind he allowed himself anymore.

He led her home the short way, past the school and over the creek, because the short way had the bridge, and the bridge had a single lane that would force her to hang back and feel, for a moment, that she was choosing to. He turned in under the wooden sign. Oak Ridge. Birchwood. Maple Lane between them, which had amused him the day he arrived and amused him still. A street named for a tree, a few streets from the one she rented on, which was named for a tree as well. She would notice that today. He had noticed it more than a year ago, and had chosen the house in part because of it. Proximity was a courtesy you did not announce.

He pulled into the driveway at number four in a single motion and turned off the engine. Down the lawn, Dave Ortega lifted a hand off the mower and called a greeting, the same open greeting he gave everyone, which was one of the better things about him. Aldric raised a hand in return. He said something kind about the stripes Dave had mowed into the grass, because they were well done and Dave had worked at them. Rosa came onto the porch with a towel on her shoulder and a question about whether he had eaten. Rosa believed no one had ever eaten, and she was usually right.

He did not look toward the mouth of the street, where a small gray hatchback sat with its engine running. He did not need to. He knew she was seeing what he had arranged for her to see. The careful man at number four. The warm family at number two, close enough to lean a ladder between the houses. And the warm family, she would already be working out, went to her church.

He had built her a door and set it two houses from his own. Now he would stand on his porch and let her believe she had picked the lock.

It was here, on the porch step, with the mower going and the bread warm against his arm, that he caught himself smiling. Not the built smile, the one he had spent twenty years making honest for the neighbors. The other one. The one he had not decided to wear. It had come up on its own at the cleanness of it, at how well she had followed, and at the pleasure of being read by someone almost good enough to do it.

He stopped it. Pleasure was not the same as correctness. A thing could be satisfying and wrong in the same breath, and the safest moment to distrust a feeling was the moment it felt best. That was a rule, and he had written it himself, years ago, for precisely this. He filed the smile, the reason it had come, and the small fact that filing it had not made it leave.

Then he went up to thank Rosa, because she had asked whether he had eaten and meant the asking. That deserved thanking. Behind him, at the mouth of the street, the little car finally pulled away. She had her door now. He had her name, and had held it longer than he had owned the house. She did not have his. That, for today, was the shape of the distance between them, and he meant to keep it that shape for as long as the work required.
