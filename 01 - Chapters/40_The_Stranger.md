# Chapter 40: The Stranger

*Monday, the 7th of April 2025*

Monday morning. The light coming off Maple Lane was the pale spring-gold that did not warm anything yet. Seven forty-two. Calvey had been parked since six.

He sat low in the rental, a charcoal Hyundai Elantra picked because it vanished into any American suburb without trying, with a paper cup of motel coffee gone cold in the holder and a copy of yesterday's *Washington Post* open across the steering wheel. Anyone who looked twice would see a man waiting for a colleague. A salesman. A surveyor on his break. Nothing.

Nobody had looked twice. Three days now. Suburb.

The file was light. It usually was on a job like this. A name, *Aldric Venn*. An address, *4 Maple Lane, Greenfield Township, Maryland*. A photograph, passport quality, two years old, taken under fluorescent light. One line of instruction. *Disturb him. Make the point. Walk away.*

Three jobs out of four were like that. The client wanted leverage, not a body. Calvey preferred it that way. He had a daughter in Bristol who got him every other weekend if he was lucky and never if he was not, and the difference between *disturb* and *body* was the difference between flying back on Friday and not flying back at all for quite some time.

The brief had come through Mosely in London. Mosely did not say who paid. Mosely never did. He said *Consultancy work, Stateside, ten days, twenty in dollars*, and Calvey had said yes before the second sentence. He always said yes to Mosely. Mosely paid on the day.

What Mosely had said about the target: *Former contractor. Civilian profile now. Won't be expecting anyone.*

What the file had said about the target: nothing useful. No service record. No employment history. No social media. No medical. Just the address and the face.

What Calvey had thought about the target, sitting in the motel on Saturday night with the file open on the bed: *Civilian. Two days, three at most.*

That was Saturday.

This was Monday.

He watched the house.

Number four was a two-storey house, beige siding like every other house on the street, with a porch that ran straight along the front and wrapped round the sides. A Volvo wagon sat in the drive. The lawn was the kind of green you got only from someone who took it personally. A flower box clipped to the porch railing, something purple growing out of it in a neat row. A flag bracket on one of the porch posts, no flag in it.

Nothing unusual. Nothing wrong. That was the point of streets like this. One builder had put up every house on Maple Lane and sold them on one at a time.

He had done the drive-by on Saturday afternoon. He had done it again Sunday morning. Now he was here on a Monday, the day the street started moving, because you could not plan an approach until you had seen the rhythm. People in suburbs ran on rhythm. They left at the same time, came back at the same time, took the dog out at the same time. Find the gap. Use the gap.

By seven forty-five the gap was already filling. Directly across from number four, a man in his sixties came out with a coffee mug and a recycling bin, set the bin at the kerb, but did not look up. Further down on the same side, a woman in scrubs got into a Camry and reversed out without checking her mirrors. Down at the cul-de-sac end, someone was walking a small white dog that took its time at every shrub.

Standard. He logged each of them and went back to number four.

At eight oh two the door opened.

Calvey lowered the newspaper half an inch.

The man who came out matched the photograph as well as passport photographs ever did, which was not very well. Mid-forties, perhaps fifty. Average height, average build. A grey sweatshirt, dark trousers, trainers that looked new. He carried a mug and a book. He sat down in the porch chair on the right of the door — the right, Calvey noted, which gave him the view of the street and the side of his own house at the same time — and he set the mug on the small table beside him. He opened the book. He began to read.

A man having coffee on his porch. The most ordinary thing on a Monday morning in April in Maryland.

Calvey watched.

What he was watching for was the gap between the photograph and the man. The photograph was always the easy part. Face, hair, height. The harder thing was the part the photograph never caught. How the man sat in his own house. How he held a cup. Whether his eyes did the small constant work of someone who had reasons to watch, or whether they were the eyes of someone who had never had reasons.

The man on the porch read his book. He drank his coffee. Once, when a cyclist passed on the far pavement, he looked up. A glance, normal length, the head not moving much, the eyes doing the work. He looked back at the book.

Calvey breathed out slowly.

That was the kind of glance most civilians did not do. Most civilians moved the head before they moved the eyes. They turned, they checked, they came back. The man on the porch had let his eyes go and kept his head almost perfectly still, and that was either nothing or it was the only thing on this street.

He told himself it was nothing.

At eight thirty-six the man stood, closed the book and went inside. The door closed. Calvey waited.

At nine o'clock exactly, the side door of the garage opened and the man came out wheeling a push mower. He set it on the grass and bent to check something on the underside. He stood up, walked to the garden tap, filled a small can with water and walked back. He did not pour it into the mower. He poured it into the flower box on the porch railing. He set the can down. He pulled the cord.

The mower started on the first pull.

Calvey watched him take the first pass across the lawn. He watched him take the second. He watched him take the third.

Something shifted in his chest.

He could not name it at first. The man was mowing a lawn like anyone else. There was nothing in the work itself he could put his finger on.

It was the walk.

Calvey shifted in the seat to get the angle. The hips did the work. The shoulders did not. The centre stayed level all the way along the row and the rest of him followed, no bob, no drop, no shift in the line of the head. He walked behind the mower. He had walked from the garage to the tap. He had walked from the tap to the flower box. Calvey had been watching it for an hour and had only just seen it.

That was something a body learned somewhere and did not give back. Calvey could not have said where. Martial arts, perhaps? Boxing? A trade that taught you to carry weight without spilling? There were a hundred ways a body got that way and none of them, on their own, meant anything. Only the body itself meant something. The body had been taught.

He set the newspaper down on the passenger seat.

At nine fourteen, just as Venn was reaching the strip nearest the porch, a Greenfield Township cruiser came down Maple from the east. Speed limit. Window down. Calvey logged the make — Ford Explorer, white over black, light bar — and the silhouette in the driver's seat. Female. Dark hair pulled back. Mid-thirties.

Venn cut the mower.

He raised his hand.

The cruiser slowed. The officer raised her hand back. She held it a beat longer than the wave required. Then she drove on.

Calvey watched the cruiser pass his own car. The officer's eyes did the wider scan in the passing — driver, plate, mirrors. Standard. He kept the paper where it was and turned a page he was not reading.

The cruiser turned right at the bottom of the block. Calvey stayed still until he had counted twenty.

Then he wrote the cruiser into the back of his mental file. *Local PD. Familiar with target. Friendly contact. Off-duty drive-bys?* He could not tell yet whether that was a problem or just a piece of information.

Someone with a badge knew Aldric Venn well enough to slow down for him.

Venn pulled the cord again. He finished the last two strips and switched it off. He tipped the mower back, walked it to the garage. He came back out with a stiff broom and swept the cuttings off the path. He coiled the cord on the garden tap. He hung the broom inside the garage. He came back out, looked at the lawn for two seconds, walked to one spot near the centre, bent down, picked up something invisible from the grass and dropped it into the bin by the side gate.

Then he stood on his porch and looked at the street.

The look took less than two seconds. The head did not move. The eyes did.

Calvey turned a page of the *Post*.

The look passed the Hyundai, paused for half a beat that might have been nothing, then went on. East along the street. West along the street. Back at the lawn. Then Venn went inside.

Calvey sat very still for a long minute.

He reached into the door pocket, took out the burner phone and texted Mosely the single word *RECCE*.

A minute later: *Issue?*

He thought about how to answer. He looked at the lawn at number four, the perfect rows, the broom marks on the path, the way the man had walked behind the mower and back to the garage. He thought about Mosely's voice on the phone Saturday night. *Won't be expecting anyone.*

He thought about his daughter in Bristol.

He typed: *Need a week. Not three days.*

The reply came in under thirty seconds. *Approved. Don't mess it up.*

Calvey put the phone away.

He stayed parked for another forty minutes, because moving now would be the wrong move. A man who had been there since six o'clock did not leave at half nine when the street was full. He stayed until ten fourteen. A postal truck came down the street. He pulled out behind it and drove the long way back to the motel, three subdivisions he did not need to see, past a coffee shop where he stopped for a fresh cup and a sandwich. He ate the sandwich in the car park.

When he got back to room 207 he sat on the bed and unfolded the file again.

The photograph. Two years old. Fluorescent light. The face neutral, the eyes looking just past the camera at something the photographer could not see.

He put the photograph back in the folder and closed it. He sat for a while with his hands on his knees, looking at the wall above the television.

Three days, he had thought, on Saturday night.

He did not think three days any more.

He had done twelve of these in nine years. He had been wrong about the time on three of them. He had been wrong about the man on none.

He picked up the phone, set an alarm for half past five and went to take a shower. He stood under the water longer than he needed to.

He could not shake the feeling that something was off.
