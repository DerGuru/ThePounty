# Chapter 32: The Letter

The envelope was in the mailbox on Monday afternoon, between the electric bill and a flyer for a roofing company that had been mailing the same flyer since November. Under the flyer was a smaller envelope, hand-addressed in her mother's thin uphill writing. Probably for tomorrow. But this envelope looked important. Maria saw the law firm's name in the top left corner before she had the rest of the mail out of the slot. *Hartmann & Reyes, Family Law, White Plains, NY.* She knew the firm. They had been her firm for ten months. 

She carried the stack into the kitchen and put it on the counter beside the screwdriver case. The case had been on the counter for nine days. She had told herself twice that she would put it back in the closet and twice she had not. The channel locks were on top of the case. The faucet was still dry.

She did not open the envelope right away. She put a kettle on. She washed the two mugs in the sink, dried them, and put them in the cupboard. She wiped down the counter she had already wiped down that morning. The kettle clicked off. She poured water over a tea bag in the larger mug and watched the color come up out of the bag in slow brown clouds.

Then she sat down at the kitchen table with the envelope and a butter knife and slit the top.

Three pages. A cover letter from Daniel Hartmann, two pages of attached correspondence from Carlos's attorney in Phoenix. She read the cover letter first.

*Dear Mrs. Alvarez,*

*Please find attached a communication received this morning from opposing counsel. As you will see, Mr. Alvarez is requesting a formal appraisal of the marital residence at 8 Maple Lane prior to finalization of the property division. This is not unusual procedure; however, the timing and the specific language used by opposing counsel suggest a strategic intent to delay finalization. Per our agreement, no appraisal can be commissioned without your signature on the attached authorization. You are not required to sign. I would like to discuss options tomorrow at 10:00 a.m.*

*Best regards,*  
*Daniel Hartmann*

She read it again. Then she read the two pages from Carlos's attorney. The language was the kind of language attorneys used when they wanted to sound reasonable while doing something that was not. *In the interest of an equitable division. Recent market fluctuations. Necessary due diligence. We propose a sixty-day window for the appraisal process, with finalization to follow.*

Sixty days. On top of the ten months since Carlos left.

She set the pages down on the table and drank her tea.

The house was quiet and the light outside was already going gray at the edges even though it was barely past three. The clock above the stove ticked. The refrigerator hummed. From the window over the sink she could see her own yard, the fence she and Aldric had painted in November, the picnic table Jesús had built with Carlos in the summer Jesús was twelve.

She had lived in this house for twenty-three years. She had brought Jesús home from the hospital through that front door. She had measured him against the door frame between the kitchen and the hallway every August on his birthday until he was sixteen and refused. The marks were still there. Never painted over.

The house was not hers. Not legally. Carlos's name was on the deed beside hers. He could hold that name there as long as he wanted. He wanted to.

She stood up and walked through the house. She didn't know why. She went into the living room and looked at the couch, the lamp, the bookshelf. She went into the hallway and put her hand flat against the door frame on the marks. Jesús, age seven. Age nine. Age twelve. Her own handwriting in pencil, the numbers getting smaller as she had run out of room near the top.

She went back to the kitchen. She tipped her head back. The water stain on the ceiling above the table was the same shape it had been in September. She had not done anything about it. She did not know what to do about it.

She picked up her phone. She did not call Jesús. She did not call her mother. She did not call her sister. She put it down and opened the laptop instead. She typed *certified residential appraiser Westchester County* into the search bar.

She wrote three numbers on the back of the electric bill envelope. Not because she had decided to commission her own appraisal. But somewhere underneath she remembered the slip of paper with three phone numbers on it from December, and how it had felt to have a list in front of her that she had not had to make.

This list she had made.

She put the envelope with the three numbers on the corner of the table, separate from the law firm pages. She rinsed her mug and made dinner. She ate it at the table with the envelope at her elbow and a book she was not really reading propped against the salt shaker. After dinner she did the dishes, took the trash out to the curb, went into the living room, and worked on the cardigan for the customer in Portland until her hands got tired. She went to bed at ten.

She did not sleep until after midnight. When sleep finally found her, it was a thin sleep, the kind where she knew she was sleeping the whole time.

Tuesday was her birthday. She was forty-three, now.

The first thing she did when she got up was check her phone. There was a voicemail from Jesús that had come in at six-fifteen — he was on the early shift at the warehouse this month. The message was short, warm, and ended with *Mama, llámame cuando puedas, te quiero.* There was a text from her sister in Tucson with three balloon emojis and the word *FORTY-THREE!!* in capitals.

She made coffee. She drank it standing at the counter. The screwdriver case was where she had left it. The law firm envelope was where she had left it. Beside it, half hidden under the corner of yesterday's pages, was the small hand-addressed envelope from her mother. She had forgotten it. 

She opened it now. A card with a watercolor of three calla lilies on the front. Inside, in the same thin uphill handwriting: *Para mi hija en su cumpleaños. Que Dios te bendiga y te dé paz. Te quiero, Mamá.* And a folded twenty-dollar bill. Her mother had been putting twenty-dollar bills in birthday cards since Maria was in college and had never stopped.

She stood it open on the table beside the salt shaker and called her mother. Five minutes, in Spanish. Her mother sang the first line of *Las Mañanitas* through the phone and Maria laughed, which surprised her, because she had not expected to laugh. Her mother asked how she was. She said she was fine. Her mother would know she was not fine. But she did not push. They talked about Jesús, about the cousins in Tucson, about the weather, and they hung up after Maria promised to come down for Easter.

She called Jesús. He was on his lunch break. They talked for nine minutes. He asked if she had plans for the evening. She said no. He said he would come by Saturday and they would go to that place she liked on Route 9. She said okay. She did not tell him about the letter. He had enough on his plate.

She did not call her sister back. She would. Later. She sent a thumbs-up emoji, a heart, and *gracias hermana te quiero,* and put the phone face down on the counter.

At ten, she called Daniel Hartmann.

He explained options. She could refuse to sign and force Carlos to seek a court order, which would take six to eight weeks and might or might not be granted. She could sign and consent to a single joint appraisal, which would lock in a value both sides would have to accept. She could sign and commission her own parallel appraisal, which would cost between four and six hundred dollars and would give her an independent number to negotiate against, which Hartmann thought was the strongest option.

"How long does the parallel appraisal take?"

"Two weeks if you call today. Three if you wait."

"And what does it actually do for me?"

"It gives you a number. If their number is higher than yours, you have a counter. You also have a number to compare, whether their number is reasonable. Either way, you stop being the person who has to wait for someone else's information."

She thought about that.

"Mrs. Alvarez?"

"I'm here."

"You don't have to decide today."

"I know."

She thanked him and hung up. She sat at the kitchen table and looked at the back of the electric bill envelope with the three appraiser numbers on it.

She picked up her phone again and made the call. A woman named Patricia answered the phone, which surprised Maria, because she had been expecting voicemail or a computer voice. Patricia listened. Patricia asked questions, all of them practical. Patricia said she could come Thursday morning at nine and the report would be in Maria's inbox the following Wednesday. The fee was four hundred and seventy-five dollars, half on inspection, half on delivery.

Maria said yes. She said it without checking with anyone, without asking for time to think about it, without a single hesitation between the question and the answer. The word came out flat and clean and her own.

She wrote *Patricia Stephenson — Thursday 9 a.m.* on the envelope together with the phone number. She put the envelope on the corner of the counter beside the screwdriver case.

The kitchen was quiet. She did not feel different than she had felt yesterday. She did not feel like anything had been solved. The letter from the law firm was still on the table. Carlos was still in Phoenix doing what Carlos did. The house was still not hers. She had spent four hundred and seventy-five dollars she did not really have on a piece of paper that would not, by itself, change anything.

But she had made the call. Without crying, without asking Jesús, without asking Daniel Hartmann to make it for her. The appraiser was coming Thursday. That was a thing she had done.

She picked up the law firm pages and put them back in the envelope. She put the envelope in the drawer of the kitchen desk where she kept the other paperwork from the divorce. 

She made lunch at noon. A sandwich and an apple. She ate it standing at the counter, looking out the window at the street.

After lunch she poured a fresh cup of coffee and carried it out to the front porch. The air was cold, but the sun was on the steps and there was no wind. She sat in the wicker chair and held the mug in both hands and looked at the empty lot at number six.

Aldric came out of his house a few minutes later with a mug of his own. She raised hers from across the way. He raised his back, then came down his driveway, walked along the sidewalk past the empty lot, and turned up her walk. He climbed her three steps and stopped at the railing.

"Good afternoon, Maria."

"Aldric. Sit, if you want."

"Thank you." He sat in the other wicker chair, the one Jesús used when he came by. He set his mug on the railing and looked out at the street. "Many happy returns of the day."

She did not answer right away. She had not told him. She knew she had not told him. The street did not know. Rosa did not know, because if Rosa had known, Rosa would have brought something over already, and Rosa had not.

"Thank you."

He nodded. He picked up his mug and took a sip. He did not look at her when he said it, and he did not look at her after. That was, she understood, on purpose.

"The Carolina wrens are back early this year," he said. "I heard one in the rhododendron behind your fence this morning."

"Are they?"

"They are. It's a good sign. They tend to come back when the worst of the cold is finished." He took another sip. "It isn't, of course. But they think so."

She almost smiled.

They sat for another minute without saying anything. A car went by at the far end of the street. Somewhere down by the cul-de-sac a dog barked twice and stopped.

"I never thanked you properly for the screwdriver set."

"It wasn't much."

"It was the right thing."

He nodded once, slowly, and looked out at the street. "Good."

That was all he said about it. He did not ask which screw or which hinge or which problem in which room. He drank from his mug and looked at the empty lot at number six.

"The realtor sign blew over again," he said. "Third time this winter."

"I noticed."

"Whoever's listing it has stopped coming out to fix it."

"They've stopped coming out to do anything."

He almost smiled at that. He did not, quite. He set his mug back on the railing and looked at her front steps.

A while passed. The cold was getting into her hands through the ceramic. The sun had moved a few inches along the porch boards.

"How are things with the divorce?" he asked. "If I may."

She had known he might ask. She had not known until that moment what she would say.

She thought about telling him. About the letter. About Thursday. About the four hundred and seventy-five dollars and the woman named Patricia who had answered her own phone. The words were lined up in her mouth like change on a counter. She did not spend them. She did not know exactly why. He would not have asked a follow-up question she could not answer. He would have nodded once, the way he had nodded about the screwdriver, and said *Good,* and that would have been the end of it. 

But she didn't.

The letter was hers. Thursday morning was hers. The four hundred and seventy-five dollars she did not have were hers. And she found, sitting on her own porch beside her neighbor, that she wanted to keep them that way a little longer.

"Not done yet. A few things still to sort out. Property side, mostly."

He nodded, slowly. He did not look at her. "If there's anything that would help."

"Thank you." She said it the way she meant it, which was: heard, registered, not taken up. "I think I've got the next piece."

"Good."

He finished his coffee in two more sips and stood up. "I won't keep you. I hope your day is a good one."

"Thank you, Aldric."

He went down the steps, past the empty lot and into his house.

She stayed on the porch a little longer with her coffee. 

She went inside.

She rinsed her mug and set it in the sink. She picked up the back of the electric bill envelope from the counter and looked again at the name and the time she had written down.

*Patricia Stephenson — Thursday 9 a.m.*

She put the envelope on the refrigerator under a magnet shaped like a strawberry that had been there since Jesús was nine.

In the afternoon she worked on the cardigan. She finished the sleeve increase she had not finished last night. She put the cardigan back on the arm of the couch, washed her face, and changed her shirt.

At five she called her sister back. Her sister sang the rest of *Las Mañanitas* and made her promise to send a picture of herself with a candle in something, which Maria did, after dinner, with a candle stuck in half an avocado because there was nothing else in the kitchen that would hold a candle. Her sister sent back four crying-laughing emojis and a heart.

She went to bed at ten.

She slept through the night.

In the morning she would call Hartmann and tell him to draft the appraisal-consent letter with the parallel-appraisal clause. She would call Patricia and confirm Thursday. She would put the screwdriver case in the closet, or she would not. She had not decided yet about the case.

But the appraiser was coming Thursday. And someone had made the call.
