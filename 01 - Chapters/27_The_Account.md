# Chapter 27: The Account

Monday morning.

Maria found the letter between the electric bill and a Bed Bath coupon. The envelope was thin, white, from First National. She opened it standing at the counter with her coffee still too hot to drink.

*Dear account holder,*

*We are writing to inform you that access to joint account ending in 4417 has been restricted per the request of the co-account holder. Effective immediately, all withdrawal and transfer privileges for the secondary holder have been suspended. Please contact our branch office to discuss resolution options.*

She read it twice. Then a third time. The words didn't change.

Maria set the letter on the counter. She picked up her coffee. It burned her tongue and she didn't notice.

Her own account had some money in it, earned from her crochet business, but not nearly enough to live by. Mortgage was $1,650. Due in nine days. Electric, internet, phone, another $340. Groceries. Gas. 

She sat down at the kitchen table. The chair made the sound it always made, the little creak on the left leg that Jesús had said he'd fix six months ago. Before the separation. Before everything became something she'd have to ask someone else to do.

She called the bank. Fourteen minutes on hold. The woman who answered was sympathetic the way bank employees are. Scripted, warm enough, unable to help. The restriction had been placed by the primary account holder. To lift it, he would need to authorize the change. Or Maria could open a new account and request a transfer of her funds through the legal process.

"How long does that take?"

"That depends on your attorney, ma'am."

Maria hung up. She called her attorney. Voicemail. She left a message. Her voice was steady. She was proud of that, afterward. Steady voice, shaking hands.

She sat at the table for a long time. The coffee got cold, but she didn't make another cup.

At eleven, she walked to the mailbox to check if she'd missed anything. She hadn't. The street was quiet. Two houses down, Aldric's Volvo was in the driveway. His garage door was closed. The study window upstairs was dark.

She went back inside. She opened her laptop and looked at the Etsy dashboard. Fifty-nine dollars in pending orders. A crochet baby blanket someone in Ohio had bought three days ago. She hadn't shipped it yet. She needed to buy the mailer. The mailers cost $3.40 each at the post office.

She closed the laptop.

The attorney called back the next day. He said he'd send a letter. He said these things take time. He said the court could compel access, but the process involved a motion, a hearing date, and a minimum of three weeks.

"Three weeks."

"I'm sorry, Maria. I know it's not what you want to hear."

"What do I do for the mortgage?"

A pause. "Do you have family who could help in the interim?"

She did. Jesús would give her the money in twenty minutes if she asked. Her mother would wire it from Tucson. Her sister would say yes before Maria finished the sentence.

She didn't call any of them.

That afternoon, she was getting the mail when Aldric came out with his recycling bin. He set it at the curb and looked over.

"Everything alright?"

She should have said fine. She said, "Carlos froze the bank account."

Aldric was quiet for a moment. Then he nodded. "I'm sorry. That's a difficult thing to deal with on top of everything else." He set the bin down. "Is your attorney handling it?"

"He says three weeks. Minimum."

"Three weeks is a long time with financial obligations." He said it simply, like stating a fact about the weather. Then he picked up the bin. "Let me know if there's anything I can do." He went back inside.

That was Tuesday.

Wednesday morning, Maria was on her porch with a cup of tea she'd made from a box she'd had since September. The tea was fine. Everything was fine. She was forty-two years old and her ex-husband had locked her out of her own money because he could, because his name was first on the account, because twenty-three years of marriage meant nothing when a man decided to be cruel.

She wasn't crying. She was sitting on her porch drinking tea. Not crying. Not calling her mother or her son or anyone. She was an adult and adults handled things.

Aldric came out of his house at eight-fifteen. He was wearing the cardigan and carrying his coffee in a mug. He walked to the end of his driveway, checked the mailbox, found it empty, and turned back.

He looked at her. Then he went back inside.

A few minutes later, his front door opened again. He came down the driveway carrying a paper bag, went over, and stopped at the bottom of her porch steps.

"Good morning, Maria."

"Morning."

"The Satsumas at the farmer's market this week were exceptional. I bought too many." He held the bag up. "Would you like some?"

"Sure. Thank you."

He set it on the porch railing beside her.

"I hope your week improves," he said. Nothing else. He went back to his house.

Maria opened the bag. Six Satsumas, perfectly ripe. Underneath them, folded once, was a piece of paper. She unfolded it.

It was a handwritten list. Three phone numbers. The first was labeled *Legal Aid — Montgomery County (free consultation, no retainer)*. The second: *Judge Patricia Webb's clerk (scheduling, family court motions)*. The third: *First National branch manager, direct line (Jennifer Okafor)*.

At the bottom, in the same neat handwriting: *The branch manager has discretion to issue a temporary access waiver for documented hardship.*

Maria stared at the paper. She stared at it for a long time.

She didn't ask how he'd gotten a branch manager's direct number or a judge's clerk's name or the existence of a form she'd never heard of in fourteen minutes on hold with the bank's general line.

She went inside. She called Jennifer Okafor. The woman answered on the second ring. Maria explained. Jennifer said yes, she could come in today. Yes, there is a way. Yes, it would take about half an hour.

Maria was at the bank by ten. By eleven, she had limited temporary access to the bank account again. Enough for rent and utilities. The restriction was still technically in place, but the override gave her breathing room. Thirty days.

She drove home. The Satsumas were on the passenger seat. She ate one in the driveway, standing beside her car, juice running down her wrist.

Thursday and Friday passed. She shipped the baby blanket. She took two new orders. She called her attorney again and told him about the override. He said that was good, that bought time, that he'd file the motion by end of the day.

Saturday. Maria was in her kitchen when the phone rang. Her attorney.

"Maria. I have good news." He sounded surprised. Attorneys didn't usually sound surprised. "Carlos's counsel contacted me this morning. Your ex has agreed to restore full access to the joint account effective Monday. Additionally, he's signed an interim support agreement. He continues the mortgage payments directly, plus five hundred a month to you for living expenses. Backdated to November first."

Maria sat down. "He did that?"

"He did. Voluntarily. No hearing required."

"Did he say why?"

"His attorney said he'd 'reconsidered his position.' That's a direct quote."

Maria thanked him. She hung up. She sat in her kitchen and looked at the wall.

Carlos wasn't the type to reconsider anything. He just didn't. Carlos decided, and then the world arranged itself around his decision, and if it didn't, he pushed harder until it did. That was who he was. That was who he had always been.

Someone had changed his mind. Someone had made reconsidering seem like the better option.

She thought about the paper. The handwriting. The three phone numbers, and the Satsumas on top, like a gift wrapping something that wasn't a gift. 

She stayed in her kitchen. She breathed. And she understood, for the first time clearly, that whatever Aldric Venn was, whatever he had been before Maple Lane, whatever he did on those mornings when he left early and came back after dark, he had done this for her. Quietly. Without asking. Without explaining.

The five hundred would arrive on Monday. The mortgage would stay paid. Carlos had reconsidered his position.

Maria peeled a Satsuma. Her hands were steady. She ate it slowly, standing at the counter, looking out the window at the street where nothing ever happened.
