# Chapter 18: Marcus Turns Six

The backyard looked like a piñata had exploded in a paint factory. Rosa had been decorating since seven in the morning, and Dave had been told three times to stop touching the streamers. They were fine. They were more than fine. They were aggressively festive.

Marcus was still in his pajamas at nine-thirty, running laps around the kitchen table. He'd been up since five. Dave had heard him whispering to himself in bed: "Today. Today. Today." Like a countdown reaching zero.

"Shoes," Rosa said, catching Marcus mid-lap. "Real shoes. Not the dinosaur ones."

"The dinosaur ones *are* real shoes."

"They have holes."

"Those are for breathing."

Dave poured his second coffee. Sixteen kids were coming. Sixteen. He'd asked Rosa twice now, if that was the whole class or just the ones she liked. She'd said both. He didn't know what that meant.

By eleven, the backyard was full. Kids on the grass, parents along the fence with paper plates. The Hendersons from two streets over. Emma's mom. A dad Dave didn't recognize who kept checking his phone. The bounce house was inflated but listing slightly to the left, which Marcus had declared "better, because then you slide."

Dave was manning the grill. Hotdogs, burgers, one sad veggie patty for Emma's mom. Rosa was everywhere at once, which was her natural state at parties. Marcus was a blur of limbs and noise in the bounce house.

Gerald was at his window. Dave could see him from the grill, diagonally across the street, standing behind the curtain with what was probably coffee. Or binoculars. Possibly both. With Gerald, you never knew.

The doorbell rang at eleven-fifteen. Dave wiped his hands on his apron and went through the house.

Aldric was on the porch. He was wearing the same cardigan above a gray shirt — he must really like that thing — together with dark slacks, and leather shoes that had no business being at a children's birthday party. He held a wrapped box under one arm and a bottle of something in the other hand.

"Happy birthday to the young man," he said. "I hope I'm not early."

"Right on time," Dave said. "Come on through."

Aldric followed him through the living room. He paused at the kitchen counter and set the bottle down. Dave glanced at the label. Sparkling cider. Non-alcoholic. Of course. Because there were children.

"The backyard is the staging area," Dave said. "Fair warning: it's loud."

"I appreciate the warning." Aldric smiled. He tucked the gift under his arm and walked out the back door like a man entering a diplomatic reception.

Rosa spotted him immediately. Dave watched her cross the yard, wipe her hands on her jeans, and greet him with the warmth she gave everyone who showed up for her kids.

"Aldric! You made it."

"I wouldn't miss it. Where is the birthday boy?"

Rosa pointed at the bounce house. A small body launched itself off the inflatable wall, landed on another child, and rolled sideways laughing.

"The one committing assault," Rosa said.

"Ah." Aldric watched Marcus with something close to genuine amusement. "Spirited."

Dave flipped a burger. He watched Aldric navigate the backyard with the ease of someone who'd studied a seating chart beforehand. He greeted Emma's mom by name. He shook hands with the phone-checking dad, who put his phone away afterward. He complimented Sandra on her earrings when she arrived with a tray of brownies, and Sandra did the thing where she touched her earrings like she'd forgotten she was wearing them.

None of it was wrong. None of it was off. It was just very, very smooth.

Marcus emerged from the bounce house at some point, red-faced and grass-stained, and spotted Aldric immediately.

"Mr. Venn!" He ran across the yard. No hesitation. Straight at him like a heat-seeking missile in Spider-Man socks.

"Marcus." Aldric crouched down to eye level. "Happy birthday. Six is a significant number."

"Why?"

"It's the first perfect number. The sum of its factors equals itself. One plus two plus three."

Marcus stared at him. "Cool," he said, in the tone of a child who understood nothing but respected the delivery.

Rosa appeared at Dave's elbow. She was holding a juice box and watching the same thing he was watching.

"Kids like him," she said.

"At least one does." He nodded at Marcus, who was still staring at Aldric with a mixture of awe and confusion. 

"He's good with them."

Dave shrugged. "He's good with everyone."

Marcus had grabbed Aldric's hand and was dragging him toward the bounce house with the determination of a tugboat. Aldric allowed himself to be dragged. He did not remove his leather shoes. He did not enter the bounce house. But he stood at the entrance and watched Marcus perform what was apparently a gymnastics routine, and he applauded at the end with both hands.

"Good form," he said. "Particularly the dismount."

Marcus beamed.

The presents came after cake. Rosa organized them on the picnic table, Marcus tearing through wrapping paper like a machine. Lego sets, action figures, a soccer ball, a book about volcanoes from Emma. Marcus said thank you to each one, mostly because Rosa was standing behind him with *the look*.

Aldric's gift was last. Not because Rosa had planned it that way, but because Marcus had set it aside. Dave noticed that. The kid had grabbed it, looked at it, and put it at the end of the line. Saving it.

Marcus tore the paper. Inside was a box. Not big. Not small. He opened it.

Dave couldn't see what it was from the grill. But he saw Marcus go still. Not disappointed-still. The other kind. The kind where a six-year-old's brain stops because it's processing something too good.

"Mom." Marcus held it up. "Mom. *Mom.*"

Rosa took it from him. Dave walked over.

It was a Lego set. A fire station. The fire station. The one Marcus had described at the dinner three weeks ago, sitting next to Aldric, using both hands to show how big it was. The one that was "probably a hundred dollars," which to Marcus meant impossible.

It wasn't a hundred dollars. Dave checked later. It was sixty-seven. But it was discontinued. You couldn't just walk into Target and buy it. You'd have to look. You'd have to remember a five-year-old's description of something he'd seen once at a friend's house and correctly identify the specific set from a hand gesture and the detail about the dog.

Marcus was already hugging the box. Not Aldric. The box. But he looked up at Aldric with an expression that Dave recognized from his own childhood: the face of a kid who feels *seen*.

"Thank you, Mr. Venn."

"You're very welcome, Marcus."

Rosa was looking at Aldric. Dave knew that look. It was the look she'd had at the dinner, when Aldric brought wine that matched a meal nobody had told him about. The look that said: *How do you know these things?*

But she didn't ask. She just said, "That was very thoughtful, Aldric."

"He described it quite vividly," Aldric said. "I merely listened."

Dave went back to the grill. The coals were dying, but there was nothing left to cook. He stood there anyway, tongs in hand, watching Aldric talk to other parents. They laughed at something he said. The phone-checking dad was telling a story, and Aldric was listening with his full attention, the kind of listening that made people talk more.

Rosa came up beside him.

"The other parents love him," she said.

"Everybody loves him."

"Emma's mom asked if he was single."

Dave looked at her. "What did you say?"

"I said I didn't know." Rosa picked up a paper plate, folded it in half. "Do we know?"

"We don't know anything about him," Dave said. It came out lighter than it was. A joke, almost. But Rosa didn't laugh.

She was looking at Aldric across the yard. He was helping Marcus open the Lego box, carefully separating the numbered bags, explaining something about the instruction booklet. Marcus was nodding like he was receiving military briefing.

Dave thought about Tuesday. The school. Lina coming home and saying Mr. Venn had made Aiden's dad "get small." He thought about how that was the same man now kneeling on their grass, sorting Lego bags for a six-year-old.

The same man.

"He's good with kids," Rosa said.

"Yeah." Dave put down the tongs. "He is."

The party wound down by three. Kids were picked up, bounce house deflated, trash bags filled. Marcus was on the living room floor with the fire station Lego, surrounded by bags he wasn't allowed to open yet because Rosa said one bag per day. Marcus had accepted this with unusual grace.

Aldric left with the other parents. He shook Dave's hand at the door, thanked Rosa for the invitation, and told Marcus he expected a full tour of the completed fire station when it was done.

"I'll show you every room," Marcus said.

"I look forward to it."

Dave closed the door. Rosa was already in the kitchen, starting the dishwasher. Lina was on the couch reading, having survived the party by staying in her room for most of it.

"Good party," Dave said.

"Good party," Rosa agreed.

Marcus made a siren noise from the living room floor. The first bag was open. The foundation pieces were being laid.

Dave looked out the front window. Aldric was walking next door to his house, hands in his pockets, same posture as always. Straight, unhurried, precise.

Gerald's curtain moved across the street.

Dave smiled.
