# Chapter 10: The Visitor

The driveway had needed cleaning since June. Rosa had mentioned it twice. Now it was late August, the kids were in the backyard, and Dave was attacking the concrete with a rental pressure washer that sounded like a jet engine. The strip work was satisfying. Dark gray turning to pale gray, years of dirt lifting in clean lines.

He was halfway through the moss stain by the garage when a silver sedan pulled up across the street.

Dave killed the pressure washer and pulled the remaining earbud out. The silence rushed back in: sprinkler at the Hoopers, a lawnmower two streets over, Lina telling Marcus to stop throwing grass at her. Normal Saturday sounds. Late August heat pressing down on everything, the smell of wet concrete and the Chens' jasmine going slightly sour in the warmth.

The car sat at the curb in front of Aldric's house for a full minute before the door opened. Current model year and not a single distinguishing feature. No bumper stickers, no dealer plate frame and no sun shade in the windshield. The kind of car you'd forget thirty seconds after seeing it. Dave only noticed it because it was parked exactly where Rosa's mom always parked, and his brain had done the comparison before he could stop it.

A man got out. Forty-five, maybe fifty. Gray suit, no tie, white shirt open at the collar. Sunglasses, which he took off and folded into his breast pocket with a motion that looked practiced. He straightened his jacket and looked at Aldric's house. Not like someone visiting a friend. Like someone arriving at an appointment.

Dave wiped his hands on his shorts. He wasn't staring. He was taking a break. Hydrating. Happening to face that direction while he checked his phone, which he now realized was in his back pocket and would be covered in concrete dust.

The man walked up Aldric's path. Good shoes. Dave noticed that because they were too good for a Saturday afternoon on a street where the fanciest footwear was Gerald's church loafers. These were airport-lounge shoes. The man's posture was straight but not stiff, his pace measured. Everything about him looked unremarkable, and the fact that Dave noticed that meant something about it wasn't quite working.

He knocked. Three knocks. Even, deliberate spacing. Dave's brain cycled through options: insurance adjuster? Financial advisor? Real estate? None of them stuck. Insurance guys had clipboards and apologetic posture. Financial advisors smiled too much. Real estate agents wore louder suits and drove louder cars. This man had nothing on his face that gave him a category.

Aldric opened the door.

Dave couldn't hear what was said. But he saw the posture, and posture was something Dave understood from fifteen years of managing construction crews. Aldric stood in the doorway for a beat longer than he stood for anyone else on this street. His shoulders didn't drop. His weight didn't shift forward. He didn't lean on the frame or open the door wider or do any of the things a person does when someone they're happy to see shows up.

He stepped aside. The man went in. The door closed.

Dave picked up the pressure washer wand. He didn't turn it on. The moss stain was still there, half-dissolved, the green edge feathering into clean concrete. Behind him, Marcus came around the side of the house.

"Dad, can I have a popsicle?"

"Ask your mom."

"She said to ask you."

"Then yes. One. Not the blue ones, those stain everything."

Marcus ran back inside. The screen door slapped shut behind him. Dave looked at the moss stain. Then at Aldric's closed front door.

Something about the man's walk. Something about Aldric not smiling.

Aldric smiled at everyone. At Gerald with his clipboard, at Kevin the mailman, at the Parkers' kid who never made eye contact. He smiled at people knocking on his door like other people breathed. It was reflexive. Always there. In three months, Dave had never seen it not be there.

He turned the pressure washer back on. The noise filled the street. He worked the moss stain until it was gone, then worked the concrete around it until the whole section matched. Marcus came back out with a red popsicle and sat on the porch steps watching, which meant Lina had claimed the backyard. Twenty minutes. Twenty-five. Dave kept glancing at number four between passes.

The door opened. He killed the washer.

The man came out first. He was putting his sunglasses back on. Aldric followed him to the porch. They stood there for a moment. Two men in the afternoon light. Aldric said something. The man nodded once. No handshake. No smile. The man turned, walked back to the car, got in, started it and pulled away without looking back.

Dave had shaken hands with every contractor, inspector and supplier he'd ever done business with. You shook hands. It was what you did. The absence of it sat wrong with him, the way a missed step on a staircase sits wrong even after you've caught yourself.

Aldric stood on the porch. His hands were at his sides. He was looking at the street, but not at anything on it. Dave had seen that look once before, on his college roommate the morning after his father called about the diagnosis. Eyes pointing outward, at nothing on the street.

Then Aldric turned his head. Saw Dave standing there with the pressure washer wand dripping onto his shoes. The smile came back. Instant, full, warm.

"Dave. Good afternoon. The driveway looks excellent."

"Thanks." Dave coiled the hose, doing something with his hands because standing still felt wrong. "Everything OK?"

"Perfectly fine. Old acquaintance." Aldric lifted one hand. A wave and a closing of a subject, both at once. "Enjoy your Saturday."

He went inside. The door closed. The porch was empty.

Dave finished the driveway. He put the pressure washer back in the garage, wiped it down and wrapped the cord how the rental place wanted. He found the lost earbud in the grass by the spigot, went inside and showered. He came downstairs to the smell of Rosa's sofrito and the sound of Marcus explaining why his dinosaur had seven legs.

"More legs, more speed," Marcus said to his drawing.

"That's not how it works," Lina said from the couch without looking up from her phone.

"How would you know? You've never seen a dinosaur."

"Neither have you."

"I've seen bones."

Dave kissed Rosa's temple on his way to the fridge. She was stirring the pot one-handed, her other arm braced against the counter, which meant her back was bothering her. He made a mental note and asked with his hand on her hip, quiet: "You OK?"

"Fine. Just stood too long at the market." She tilted her head toward the stove. "Taste."

He took the wooden spoon. Tomato, garlic and cumin. Perfect. "Good."

"I know." She smiled. "Beer's in the door." Then, without turning: "Why did you let Marcus have a popsicle? Dinner's in twenty minutes."

"He said you told him to ask me."

Rosa turned around. "I told him no."

"That little— " 

He got the beer, opened it and leaned against the counter next to her.

"Aldric had a visitor today," he said.

Rosa looked up. "Oh? Who?"

"Don't know. Guy in a suit. Silver rental car." He took a sip. "Something felt off about it."

"Off how?"

Dave peeled at the label on the bottle, trying to find the word. "Smooth," he said. "Too smooth. Like everything about the guy was deliberate."

Rosa's spoon paused in the pot. "Isn't everything about Aldric deliberate?"

"Yeah, but—" He took another sip. "Aldric does it warm. This guy did it cold." He looked at the label and kept peeling. "Like someone who practices looking normal. But hasn't gotten to the part where it stops looking like practice."

Rosa went back to stirring. The sofrito bubbled. Marcus had moved on to giving his dinosaur wings. Lina had disappeared upstairs. Outside, the evening was settling in. Dave could hear someone's wind chimes and the faint bass of the Greens' daughter's music three houses down across the street.

"He's not on the porch," Rosa said.

Dave turned and looked through the kitchen window. Aldric's porch. The lamp was on, same as every evening. But the chair was empty. At seven-thirty on a Saturday evening in August, when the heat finally broke and the street smelled like barbecue and cut grass, Aldric wasn't out there.

In three months, Dave had never seen that chair empty on a warm evening. Not once.

"No," he said. "He's not."

Rosa didn't say anything else. She turned the heat down under the sofrito. Marcus asked if dinosaurs could fly and answered his own question before anyone else could.

Dave looked at the lit, empty porch one more time. Then he decided not to think about it.
