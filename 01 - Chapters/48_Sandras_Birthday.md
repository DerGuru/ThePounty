# Chapter 48: Sandra's Birthday

*Thursday, the 19th of June 2025*

Sandra had decided on the garden in May, which gave her a month to worry about the weather, and she had used every day of it. She had a backup plan involving the garage and a rented canopy, and a backup to the backup that was simply the living room with the good chairs brought in from the dining room. None of it had been necessary. The nineteenth came up warm and bright and almost rude about it, the kind of June morning that made a person who had planned for rain feel faintly cheated.

She was sixty-two. She had said the number out loud to the bathroom mirror at seven that morning, as she did every year, to take the sting out of it before anyone else could. Sixty-two. It did not sound like her. It sounded like someone's aunt.

By four o'clock the backyard was ready. The gravel paths were swept. The raised bed was full and the ornamental grass had filled in over its first spring exactly as the young man had promised it would, the autumn before, the grass will fill in by June. It had, just as he'd said. Sandra still thought of him when she looked at it, the contractor who had come back, though she had never learned his real name and Aldric had never offered it. She had folding tables with cloths on them and a cooler of drinks and a cake in the kitchen under a glass dome, lemon with cream cheese frosting, which she had made herself because she did not trust the bakery on Route 9 to do it without putting raspberry in places raspberry did not belong.

"You've done too much," Gerald said. He was standing at the back door with a beer he had not opened, watching her straighten a tablecloth that did not need straightening.

"It's a party. There's no such thing as too much."

"There's a documented such thing. You're looking at it."

"You can carry the chairs out. That's your job. Four from the dining room."

He went to carry the chairs. He had been better the last few weeks, she thought, or at least quieter about whatever it was, which with Gerald amounted to the same thing. He still came down in the mornings looking like a man who had spent the night arguing with someone in another room. She had stopped asking. Forty years had taught her that Gerald told you things when the things were ready to be told and not one minute before, and that the asking only made the telling take longer.

The neighbors came at five, more or less together, as they always did. Rosa first, with Marcus running ahead of her across the grass and Lina behind carrying a dish her mother had clearly made her hold the entire walk over. Dave with a cooler he did not need to bring because Sandra had her own, but men brought coolers, it was a thing they did, and she let him put it next to hers and say something approving about the ice situation. Maria came with flowers from her own garden, sweet peas in a jar, and Sandra put them on the cake table and meant it when she said they were perfect. Tamara Flynn came with a card the whole cul-de-sac had signed, which must have taken her three days of door-knocking, and Sandra got unexpectedly close to crying over it and covered the feeling by reading every signature out loud.

Ms. Yuen came last, walking, carrying nothing, which from Ms. Yuen was its own kind of gift, the gift of having been asked and having come. She sat in the good chair Sandra had set in the shade, accepted a glass of lemonade, and watched the children the way she watched everything, while the party arranged itself around her like furniture around a lamp.

Aldric came at five-fifteen, through the side gate, because the side gate was the neighborly way in and Aldric did not knock at front doors when a side gate was available. He was wearing a linen shirt the color of weak tea, sleeves turned back, and he was carrying something wrapped in burlap at the base, held in both hands, level, the way you carry a thing you do not want to jostle.

"Sandra. Many happy returns of the day."

"Aldric. You made it." She had worried, a little, that he would not come, the same worry she had carried to the HOA meeting almost a year ago, before she had learned that Aldric did exactly what he said he would do and nothing he didn't. She had invited him on a Tuesday, on the sidewalk, and he had said he would be honored, and here he was being honored, on time, with his sleeves turned back.

"I brought you something for the garden. I hope that's not presumptuous. A garden one has watched recover is a difficult thing to buy a gift for. One doesn't want to crowd it."

"You didn't have to bring anything."

"No," he agreed, pleasantly, and held it out.

She took it. It was heavier than it looked, a nursery pot inside the burlap, and rising out of it a rose, not a cut rose, a whole young plant, three canes and a scatter of dark leaves and one bud just beginning to break at the tip, showing the first thread of color. The color was a deep, old pink, almost red where the petals folded in on themselves.

Sandra looked at it for a moment without understanding. Then she understood, and the understanding went through her like cold water.

"This is—" She stopped. She knew this rose. She had not seen it since last fall. It had grown along the south fence, six bushes of it, planted by the people who had owned the house before the people who had owned it before her, an old variety nobody sold anymore because it only bloomed once and the once was in June. Her mother would have called it a cabbage rose and been wrong. Sandra had spent twenty years not knowing its name and loving it anyway, and then a man named Kyle Brewer who was not named Kyle Brewer had torn it out in an afternoon and left the bed as dirt, and the garden had come back better than before with marigolds, sage, and grass, and not one person in the world had known that the better garden was missing the only thing in the old one she had truly grieved.

She had never said so. Not to Gerald. Not to anyone. There had not seemed to be a way to say it that didn't sound ungrateful, with the gravel paths so level and the cedar bed so square.

"How did you—" Sandra tried again and gave up on the question, because the question had no answer she could imagine, and because Aldric was watching her with that pleasant, attentive patience that gave nothing back.

"It's a Rose de Resht," he said, as though that were an explanation. "Slow to find. They're not much grown now. I'm told it wants the south side, against a fence if you have one." He paused. "I believe you have one."

"I had six of them," Sandra said. Her voice did not come out as she wanted it to. "Along the back. Before."

"I know," Aldric said.

That was all. *I know.* He did not say how he knew. He did not say that he had stood over a bed of turned dirt in her ruined garden last October and read there, in whatever it was he read things in, the ghost of what had grown. He did not say anything at all that would let her hold it and turn it over and understand it. He only stood there holding the patience the way other men held a hat, and let her have the rose.

"Thank you," Sandra said. It was not enough. She knew it was not enough. It was the same two words the contractor had asked her to carry across the street, *tell him you're happy*, and they had not been enough then either, and they were all anyone ever had.

"You're very welcome." He inclined his head, the small bow that was not a bow. "Don't plant it today. Let it sit in the shade and drink. Tomorrow evening, when it's cool. It'll want a good soaking and then to be left alone." He smiled. "Rather like the rest of us, on a birthday."

And that was the moment the party took him back, because Marcus had appeared at Aldric's elbow to report something urgent about a beetle, and Aldric crouched to beetle height with the seriousness the news deserved, and the strangeness folded itself away as it always did around him, into something that looked, if you were not paying attention, like an ordinary man being kind at an ordinary party.

Sandra carried the rose to the shade herself and set it by Ms. Yuen's chair, where she could see it.

"That's the Resht," Ms. Yuen said, not a question.

"You know it?"

"My mother grew it. In another country." Ms. Yuen looked at the bud for a while. "He found you a good one. That's a hard rose to find now." She did not say anything about how he had known to look. She lifted her lemonade, and that was the end of what she had to say, which Sandra was learning to recognize as the shape Ms. Yuen's sentences came in.

They ate. The cake was right, no raspberry anywhere near it, and Marcus got frosting on a part of his face that frosting had no business reaching, and Lina read Sandra's card aloud in a voice doing its best to sound grown, and the afternoon slid down toward evening as good afternoons did. Sandra moved through all of it as she did every gathering she hosted, a small weather front with words for everybody, but she found her eye going back to the rose in the shade, and each time it did something in her chest tightened and let go.

It was a little before seven when she noticed Gerald.

He was sitting at the end of the second table with his beer, which he had finally opened, and he was not talking to anyone. That was not unusual. Gerald at a party was a man waiting politely for the party to be over. But he was watching Aldric. He was watching Aldric as she had seen him watch the weather radar before a storm he was sure was coming, that fixed, unhappy attention, the attention of a man checking a figure he already knows is wrong.

Aldric was across the garden with Dave, saying something that made him laugh, the loud laugh, the real one. He had a glass of wine he was not drinking. He looked like a neighbor at a neighbor's party. He looked like the most ordinary thing in the world.

Sandra came and sat down next to her husband. She bumped his shoulder with hers.

"You're brooding at my party."

"I'm not brooding. I'm sitting."

"You're brooding-sitting. I know the difference. I've had forty years of field research." She followed his eyes across the garden to Aldric and back. "He brought me my roses, Gerald. The old ones. From the back fence. The ones that man pulled out." She heard her own voice go unsteady again and let it. "Nobody knew about those roses. I never said. And he just— he found them. He found my roses."

Gerald was quiet for a moment. She watched him take that in, turn it over, set it down somewhere with all the other things he turned over and set down and would not show her.

"Yeah," he said finally. "He's good at finding things."

It was not the warm thing she wanted him to say. But there was something underneath it, something heavy and tired and almost like grief, that she did not understand and did not, on her birthday, with her roses breathing in the shade and her neighbors loud and golden in the last of the light, choose to chase.

She leaned her head on his shoulder instead. After a moment his hand came up and patted her knee, twice, the old signal, the one that had meant *I'm here* across forty years of rooms.

Across the garden Aldric was crouched down again, showing Marcus how to hold a beetle so the beetle stayed calm. The boy's whole face was open with the wonder of it. Sandra watched the man who had found her roses teach her neighbor's child to be gentle with something small, and she thought, not for the first time and with no idea how right she was, that there was a great deal about Aldric Venn that none of them would ever know, and that on the whole the street was better for having him in it.

The sun went down behind the Hoopers' good cedar bed. Somebody turned on the string lights Sandra had spent an hour hanging. The party went on into the warm dark, as the best ones did, and Gerald sat through all of it with his hand on her knee and his eyes on the man across the garden, and said nothing at all.
