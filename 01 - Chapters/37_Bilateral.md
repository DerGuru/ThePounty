# Chapter 37: Bilateral

Thursday afternoon. Kim was on Route 9 northbound when dispatch came on with a welfare check. A 911 hang-up from a residence on Birch Street, no answer on the callback. The address was familiar from the file — two prior domestic disturbance calls, eighteen months apart, both withdrawn by the wife. She put on her flashers and turned at the next light.

The house was a one-story with brown trim and a chain-link fence set back from the street. The driveway held a blue pickup with a magnetic sign on the tailgate that said Lasko Plumbing & Heating. The front walk was clear. The blinds in the front room were down. The porch light was on in the middle of the afternoon.

She rang the bell. She waited. She rang again.

"Greenfield Police. Welfare check. Please come to the door."

Nothing. No movement behind the blinds. A television was on somewhere inside, low, a sports announcer talking through what sounded like a basketball game.

She tried the door. It was unlocked.

A door unlocked in an empty house in the middle of the afternoon was not the worst thing she had ever found. But unlocked while someone was inside and not answering was a thing she had learned to read. She drew her weapon and held it at low ready. She announced herself again at the threshold and stepped in.

He was in the kitchen on his back. The television was in the living room and she could hear the game still going. He was a heavy man, blond, awake, with both hands resting on his stomach in a way that no person ever rested their hands on their stomach. His face was untouched. His shirt was not torn. There was no blood on him that she could see. The hands were the wrong shape.

She holstered. She knelt.

"Sir. I'm Officer Kim, Greenfield Police. Can you hear me?"

"Yes."

"Are you injured anywhere besides your hands?"

A small pause. "No."

"What's your name?"

"Brian Lasko."

"Brian, I'm calling EMS now. Don't move. Can you tell me what happened?"

"I fell."

She looked at his hands. Then at the kitchen floor — clean linoleum, no scuff, no overturned chair, no thing to fall onto. Then back at his hands.

"All right," she said. She keyed her shoulder mic.

"Dispatch, twelve."

"Go ahead, twelve."

"Welfare check at the Birch Street address is a medical. Adult male, conscious, bilateral hand injuries, no other visible trauma. Need EMS code two. No suspect on scene. I'll be securing the residence."

"Copy, twelve. EMS rolling. Do you need backup?"

She looked at Lasko on the floor. At his face. At the clean linoleum around him.

"Negative on backup. Send a second unit when one's free. I want photos."

"Copy. Second unit when available."

She let go of the mic.

The ambulance came. The paramedics were an older man and a younger woman. They checked vitals, asked Lasko the same questions she had. He gave the same answers. They stabilized the hands with quick splints and got a line in. The older man asked him if he could sit up. He could. He asked him if he could stand. He could. They walked him out between them, slow, and the older paramedic looked at Kim on the way past and did not say anything.

She was alone in the house with the television still on when a gray Corolla pulled into the driveway.

Nora Lasko got out. She was thirty-two, dark brown hair pulled back, gray sweater. She looked at the cruiser. She looked at the ambulance, which was just pulling away from the curb with the lights on but no siren. Then she looked at the front door, where Kim was standing.

She walked up the front walk without hurrying. She did not run.

"Mrs. Lasko?"

"Yes."

"I'm Officer Kim, Greenfield Police. Your husband has been taken to Mercy Regional. He has injuries to both hands. He is stable. He was conscious when EMS took him."

Nora did not say anything. She looked at the door behind Kim, then at the door.

"Can I go in?"

"Yes. I'd like to ask you some questions inside, if that's all right."

"Of course."

They went in. Nora stopped in the kitchen. There was a small dark spot, possibly coffee, near the leg of the table.

Kim said: "Was the door locked when you left the house this afternoon?"

"Yes."

"What time did you leave?"

"A little before two. I picked up Lily from school at two and dropped her at the community center on Pine. She has a program there on Tuesdays and Thursdays."

"What time are you due to pick her up?"

"Four-thirty. They close at five."

Kim looked at the clock on the wall. It was three-forty-five.

"Mrs. Lasko, do you know of anyone who would have reason to harm your husband?"

A pause. "No."

"Does your husband owe anyone money?"

"I don't know."

"Has anyone come to the house this week who shouldn't have?"

"No."

"Has your husband been afraid of anyone?"

A longer pause. "No."

Kim watched her face. Nora was not crying. She was not still. She was somewhere in between, in a place Kim had seen before in women in kitchens and in others — the place where the body has not decided which way the news goes.

"Mrs. Lasko, I want you to go get your daughter. Don't be late. I'll stay here and finish what I need to finish. Do you have a key, that I can use to lock the door when I leave. If anything in the house looks out of place to you tomorrow, you can call me." She wrote a number on the back of a card and gave it to Nora.

Nora took the card. She held it for a moment without looking at it. Then she said: "Is he — am I supposed to go to the hospital?"

"He's at Mercy. You can go anytime. He's not under arrest. He's a victim."

The word *victim* landed somewhere on Nora's face and did not move.

"Okay," she said.

She picked up her keys from the counter, put the card in her coat pocket without folding it, and went out to the Corolla.

She sat in it for a while before she started it. Her head was against the driver's window. She did not cry.

Kim watched the Corolla pull out and turn east on Birch. East was the way to the community center. Not the way to the hospital.

She did not write that observation down.

She finished her walk-through of the house. No signs of forced entry. No signs of struggle in any room, and the kitchen showed no signs of struggle either, which was the part that did not fit. She photographed the kitchen, the front door, the back door, the locks. She locked the front door behind her when she left.

She keyed her mic at the cruiser.

"Dispatch, twelve."

"Go ahead, twelve."

"Scene at Birch Street is clear. I have the photos. Cancel the second unit."

"Copy, twelve. Second unit canceled."

She drove home. She did not sleep much.

Friday morning. Kim was at the second light on Route 9 when the dispatch confirmation came through. Brian Lasko, admitted Thursday afternoon, stable, awake, declining to make a statement. She had requested the follow-up herself before she left her shift.

In the parking lot of the regional hospital she sat with the engine off and her coffee going cold. Then she went in.

She read the chart at the nurse's station before she went to the room. The orthopedic notes were detailed.

*Bilateral fractures, both scaphoid bones. Bilateral hamate fractures. Right hand: third, fourth, fifth metacarpals at the neck. Left hand: fifth metacarpal at the neck. Multiple distal phalanges. No fractures proximal to the wrist. No skull, facial, rib, or long-bone trauma. No defensive wounds on forearms.*

A man in a bar fight broke his face, his ribs, and his knuckles. A man who fell from a height broke his wrists trying to catch himself, and the breaks were dirty, comminuted, with the rest of the arm taking the rest of the energy. A man who had been hit by a car broke long bones.

A man whose hands had been broken, only his hands, every bone in both hands that mattered for grip, and nothing else anywhere on his body, had had his hands broken on purpose, one by one, by someone who knew which bones were which.

A plumber's hands. Kim had read the magnetic sign on the truck.

She closed the chart and went into the room.

Brian Lasko was on the bed bandaged from both elbows down. Both wrists in casts. Three fingers on the right hand splinted. One on the left. The left thumb was wrapped separately. His face was untouched. His eyes were wet but not from pain. The pain was managed. The eyes were something else.

"Mr. Lasko. We didn't finish yesterday."

"I don't want to make a statement."

"All right." Kim sat in the chair. She did not open her notebook. "Can you tell me anything about what happened?"

"I told you yesterday. I fell."

"All right."

She waited.

"Did you see who did it?"

"No."

"Was anyone else home when it happened?"

A pause. "No."

That was the answer that mattered. A man does not fall and break only both hands in his own kitchen with no one else there. Kim let the answer sit.

"Mr. Lasko." She kept her voice flat. "I'm not going to push. You don't have to talk to me. But I'm going to tell you what I see, because what I see is going to be in my report whether you talk to me or not."

He looked at the ceiling.

"What I see is a person who was held down by one person while a second person did the breaking. There aren't any defensive marks on your forearms, so you weren't fighting back when it happened. The breaks are on both hands in the same places, which means whoever did it knew how to do it. Your face is fine, your ribs are fine, your legs are fine. Whoever did this had a very specific reason to leave the rest of you alone."

She let that sit.

"You're going to heal," she said. "The orthopedic surgeon says nine to twelve weeks for full function. You will not be able to grip anything for that time. You will not be able to make a fist."

He did not look at her.

"If you want to tell me anything, even off the record, I'll listen. If you don't, that's your right. I'll come back next week."

She stood up. She was at the door when he said it.

"He asked me."

She turned.

"What?"

"The one who did it. Before. He asked me." His voice was small. "If I will to stop. He said it like he meant it."

"Stop what?"

He did not answer.

"Mr. Lasko. Stop what?"

He closed his eyes. A tear went sideways into his hair.

Kim waited. He did not open his eyes again. After a minute she stepped out of the room and closed the door behind her.

She stood in the hallway for a long time.

She did not drive past Maple Lane on the way back to the station. She drove the direct route.

She wrote her report at her desk. *Welfare check Thursday 14:54 at residence on Birch Street following 911 hang-up. Subject Brian Lasko (34) found on kitchen floor with significant bilateral injuries to both hands, no other trauma. Subject conscious, refused to identify assailant, stated only that he had fallen. EMS transported to Mercy Regional. Wife (Nora Lasko, 32) returned to residence at approximately 15:30, declined to identify possible assailants or describe any precipitating events. No signs of forced entry. Door reported locked at time of departure but unlocked at officer's arrival. Follow-up statement attempted Friday 08:40 — victim again declined formal statement. Victim made one statement of note, off the record, to the effect that he had been offered an opportunity to stop a behavior prior to the assault. Victim did not specify the behavior. No suspect identified. Two prior domestic disturbance calls at this address, both withdrawn. Recommend follow-up contact with both parties in seven to ten days.*

She read it back. It was a clean report. It said exactly what had happened, and exactly nothing about what she thought.

She filed it.

At the end of her shift she signed out the cruiser and drove home. The route home went past Maple Lane, because the route home had gone past Maple Lane for four months now.

It was six-ten and the sky was already going dark. Aldric Venn was on his porch in the cardigan, a mug in his hand, looking at the street.

He saw the cruiser and raised his hand.

Kim raised hers back.

A man had been found on a kitchen floor with hands that would not grip for three months. He had used those hands on his wife for at least eighteen months, probably longer. She had not pressed charges twice. The neighbors had given up calling. The system, as Kim had to use the system, had no entry point.

Maybe the man on the porch had found one.
