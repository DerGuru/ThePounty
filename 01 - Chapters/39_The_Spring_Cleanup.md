# Chapter 39: The Spring Cleanup

Saturday morning, a little past eight. Dave came down the stairs in his oldest jeans and the sweatshirt Rosa had been trying to throw out for two years. The kitchen smelled like coffee and the warm yeast of the cinnamon rolls she had pulled from the oven ten minutes ago. The pan was already in the carrier on the counter, the foil tucked under at the corners, every fold the same width.

"Sandra's been at the corner since seven-thirty," Rosa said without turning around.

"Of course she has."

"With the clipboard."

"Of course."

Dave poured a cup and stood at the window. From the angle of the kitchen he could see the south end of the block, where the bench and the bed of bark mulch sat in the small patch of grass that nobody officially owned and everybody mowed. The daffodil bulbs Rosa had planted in October were finally showing green tongues. Sandra was there with the clipboard. Tamara Flynn was there too, with what looked like a clipboard of her own. They were standing about four feet apart and not quite facing each other.

"This is going to be a day," Dave said.

"It always is."

"Where are the kids?"

"Garage. Marcus is trying to convince Lina that he can carry both paint cans."

"Can he?"

"He can carry neither."

Dave drank his coffee. It was already warmer outside than it had been all week. The brown along the front lawns had been brown since November, and the green was coming up underneath it in patches that did not connect yet. The trees on the block were still bare. The forsythia at the Chens' was just starting, yellow buds along the bare twigs, the kind of color that did not look like much from a distance and made the whole street look hopeful up close.

Aldric's Volvo was in his driveway. The porch was empty. Not even a mug was on the table.

"He's already over there," Rosa said, following his look. "I saw him cross with something."

"With what?"

"I couldn't tell. Long. Wrapped."

"That sounds promising."

"It sounded like wood."

Dave thought about that for a second and then decided not to think about it any further until he had to.

By nine the corner was a small economy. Sandra had taped a piece of butcher paper to the back of the bench with the tasks written out in colored marker. Playground in green. Beds in yellow. Bench in blue. A column for names beside each. Most of the names were already filled in. Sandra knew how to get people to write their own names next to a job before they had time to think about whether they wanted the job.

Gerald was at the end of the bench with a coffee in one hand and an expression that suggested he had been talked into wearing the work pants he kept in the back of the closet for exactly this kind of weekend. He nodded at Dave. Dave nodded back. That was the entire conversation they needed.

Tamara's clipboard, it turned out, was a petition against the new cell tower the county was talking about putting up by the elementary school. She had walked down from the cul-de-sac with it and was now standing at the edge of Sandra's operation in a way that suggested she had not been invited and did not need to be. She had positioned herself such that everyone who came to add a name to Sandra's chart had to walk past hers. Sandra had positioned the bench such that Tamara had to stand in the wet grass. Sandra thought petitions were noise. Tamara thought benches were a distraction. Neither of them was looking at the other.

The Chens were unloading two flats of pansies from the back of their hatchback. Mr. Chen was carrying both flats and walking slowly. Mrs. Chen was carrying a thermos and a tape measure that she did not, on inspection, seem to need.

Hector Blum's truck was at the curb. Hector was at the bench end with a tape, a pencil, and the kind of patience that came from having measured ten thousand things in his life. He was prepared to measure ten thousand more. Beside him, on a tarp on the grass, were four boards. They were long. They were planed smooth. The ends were cut at angles that Dave, who had cut a lot of boards badly over the years, recognized as the work of a man who had cut a lot of boards well.

Aldric stood beside the tarp with a clipboard of his own. It was smaller than Sandra's. It had numbers on it.

"Morning, Aldric."

"Good morning, Dave. The wood is dry. I let it sit in the garage through the week to acclimate."

"Acclimate."

"The shop kept it under a fan. Bench wood should sit indoors for at least four days before assembly. Otherwise it cups."

"Cups."

"Bows in the middle. After a season."

"Of course." Dave nodded the nod of a man who was not going to say *I have never thought about this in my life.* "Hector, you good with these?"

Hector picked up one of the boards, sighted along the edge and set it down. He picked up another, did the same and nodded once.

"Good wood," he said.

Aldric inclined his head a quarter of an inch. The compliment was logged.

Rosa came over with the cinnamon roll pan and put it on the table next to the coffee urn Sandra had brought. Three other dishes were already there. All were foil-covered, weighted with small rocks against the breeze.

Aldric crossed the grass to the table. He stopped in front of Rosa.

"I owe you a belated greeting," he said. "March eighth was a Saturday. I let it pass without saying anything. I apologize."

Rosa blinked. Then she smiled the smile she had for people who said things she had not expected.

"Aldric. You don't owe me anything."

"I owe you the courtesy of having mentioned it on the day. I did not. I am mentioning it now."

"Thank you."

"Many happy returns."

"Thank you."

He turned, walked back to the tarp, and began handing Hector pre-counted screws from a small wax-paper envelope. 

Dave looked at Rosa.

"Did you know he knew when your birthday was?"

"He asked. In November. He said he wanted to make sure not to miss it."

"And then he missed it."

"Two weeks late," Rosa said. "For Aldric, that's a moral injury."

She picked up the pan and went to set it down at a better angle.

By ten-thirty the playground was half painted. Green, the same green it had been before, but cleaner. Lina was holding the lower rungs of the climbing frame while Marcus pretended to paint and actually got more paint on his sleeve than on the metal. Mrs. Chen had organized the pansies into three colors and was placing them one by one along the bed with the methodical pleasure of a person who did not get to arrange enough things in her life. Mr. Chen had been promoted from carrier to digger and was working a small spade in the soft soil between the daffodil tongues. He looked happy.

At the bench end Hector and Aldric had set the first two cross-pieces and were waiting for the third to be predrilled. Aldric held the drill carefully, two-handed, the tip steady. Hector held the board and did not say anything. He did not need to.

Sandra moved through the whole operation like a small weather front. She had words for everybody. She corrected Marcus's grip on the brush. She told Gerald his coffee was getting cold and refilled it before he could refuse. She crossed the grass to Tamara's clipboard, signed the petition with a flourish, and crossed back without breaking stride. Tamara looked at the signature. She did not smile, but she made a small mark beside the name.

Dave dug. The bed at the south end was his job. The soil was good, better than he remembered it being, which probably meant Rosa had been over here last fall doing things she had not bothered to mention. He worked the spade in, turned a square, lifted the brown winter mat off the top and shook the loose dirt back. Underneath there were earthworms. Good sign.

Ms. Yuen came down the sidewalk with a paper bag and stopped at the table. Without a word she put the small, square bag down and set a folded note on top with one of the foil rocks. Then she nodded at Sandra, nodded at Rosa, and walked back the way she had come. Sandra opened the bag, looked inside and closed it again with a kind of reverence Dave had only ever seen her use for boxes from her mother.

"What is it?" Dave called.

"Sesame brittle," Sandra said. "Don't touch it."

"I wouldn't."

"Don't even think about touching it."

He went back to the spade.

A cruiser came down Maple at eleven-fifteen. Dave heard the engine before he saw it, the soft idle of a vehicle moving below the speed limit on a residential street. He looked up because everybody looked up.

It was Officer Kim. She had her window down. She raised her hand from the steering wheel as she passed the corner.

Across the grass, at the bench, Aldric raised the hedge shears.

Hector had asked him to trim a bit of overhang off the boxwood that was crowding the new bench's footprint, and he had been about to do that when the cruiser came past. He lifted the shears like another man might have lifted a hat. Kim's hand stayed up a half second longer than it strictly needed to, and then she was past, the cruiser at the end of the block and around the corner.

It was nothing. People waved at police officers on this kind of street. Officers waved back. That was the whole transaction.

Dave looked at Sandra.

Sandra was looking at Aldric. She had stopped moving. The clipboard was at her side. Her head was tilted slightly to the right, the same tilt she used when she was watching one of the kids do something she did not understand yet.

It lasted maybe a second and a half. Then she was moving again, calling something to Tamara about the second flat of pansies, and the moment was gone.

Dave waited a beat to see if she would look at him.

She did not.

He went back to the spade. He filed it. He did not know where, but he filed it.

By one in the afternoon the bench was assembled, the playground was green and the beds were planted. Sandra had laid out the potluck on the table in a configuration that left no question about where anybody should sit. Marcus was eating a cinnamon roll with both hands. Lina was eating two of Ms. Yuen's sesame brittle pieces while watching her brother's paint smeared face.

Aldric stood at the edge of the grass with a paper plate and an upright posture. He took small bites of everything. He commented on Mrs. Chen's potato salad with a specificity that made her flush. He thanked Sandra for organizing. He thanked Tamara for the pansies, which Mrs. Chen had brought but which Tamara had selected, and Tamara smiled the first real smile of the morning.

Hector ate standing up beside his truck. Gerald sat at the bench they had just built and tested it with his weight. He nodded at Aldric. Aldric inclined his head.

Dave found himself sitting on the grass next to Rosa. She had taken her shoes off. The sun was on her face. The brown along the lawns was, from down here, mostly green underneath.

"Good day," Rosa said.

"Good day."

"It's going to rain tomorrow."

"That's fine. The beds need it."

She leaned against him. He let her. The street was quiet in the way of a street that had something to show for itself. The bench held Gerald's weight. The paint was drying. The pansies were where they were supposed to be.

Aldric paused at the corner of the playground on his way back to his house. He stood there for a moment and looked east. Birch Street was three blocks that way, not visible from here, but it was the direction. Then he turned, walked across the grass, said something polite to Mrs. Chen, and went home.

Dave watched him go.

"What?" Rosa said.

"Nothing."

She made the small sound she made when she did not believe him. She did not press. She rarely did. She knew how to let him think about things until he was ready to talk about them, and how to be there when he was ready.

He did not know what he would say if she did press. There was nothing to say. The day was good. The street had worked. Aldric had brought wood that did not cup. Kim had waved. The bench was a bench.

He sat with Rosa and watched the corner empty out. Sandra was wrapping leftovers. Marcus was asleep on a folded coat. Hector was loading his tape and pencil back into the truck.

The afternoon felt like something he didn't want to set down.

He set it down anyway.

He got up and went to help Sandra carry the leftovers home.
