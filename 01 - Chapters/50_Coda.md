# Coda

*Saturday, the 21st of June 2025*

The first tomatoes worth buying came at the end of June, and Miranda took her time over them, which gave her a reason to stand at the stall longer than the buying required. She liked the market for the same reason she liked the back pew on Sundays. A person could be still in a crowd without anyone asking why, and from a still place in a moving crowd you could see almost everything.

She was not choosing the tomatoes the way the woman beside her was choosing them, turning each one to the light and pressing the shoulder with a thumb. Miranda had stopped seeing the tomatoes a minute ago. She was watching the man behind the table.

He had a system, and it was a good one, and she had needed three customers to be sure of it. He weighed honestly for the regulars, the ones who came every week and would notice. For the strangers he let the side of his hand ride the lip of the pan, a few ounces of forearm leaning into the weight, gone again before the needle settled. The man ahead of her paid for a pound and a half and carried off a little over a pound, smiling, thanking him, none the wiser. It was small. It was careful. It was the kind of thing a person did for years because nobody who caught it ever bothered to say so.

Something in her did not like it. She had never been able to name the something, or build anything on top of it, as she imagined other people built their principles into tidy walls they could point to. She only had the feeling, which arrived the way heat arrives off a stovetop, present and physical and asking to be answered. It was not outrage. Outrage was loud and she was never loud. It was closer to tidiness. A thing was crooked and her hands wanted it straight.

She did not make a scene. She had learned a long time ago that a scene gave a man somewhere to put his guilt, an argument to have instead of the silence he deserved. So when her turn came she set her tomatoes in the pan herself and watched the needle find its number, and she looked at it, and she looked at him, and she let him watch her read it.

"That's a fair scale today," she said. Pleasant. A neighbor's voice. "I'll know next week."

His face did several things at once and settled on none of them. He gave her the right change without being asked. She thanked him and walked on, and that was all of it, and it was enough, and she did not think about him again.

What she thought about, three steps into the crowd, was that someone was looking at her.

She knew the feeling as she knew the heat off the stovetop, by the body before the mind. Most days the market was full of looking and none of it landed on her, because she had spent her life learning not to be the kind of woman a glance caught and held. The looking that found her now was not a glance. It had weight. It had the particular stillness of attention that has decided to stay.

She did not turn her head. She let her eyes do the work while her face stayed with the tomatoes, and she found him at the edge of the green, by the bread van, a gray-haired man in a linen shirt with a loaf he did not seem in any hurry to pay for. He was not looking at her now. He was looking at the middle distance with the mild, pleasant emptiness of a man waiting for nothing in particular, and the emptiness was so well made that she almost let it go.

She almost let it go. Then she placed him, and the bottom dropped a quiet inch out of the morning.

The church. Early May, then the Sundays after. A man who came in late and sat at the back and did not kneel when the others knelt, did not find the hymn, did not do any of the small bodily things that a person does without thinking when the building is theirs. He had watched the congregation the whole hour with the same mild emptiness he was wearing now. She had noticed him then for the simplest of reasons. He was doing what she was doing. Two people in a room full of bowed heads, both of them upright, both of them there to watch and not to pray. She had wondered, once, idly, what he was looking for. Then he had stopped coming, and she had stopped wondering.

Here he was, not looking at her, at the one market she came to, on a Saturday, with a loaf he wasn't buying.

A normal person would have caught none of it. That was the thought that stayed with her, cold and clear, while her hands tucked the tomatoes into her bag as though her hands had nothing else on their mind. A normal person would have seen a tired man with bread. She was not, in this one narrow way she never spoke about, a normal person, and neither, she understood, was he. He had the same trick of standing in a crowd as though assembled out of its spare parts. He had built that emptiness on his face the way she built hers, and she knew it was built because she knew the work.

He had not seen her see him. She was fairly sure of that. His eyes had moved off her a half-second before her own arrived, and a man that careful did not give you a half-second by accident unless he believed he didn't need to. He thought he was invisible. He was very good, and being very good had made him certain, and his certainty was the one thing she had that he didn't.

So she did the hardest thing, which was nothing. She did not stare. She did not let her step change. She did not give him the gift of knowing he'd been caught, because a watcher who thinks himself unseen keeps coming, and keeps showing you the shape of what he wants, and a watcher who's been warned only gets better at hiding. She had not built that rule out of anything. She simply knew it, as she knew the rest.

She settled the bag on her shoulder and walked toward the gap in the stalls where the lot began, unhurried, a woman with her tomatoes and her Saturday and nothing on her mind. She did not look back. She would not look back today.

But she would be at the market next Saturday, and at the back pew tomorrow, and from now on she would be looking too, and she would find him before he knew she was trying, or she would not, and either way the watching ran in both directions now.

She just had to make sure he was the last to know it.
