# Epilogue: The Guy From Church

*Saturday, the 21st of June 2025*

The first decent tomatoes just showed up at the market, and Miranda took her time over them, which gave her a reason to stand at the stall longer than picking fruit could explain. She liked the market for the same reason she liked the back row at church. You could stand still in a crowd and nobody asked why, and from somewhere still in the middle of something moving, you could see just about everything.

She wasn't sorting the tomatoes like the woman beside her was sorting them, turning each one to the light, pressing the shoulder with a thumb. Actually, she'd stopped seeing the tomatoes a minute ago. She was watching the guy behind the table.

He had a system. A decent one, too. She had to give him that. It had taken her three market days to be sure of it. He weighed honest for the regulars, the faces that came every week and would notice. For the strangers he let the side of his hand ride the lip of the pan, a little forearm leaning into the weight, gone before the needle settled. The man ahead of her paid for a pound and a half and walked off with maybe a pound, smiling, thanking him, none the wiser. Small. Tidy. The kind of thing a guy got away with for years because nobody who caught it ever bothered to say so.

It bugged her. She'd never been able to say why, or stack the why into anything the way other people seemed to keep their reasons walled up and ready to point at. She just had the itch. It came up like heat off a stovetop, there and physical and wanting something done about it. Not anger. Anger was loud and she didn't like loud. More like a crooked picture on a wall. Her hands wanted it straight. Other people had their walls. She'd never needed one.

She didn't make a scene. A scene gave a guy somewhere to put it, an argument to have instead of the quiet he had coming. So when it was her turn she set the tomatoes in the pan herself, watched the needle find its number, looked at the number, looked at him, and let him watch her read it.

"Fair scale today." Friendly. Easy. "I'll know next week."

His face tried four things and landed on none of them. He gave her the right change without being asked. She thanked him, meant it about as much as he'd meant the smile, and moved on. Done. She didn't think about him again.

She drifted down the green to the fruit stall, the first cherries beside the last of the strawberries, and was turning a handful over for something to do with her hands. That was when she felt it.

A gut feeling. Something she couldn't pinpoint, but her head was already working on it. Most days the market was all looking and none of it stuck to her. She'd spent her whole adult life learning not to be the kind of woman a look caught on. This wasn't the market's idle looking. This had weight. It had that flat, patient stillness of attention that has decided to stay a while. Someone had settled on her and wasn't moving off.

She didn't turn her head. She let her eyes do the searching while her face stayed down with the fruit, and there he was, off at the edge of the green by the bread van. Gray hair, linen shirt, a loaf in his hand he was in no rush to pay for. Not looking at her now. Looking at the middle of nothing with the mild, pleasant blankness of a man waiting on nothing at all, and the blankness was so clean she nearly bought it.

She nearly bought it. Then she placed him, and the morning dropped a quiet inch out from under her.

Church. Early May, then the Sundays after. He'd come in late and sat near the back. And he didn't move like a man raised in the liturgy. He knelt when the rest of them knelt, but a beat late, always the same beat late, going down after the motion had already passed through the pews. He rose a beat late. He found the hymn a beat late, his eyes cutting first to the open book beside him. A man copying the room instead of remembering it. To everyone else, a newcomer still learning the floor. To her, watching from the back as she always did, the tell. He wasn't there for the prayer any more than she was. He was there for the people. He'd folded himself into the kneeling and the standing to hide it, a beat behind. She'd never bothered to hide it at all. She'd wondered once, lazily, what he was after. Then he quit coming, and she quit wondering.

And here he was now. Not looking at her. At the one market she used every Saturday. Holding bread he didn't seem to be buying.

Nobody normal would have caught a thread of it. That was what stuck, cold and clear, while her hands tucked the tomatoes away like they had nothing else going on. Somebody normal would have seen a tired man with a loaf. She wasn't normal, in this one narrow way she never talked about, and neither, she got it now, was he. He had the same trick of standing in a crowd like he'd been built out of its spare parts. He'd put that blankness on the same as she put on hers, and she knew it was put on because she knew the work that went into it.

He hadn't seen her see him. She was pretty sure of that. His eyes had slid off her half a second before hers got there, and a guy that careful didn't hand you half a second by accident unless he was sure he didn't need it. He thought he was a ghost. He was good, and being that good had made him certain. His certainty was the one thing she had on him.

And she knew certain. She'd been reading it her whole adult life, off every person she'd ever caught at something. It always sat the same: worn right up to the second the floor went out from under them. Certain was what people put on when they'd never once been seen through. She'd seen through every one of them. This one was better. Older. Smoother. A cleaner build than anything she'd run into. But better was still a thing you took apart, given a few quiet Saturdays to do it in. She'd never met the front she couldn't find the seam in.

So she did the hard thing, which was nothing. She didn't stare, didn't change her step, didn't hand him the gift of knowing he'd been made. A watcher who thinks he's invisible keeps coming back, keeps showing you the shape of whatever he's after. A watcher who knows he's been spotted just gets better at hiding. She hadn't built that out of any rulebook. She just knew it, same as she knew the rest.

She got the bag up on her shoulder and walked for the gap in the stalls where the lot started, easy, a woman with her tomatoes and her Saturday and nothing on her mind. She didn't look back. She wasn't going to give him that today.

But she'd be here next Saturday. And in the back row tomorrow. And from here on she'd be looking too, and she'd find him before he ever caught her at it.

She just had to make sure he was the last to know it.
