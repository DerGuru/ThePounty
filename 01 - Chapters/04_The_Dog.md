# Chapter 4: The Dog

The dog at number seven had no name. Or rather, it had a name, but nobody on Maple Lane used it because the dog had never responded to it and the owners had stopped trying. The dog was a mutt — part shepherd, part something else, part rage — and it barked at everything that moved. Cars. Bikes. Squirrels. The wind. Its own shadow on a cloudy day. The Parkers' kid was afraid of it. Mrs. Alvarez crossed the street to avoid its yard. Even Gerald, who was afraid of nothing except possibly his own imagination, gave it a wide berth.

The dog barked at Dave every single time Dave took out the trash. Every single time. Like it forgot him overnight and woke up furious at his existence each morning.

It did not bark at Aldric.

Dave had noticed this in week two. Aldric walked past number seven on his way to the mailbox. The dog was in the yard, on the chain, the way it always was. Dave braced for the explosion of noise. Nothing. The dog sat there. Watched Aldric pass. Watched him come back. Quiet.

Dave mentioned it to Rosa. Rosa said dogs were good judges of character. Dave said dogs were good judges of nothing; their own dog growing up had been terrified of the vacuum cleaner and once ate an entire crayon box. Rosa said that was different. Dave didn't ask how.

It was a Thursday. Dave was working from home, laptop on the kitchen table, coffee going cold. He was on a call that didn't need him, which was most calls, muted, occasionally typing something into the chat to prove he existed. Through the window he could see the mailman's truck working its way up the street. The dog could see it too.

The dog's relationship with the mailman was the central conflict of its life. More important than food, more important than squirrels, more important than its own name. The mailman came every day. Every day the dog lost its mind. Every day the chain held. The mailman put mail in boxes. The dog screamed at the injustice of it. The mailman left. The dog won. This had been happening for three years.

Today the chain didn't hold.

Dave saw it happen in slow motion. The mailman's truck pulled up to number seven. The dog launched. The chain snapped at the stake — not the collar end, the ground end, the rusted bolt that Dave had vaguely thought someone should replace for at least a year. The dog hit full speed in three strides.

The mailman — Kevin, his name was Kevin — saw sixty pounds of angry mutt coming and did what anyone would do. He grabbed his bag and held it in front of him like a shield and backed up against the truck.

"Hey! HEY!" Dave was already standing. His coffee went sideways. He was at the front door by the time the mug stopped rolling.

The dog was in the street. Kevin was pressed against his truck. The dog wasn't biting. It was doing something worse — circling. Low. Growling. The kind of growl that came from somewhere in the chest that wasn't quite a bark and wasn't quite a decision yet. The deciding growl. The one where the dog was figuring out what to do next.

Dave was in his socks. He didn't have shoes. He went anyway.

"Hey! Hey, dog! Get back!"

The dog didn't care about Dave. The dog had never cared about Dave. Dave was furniture. Dave was trash-day noise.

Then Aldric was there.

Dave didn't see where he came from. The front door, probably. He'd been outside — Dave had seen him on the porch earlier, coffee, the usual. But between Dave's "hey" and the dog's next growl, Aldric was in the street. Not running. Walking. Straight line. No hesitation. No hands up. No noise.

He stopped ten feet from the dog.

The dog turned.

For one second — one full second that felt like a month — the dog and Aldric looked at each other. The dog was low. Hackles up. Teeth showing. Every signal in its body said *I am dangerous. I will hurt you.*

Aldric didn't move.

He didn't speak. Didn't crouch. Didn't offer a hand. Didn't do any of the things Dave had seen people do with aggressive dogs. He just stood. Feet shoulder-width. Hands at his sides. Looking at the dog. Not at its eyes — Dave noticed that later, replaying it. He was looking at something lower. The chest, maybe. The shoulders.

The growl faded.

Not stopped. Faded. Like a radio losing signal. The dog's body changed. The tension went out of it in pieces — first the tail, which dropped, then the shoulders, then the head. In five seconds the dog went from attack posture to something Dave had never seen it do before.

It sat down.

In the middle of the street. Sat down. Looked at Aldric. Panted.

Aldric took one step forward. Then another. Slowly. Not cautiously — Dave could see the difference, and it mattered. Cautious meant afraid. Aldric wasn't cautious. He was deliberate. Like each step was a sentence he was saying in a language the dog understood.

He reached down. Took the trailing chain. Wrapped it once around his hand. The dog didn't flinch.

"Good," Aldric said. One word. Quiet. The dog's tail moved. Not a wag. A twitch. Like *good* meant something to it for the first time.

Kevin the mailman was still pressed against his truck. "Holy shit," he said.

"I'm sorry about that," Aldric said to him. "Are you all right?"

"I— yeah. Yeah, I'm fine. How did you—"

"He's overstimulated. The territory instinct triggered and then the adrenaline had nowhere to go. He wasn't going to bite. He didn't know what to do next." Aldric was walking the dog back to number seven's yard. The dog was walking next to him. Heeling. Like they'd practiced this. "The stake bolt was rusted through. It needs replacing."

"I'll tell the Greens," Dave said. He was still standing in the street in his socks. He'd gotten there after Aldric despite leaving first. He wasn't sure how that worked.

"I'll replace it," Aldric said. "If they don't mind. I have the right bolt."

Of course he did. Of course he had the right bolt. For a neighbor's dog chain stake. In his house. Already.

Aldric led the dog back to the yard. Tied the chain to the porch railing temporarily — a loop Dave recognized as something specific, some kind of knot that wrapped twice and held without tightening. Then he went to his garage and came back with a bolt, a wrench, and a new ground stake. Stainless steel. The old one was cast iron. Rusted.

He replaced it in four minutes. Attached the chain. Tested it twice. Nodded.

The dog sat on the porch and watched. Still panting. Still calm.

"I'll leave a note for the Greens," Aldric said. "And I'll bring some water out for him. He's been in the sun."

He said this the way you'd say *I'll get milk on the way home.* Ordinary. Obvious. What you do.

Kevin finished his route in silence. Dave went back inside. His coffee was on the floor. The call was still going. Nobody had noticed he was gone. He unmuted for a second, said "agreed," and muted again.

He cleaned up the coffee. Sat back down. Stared at his laptop.

He kept thinking about the dog. Not the dog getting loose — that was just a thing that happened, chains broke, dogs ran. He kept thinking about the moment in the street. Aldric, standing there. Not moving. Looking at the dog's chest instead of its eyes.

Dave didn't know anything about animal behavior. But he knew what confidence looked like, and he knew what control looked like, and he knew that what Aldric had done wasn't either of those things exactly. It was something else. It was knowing — *knowing*, before the outcome was clear — that the situation would go the way he wanted it to go. Not hoping. Not guessing. Knowing.

That was different from bravery. Dave had seen brave people hesitate. Aldric hadn't hesitated.

The dog barked at Dave's trash can that evening. Normal. Loud. Angry.

Dave looked over at Aldric's house. Aldric was on the porch. Coffee. The chair. The quiet. The dog at number seven was quiet too, watching him through the slats.

"It still likes you," Dave called over.

"I don't think it likes me," Aldric said. "I think it recognizes me."

"What's the difference?"

Aldric thought about it. Or seemed to. "Liking requires comfort. Recognition only requires understanding."

Dave didn't know what to do with that. He waved. Went inside. Rosa was making dinner. The house smelled like onions and normalcy.

"Did you hear about the dog?" he said.

"Sandra texted me. She said Aldric 'tamed it with his mind.'"

"He didn't tame it with his mind."

"What did he do?"

Dave opened the fridge. Stood there. Closed it again without taking anything. "He just... stood there. And it stopped."

Rosa turned from the stove. "He just stood there."

"Yeah."

"And a dog that's been aggressive for three years just... stopped."

"Yeah."

Rosa didn't say anything. She went back to the onions. Dave sat at the table. He could still see Aldric's porch through the kitchen window. The chair. The coffee. The circle of yellow light.

Normal.

Tomorrow he'd ask Aldric how he did it. Ask him where he learned that. Normal question. Neighbors asked each other things all the time.

He wouldn't ask, though. He already knew that. He wouldn't ask because the answer would be polite, and complete, and friendly, and he'd walk away knowing less than before.

That was the pattern. He was starting to notice the pattern.
