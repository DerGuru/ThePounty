# Chapter 42: Maria's Window

*Wednesday, the 9th of April 2025*

The tape never went on straight the first time. Maria had learned that with the fence and again with the tub. She had also learned not to mind it. She peeled back the crooked inch, set it down again along the edge of the glass and pressed it flat with her thumbnail, as the man at the hardware store had shown her, without making it a lesson. Blue tape along the muntins. Newspaper on the sill. The window frames had been the gray of old teeth since before Carlos left, and this week she had decided they would be white by Sunday.

The faucet had been first, back in January. Then the tub, once the caulk had sat on the kitchen table long enough to shame her into it, the old bead scraped out and a clean white line run in its place. The brown scallop on the ceiling underneath had stopped spreading after that, and last month she had primed it and painted over it, and now it was just ceiling. Some mornings she stood under it with her coffee and could not find where it had been. At this rate, she had told Jesús on the phone, she might put up a second shingle. Crochet on Etsy and handyman on Thumbtack. He had laughed, but she had half meant it.

It was a little after nine on Wednesday morning. The light came in low and clean across the front room. She worked the tape down the long edge. She was reaching for the scissors when she looked up through the glass and saw the car.

It sat at the curb in front of number six, the empty house, where the For Sale sign had been leaning at the same tired angle since it had been reset in fall. A gray sedan, the kind you forgot while you were still looking at it. There was a man in the driver's seat. He had a paper cup in his hand and a cap pulled low. He was not reading the newspaper folded against the wheel, though it was open as if he were.

Maria knew the cars on Maple Lane. Not as Gerald knew them, like he kept books about them. She knew them by heart. The way she knew which step on her porch creaked, or which of the Chen children was crying across the street without looking. The car did not belong. It had been there yesterday too, further down, in front of the Chens'. She had noticed it like a chair moved six inches in a room she had lived in for twenty-three years.

She held the scissors and looked at it for a moment. Then she looked past it, to number four.

Aldric was on his porch.

He was not reading. The mug sat on the table. The book lay beside it, closed. He stood at the rail with his hands resting on the top of it, looking out at his own street on a Wednesday morning. He had her cardigan on, the one she had made him. From here, down the street past the empty house, the slate blue went gray in the morning light. She had spent eleven days on that yarn and she could still find her own tension in it from her kitchen window. He wore it most mornings now. Coffee. Spring coming in. Nothing in it.

Except that Maria had spent a whole Saturday three feet from those hands.

She had not thought about it in those words before. But she had been beside him for six hours with a brush in her own hand, and a body learns another body at that distance, like a song you did not mean to memorize. She knew how his hands rested when they were between tasks. Loose. Open. The fingers giving up their weight to whatever they lay on. That was how he had held the brush when he stopped to let her catch up, easy, the tool forgotten in his grip.

His hands were not resting like that now. They lay along the rail and they looked the same as ever to anyone who had not stood beside them, but they were not giving up their weight. They were waiting to be asked for it. She could not have said how she knew, only that her own hands, wrapped around the scissors, had gone still in sympathy, a stillness with waiting in it.

She set the scissors down on the newspaper.

He was watching the street. He was good at watching the street without seeming to, she knew that too, everyone on the block half-knew it. But this morning there was a thing under the watching. He stood like Jesús at the top of a ladder, before he trusted his weight to it. Settled and not settled. Ready to move and choosing, for now, not to.

The car was right there. Between Aldric and her, parked in front of the one house on the street with no one in it to wonder why. A man with a cold coffee, watching number four.

And Aldric, on his porch, not once looking at it.

That was the part that turned in her chest. Not the car. Aldric would look at a squirrel that crossed the lawn wrong. He had wished her many happy returns on her birthday in January, the day itself, which had unsettled her, because she had never told him when it was, and no one on the street had known either. He saw the street. He saw all of it, always, as Maria saw her own kitchen. A strange car sitting in front of the empty house for two mornings was not a thing Aldric Venn would fail to see.

So he was not failing to see it. He was choosing not to look. And a man only worked that hard at not looking at a thing he had already counted.

She understood, standing at her half-taped window with newspaper under her feet, that the two things in front of her were one thing. She did not have Gerald's word for it, or Kim's, or whatever word Aldric himself kept for mornings like this. She only had the feeling, plain and cold and certain, that something was coming up the quiet of her street. The man at number four had known it was coming before the car ever parked. He stood very still on his porch, the way you stand still when the thing has not arrived yet but will.

Aldric straightened from the rail. He picked up the mug and the book. He looked, for a moment, toward her window.

Maria stayed where she was. He lifted the hand with the book in it, an inch, the smallest wave. She lifted hers, the one still tacky with tape.

He almost smiled. He did not, quite. Then he went inside and the door closed. The porch was only a porch again, the mug gone, the chair empty, the morning going on as mornings did.

The gray car stayed where it was.

Maria stood at the glass a while longer. She thought about going over. She thought about what she would even say. *There's a man in a car.* He knew about the man in the car better than she did. *Are you all right.* He was always all right. That was the whole trouble with him. He was the man who had brought her photographs back to her door, fixed Sandra's garden, and put the right number in her own hand when the bank closed her out. He was always all right. The cost of it went somewhere she could not see and was not invited.

She knew something else now too, and it sat quieter than the rest. There was no work to share this time. The fence had been hers to learn beside him. This was not. Whatever was coming up Maple Lane was his alone, and there was no brush he could hand her, no edge he could show her how to hold. For once she did not hate the not-being-able. She only minded it, like weather you could not stop, on behalf of someone standing out in it.

She picked the scissors back up. The tape was waiting, crooked at the corner where she had let go of it. She peeled it back, set it down again, straight this time, and pressed it flat with her thumb.

Outside, the car did not move. She worked, kept the window in front of her, and did not look away from it as often as her hands would have liked.
