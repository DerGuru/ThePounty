# Chapter 24: Gerald's Mistake

Gerald had been following Aldric Venn for eleven minutes.

Not following. Walking in the same direction. At roughly the same pace, three blocks behind. That was different. Anyone could be walking to the library on a Thursday afternoon. Gerald went to the library. Not often, but sometimes. Sandra had books on hold. He could pick those up. That was a reason to be here.

He'd seen Venn leave his house at 2:15. On foot, which was unusual. Venn drove almost everywhere. The Volvo was precise, maintained, never left out overnight. A man who walked when he could drive was a man going somewhere close enough that driving would look strange.

Three blocks. The library was three blocks south.

Gerald stayed on the opposite side of the street. He wore the gray jacket, not the blue one. The blue one was memorable. Sandra had said so once. "You look like a retired mailman in that thing." The gray one blended. Gray blended with everything.

The library was a single-story brick building from the seventies, flat roof, narrow windows, the kind of architecture that said *municipal budget* in every line. Gerald had lived in this neighborhood for thirty-one years. He'd been inside maybe forty times. Sandra more. Sandra read a book a week, minimum, but she used the digital loans now. He couldn't remember the last time either of them had physically walked in.

Venn went up the steps. Gerald waited. He counted to sixty. Then he crossed the street, walked past the entrance, and glanced through the glass door. Venn was at the front desk, talking to the librarian. Pleasant. Smiling. The librarian smiled back.

Gerald went in.

The layout was simple. Front desk to the left. Fiction straight ahead, six rows of shelves. Non-fiction to the right, organized by Dewey. Study tables in the back by the windows. Two computers, both occupied by teenagers. A children's section in the far corner with beanbags and a mural of a tree.

Venn was gone from the front desk. Gerald scanned the room.

There. Non-fiction, second row from the right. Local history. Gerald could read the section sign from here: 970–979. Venn was pulling a book from the shelf, turning it over, reading the back. He put it back. Pulled another. Kept that one. Moved down the row.

Gerald went to fiction. Mystery section. He pulled a random paperback and opened it to the middle. He held it like he was reading. Over the top of the page, he watched.

Venn collected three books. He took them to one of the study tables by the windows, sat down, and opened the first one. He took out his notebook and started writing in it while he read. Occasionally he turned pages. Occasionally he wrote. The pace was unhurried. Academic, almost. Like a man doing research, not looking for something specific.

Gerald moved closer. Not obvious. He went to the reference section, which was one row over from the study tables. Property records, city planning documents, bound copies of the township newsletter going back to 1982. He pulled a binder and set it on the shelf at eye level. Through the gap between the binder and the books beside it, he could see Venn's table.

The books. He needed to see the books.

The first one was face-down, spine up. Gerald squinted. Gold lettering on dark green. *Greenfield Township: A History, 1847–1987.* The second was thinner, lighter cover. He couldn't read the title from here. The third was open in front of Venn. Large format. Old photographs. Gerald could see the sepia from here.

Local history. Aldric Venn was researching local history.

Gerald stood there for a long time. The binder was heavy in his hands. He realized he hadn't turned a page.

He put the binder back. Walked to the study tables. Passed Venn's table on the way to the water fountain. Close enough to see.

The open book was a photographic history of the township. The page showed an aerial shot from the 1960s, back when half the neighborhood was farmland. Venn's notebook had a sketch in it. A map. Streets and lot lines, with small annotations Gerald couldn't read without stopping, and stopping would mean looking, and looking would mean being caught looking.

He drank from the fountain. Cold, metallic water. He straightened up and wiped his mouth.

Venn looked up.

"Gerald. Good afternoon." No surprise in his voice. None at all. Like finding your neighbor in the library on a Thursday was the most natural thing in the world. "I didn't know you came here."

"Sandra has books on hold." The lie came easily. He'd prepared it.

"Please give her my regards." Venn smiled. The polite one. The one that covered everything and revealed nothing. "Have you seen this?" He turned the book toward Gerald. The aerial photograph. "This is Maple Lane in 1962. Before the cul-de-sac was built. It used to go straight through to Birch. Fascinating how much the topology changed."

Gerald looked at the photograph. He could see it. The road, straight and narrow, cutting through what was now the park at the end. No houses past number seven. Fields beyond that. A barn where the Weavers' house stood now.

"I didn't know that," Gerald said. Which was true.

"The soil composition is different on the west end. That's why the lots there are larger. The original developer couldn't build as dense because of the clay layer." Venn tapped a section of the photograph. "Your house is built on the original farmhouse foundation. Did you know that?"

Gerald did not know that.

"The records are in the township archive. If you're ever curious." Venn turned the book back and continued reading. Dismissed. Politely, warmly, but dismissed.

Gerald walked to the front desk. He asked about Sandra's holds. There were none. He said thank you and left.

Outside, the November air was sharp. He stood on the library steps and breathed.

Local history. Old photographs. Soil composition. Lot lines from 1962.

He thought about the notebook. The other notebook. His own, at home, in the top drawer of his desk with seventeen pages of observations, dates, patterns. Times Venn left and returned. License plates of unfamiliar cars. The shoes. The posture. The way he never locked his Volvo but always locked the house twice.

He thought about what he'd just seen. A man in a library, reading about the history of his own street. Making notes. Drawing maps. Being friendly to his neighbor when that neighbor appeared, uninvited, at his elbow.

That was it. That was all Gerald had found. After five months of observation, of noting, of watching from the angled blinds. A man who was interested in where he lived.

Gerald walked home. He went to the study, opened the drawer, and looked at the notebook. The black cover was worn at the corners from being picked up and set down. Seventeen pages. Five months. And what had it produced?

Aldric Venn was polite. Aldric Venn was precise. Aldric Venn was interested in local history and soil composition and aerial photographs from before either of them was born.

Gerald picked up the notebook. Held it for a moment.

He put it in the drawer. Closed the drawer.

Sandra came in from the kitchen. "Did they have my books?"

"Nothing on hold."

"Hmm. Maybe next week." She looked at him. That look she had when she was about to say something and then didn't. Then she said it anyway. "You okay? You look like you've been thinking."

"I ran into Aldric at the library."

"Oh?" Her eyes lit up. That meant she'd want details. Every detail.

"He was reading about local history. The street. Old photographs."

Sandra's face did something complicated. Disappointment, mostly. "That's it?"

"That's it."

"He's a history nerd." She said it like it was an accusation against the universe. Like the universe owed her something more interesting.

"Apparently."

She went back to the kitchen. Gerald heard the kettle click on. He sat in his chair and looked at the closed drawer.

Five months. And the man was a nerd. An extremely polite, impeccably dressed nerd who'd moved to Maple Lane with three boxes and a perfect credit score and settled in like he'd always been here.

Gerald felt something he didn't often feel. Embarrassment. Not the hot, public kind. The quiet, private kind. The kind that came from realizing you'd built something large and serious and important out of nothing.

He'd been wrong. Not about the strangeness. Venn was strange. But strange wasn't dangerous. Strange was just different. Strange was a man who researched the clay composition of his own street and told you about it with genuine enthusiasm. Strange was polishing shoes on a porch at nine PM because that was when you polished shoes.

Gerald opened his laptop. Checked the weather. Read a headline about something happening in Congress. Normal things. Thursday things.

He didn't open the drawer again that evening. Or the next day. Or the day after that.

Sandra asked him once, that weekend, if he'd noticed anything about the new neighbor lately. He said no. He said it easily. He said it like a man who had moved on.

The notebook stayed in the drawer. The pen stayed capped. For the first time in five months, Gerald Hooper went to bed without thinking about the man across the street.
