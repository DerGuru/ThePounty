# Chapter 19: The Gutter

*Sunday, the 27th of October 2024*

The water had been running down the wall for three days before Maria called Jesús.

She'd noticed it on Thursday. A dark stripe on the siding below the gutter, wet even when it hadn't rained in hours. She'd gone outside and looked up. The gutter was sagging near the downspout, pulling away from the fascia board. Leaves packed in tight, a greenish-brown paste that smelled like decay when she stood close enough.

She knew what the problem was. She could see it. A clog, maybe a broken bracket. The kind of thing that needed a ladder, five minutes, and hands that weren't afraid of heights.

Maria was afraid of heights.

She thought about it Thursday evening while she crocheted. She thought about it Friday morning while she checked her Etsy orders. By Saturday, the dark stripe had widened. The paint was starting to blister at the bottom edge, tiny bubbles forming in the white. She could hear the water at night when the house was quiet. A slow, irregular drip. Like a leaking faucet, except it was outside and she couldn't reach it.

She called Jesús on Saturday afternoon.

"Hey, Ma. Everything okay?"

"The gutter's clogged. Above the kitchen window. It's pulling away from the house."

"I'll look at it tomorrow. I'll bring the tall ladder."

"Thank you."

"Don't try anything."

"I wasn't going to."

He showed up at nine-thirty. The mower came down first, then the extension ladder, a bucket, and gloves. He leaned the ladder against the siding, climbed it, and was at the gutter before Maria had finished tying her robe.

She stood in the driveway with coffee and watched him work. He scooped the muck into the bucket, hand over hand, quick and efficient. He checked the bracket, tightened something with a wrench he'd brought in his back pocket. He ran water from a bottle down the gutter and watched it flow into the downspout. Clean.

Five minutes. Maybe six.

He came down the ladder with the bucket in one hand. "All good. The bracket was loose, too. I tightened it, but you might want to replace it eventually. The screw's stripped."

"Thank you."

"No problem." He set the bucket down and pulled off the gloves. His hands were dark with decomposed leaves. He rinsed them with the garden hose and shook them dry. "You want me to check the other side too?"

"No, it's fine. That side's fine."

"You sure? While I'm here—"

"It's fine, Jesús."

He looked at her. Not worried. Just checking. The look he'd started giving her after Carlos left. The look that meant: *Are you okay, or are you saying you're okay?*

"It's really fine," she said. "Really. Thank you."

He nodded. Started folding the ladder.

Aldric came around the corner from his house. He was wearing the cardigan over a white t-shirt, hands in his pockets, walking with that unhurried stride he had when he was going somewhere but not in a rush. Heading toward the park, probably. He did that on Sunday mornings sometimes.

He saw them and adjusted his path toward the driveway.

"Good morning, Maria." He looked at Jesús, then back at her. Pleasant, open, waiting to be introduced.

"Morning, Aldric. This is my son, Jesús. Jesús, this is Aldric Venn. From next door." She gestured vaguely down the street toward his house.

Aldric extended his hand. "A pleasure. I've heard good things."

Jesús wiped his hand on his jeans before shaking. "Likewise. You're the one who got her photos back."

"I had a conversation with the right person at the right time." Aldric smiled. "She tells me you do landscape architecture. That's a field I respect enormously. The intersection of utility and beauty."

"Thanks." Jesús glanced at the bucket. "Today's more utility than beauty."

"Both have their place." Aldric nodded at Maria. "Enjoy the morning." He walked on, same pace, toward the park.

Jesús watched him go. "Seems nice."

"He is."

"Formal, though."

She lifted one shoulder. "That's just how he is."

Jesús loaded the ladder onto the truck. Then he got the mower down, put in his headphones, and did the lawn. Twenty minutes, same pattern as always. The turns at the fence line. The pause at the flower bed. Maria went inside and listened to the rhythm of it through the open window.

When he was done, he knocked on the back door. She handed him water. He kissed her cheek, told her to call if anything else came up, and drove away. The bucket sat on the driveway with its load of wet leaves. Jesús had probably forgotten it.

Maria stood there.

The gutter was fixed. The water wouldn't run down the wall anymore. The paint would dry, the blister would probably peel, and she'd have to repaint that section eventually. But the problem was solved. Had been solved for her. In five minutes, by her twenty-two-year-old son, on a Sunday morning, because she'd called and asked him to come.

She picked up the bucket. It was heavier than it looked. She carried it to the green bin, dumped the leaves and rinsed the bucket with the hose. She set it by the garage door because she didn't know where Jesús had gotten it from and he'd probably want it back.

She went inside, made more coffee, and sat at the kitchen table.

The house was quiet. The dripping had stopped. That should have felt good. It did feel good, technically.

But.

She was forty-two years old. She'd lived here for twenty-three years. She knew where every light switch was, which floorboard creaked, how the heating system hummed differently in November than in January. She knew this house better than anyone alive.

She couldn't fix her own gutter.

It wasn't the height. Not really. The height was an excuse. A good one, a reasonable one. No one expected a forty-two-year-old woman to climb a ladder alone. That was smart. That was safe.

But the gutter was just this week. Last month it had been the dryer vent. The month before that, the squealing brake on her car. Before Carlos left, he'd handled those things. Not because she'd asked. Because he was there. Because that's what husbands did, or what her husband had done, or what she'd let him do until it became the shape of things.

Now Carlos was gone. Had been gone for seven months. And the shape hadn't changed. She'd just swapped one man for another. Her son instead of her husband. Same phone call. Same waiting. Same standing in the driveway with coffee while someone else climbed the ladder.

She looked at her hands. They were good hands. Strong from the yarn, the hooks, the needles. They could make a cardigan in eleven days. They could produce blankets that people paid real money for. They could hold a crochet hook for six hours straight without cramping if she stretched between rows.

They had never held a wrench. Not once in forty-two years.

She drank her coffee. She looked at the wall where the water had been. The dark stripe was already lighter. By tomorrow it would be a ghost.

She thought about the hardware store on Third. She'd driven past it a hundred times. She'd never been inside.

Maybe she would. Not today. Not for the gutter. But maybe for the next thing. Whatever the next thing was.

She put the mug in the sink and went back to her crochet. The blanket for Portland was due Wednesday. She counted stitches, found her place, and started the row. Her hands moved. The yarn moved. The hook caught and pulled, caught and pulled.

Outside, the wall was drying in the sun.
