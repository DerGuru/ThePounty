# Chapter 8: The Teenagers

*Tuesday, the 13th of August 2024*

Ms. Yuen's morning walk took her past the Henderson fence at 6:15, same as every Tuesday. The air was cool, the street empty, the oak at the corner still holding its nighttime silence. She was thinking about breakfast when she saw the red.

Three large letters on Mrs. Henderson's white picket fence. Freshly painted last April, ruined overnight. The letters didn't spell anything. They were practice marks. Someone learning to tag, not someone with something to say.

She stood in front of it for a moment. She looked at the angle of the spray, the drip patterns and the height. Whoever did this was short or crouching. The paint was cheap. Hardware store brand. The cap had been too close to the wood—thick lines, pooling at the bottom of each stroke.

Children. Learning.

She left her camera in the pocket. She continued her walk. The fence would still be there in an hour.

By eight, Sandra Hooper had photographed it from three angles and posted it to the neighborhood group chat. By eight-thirty, Gerald had replied with a timestamp and a request that everyone check their doorbell cameras. By nine, Diane Kessler had sent a message about property values.

Ms. Yuen read none of this. She didn’t have the group chat. She had a landline and a kitchen window that faced the street. That was sufficient.

She knew who had done it. Not because she’d seen them do it. Because she’d been watching this street for thirty-one years, and she knew what bored teenagers looked like two weeks before they became vandals.

Three boys. Fifteen, maybe sixteen. They came through the park every afternoon around four. They sat on the bench near the basketball court that no one used anymore. They had a speaker that played music she didn’t recognize. They left candy wrappers on the ground. Two weeks ago, one of them had started carrying a backpack that clinked when he set it down. Cans. Spray cans make a specific sound against each other.

She had noticed. She hadn’t said anything. It was not her fence.

The Henderson boy had moved out last year. Mrs. Henderson was seventy and didn’t get out much anymore. She might not see the fence for days unless someone told her.

Sandra would tell her. Sandra told everyone everything.

Ms. Yuen made tea. She sat by the kitchen window and watched.

At ten, Dave Ortega walked past with a coffee. He saw the fence, stopped, looked at it, shook his head and kept walking.

At eleven, Gerald walked past. He stopped longer. He crouched down. He looked at the paint. He stood up, looked left, looked right, and walked to the park entrance. He stood there for a full minute, scanning the empty basketball court. Then he went home. He would be writing something down now. Ms. Yuen was certain of that.

At two in the afternoon, Aldric came out of his house.

Ms. Yuen set her teacup down.

He was wearing his usual smile and walked his usual pace. Unhurried. He passed the fence. He stopped.

Something left his face. Ms. Yuen couldn't name it from this distance, but she could see the absence. The pleasantness that usually sat on his features like a default setting — gone. For three seconds, maybe four, the man standing in front of Mrs. Henderson's ruined fence was not the man who waved at neighbors and asked about their children. He was someone else. Someone looking at a problem the way you'd look at a stain on a shirt you intended to keep.

Then it passed. His shoulders settled. His chin lifted. He touched the paint with one finger, rubbed it between his thumb and forefinger, looked at the red on his skin, and wiped it on his handkerchief.

He looked at the park. Then he walked to the park.

Ms. Yuen watched him cross the street, pass through the gate, and follow the path toward the basketball court. The bench was empty now. Two in the afternoon. The boys came at four.

Aldric sat down on the bench. He produced a book from his coat pocket and started reading.

Ms. Yuen watched him for ten minutes. He didn’t move. She went back to her tea, refilled it and came back. He was still there. Reading.

At four-twelve, three figures appeared on the path from the south end of the park. Ms. Yuen could see them from her window. Baggy clothes. The one in front had the backpack. They were loud — she could hear the laughter from here, could see the way they walked, shoulders wide, claiming the path.

They saw Aldric on their bench.

They slowed. The laughter dropped. Nobody sat on their bench. That bench was understood to be theirs between four and dark. Everyone on this street knew that.

Aldric looked up from his book. He said something. Ms. Yuen could not hear what. One of the boys responded. Aldric said something else. Longer. He gestured to the bench beside him. An invitation.

The boy with the backpack sat down first. Then the other two. Aldric closed his book but kept his finger in the page.

He talked. They listened. One of them said something. Aldric nodded. He talked some more. He talked for a long time. Ms. Yuen could see his hands moving occasionally — not gesturing widely like Sandra, but precisely. Small movements. Pointing at things. The fence, perhaps. The park. The boys themselves.

At one point, the boy with the backpack opened it. He took something out. He showed it to Aldric. Aldric looked at it. He nodded. He said something.

The conversation lasted twenty-two minutes. Ms. Yuen timed it by the clock above her stove.

Then Aldric stood. He shook each boy's hand. Individually. With attention, with eye contact and with that small nod of his that made people stand straighter. The boys stood when he stood. Ms. Yuen noticed that. They stood up because he stood up.

He walked back across the park. Through the gate. Down the sidewalk. Past the fence without looking at it. Up his porch steps. Door closed.

The boys stayed on the bench for another ten minutes. Talking. Quieter now. Then they left, going south the way they’d come.

Ms. Yuen finished her tea, washed the cup, dried it and put it away.

She did not tell anyone what she’d seen. It was not finished yet.

The next morning, Wednesday, Ms. Yuen woke at six as always. She made tea as always. She sat at her window as always.

At seven-forty, a car she didn’t recognize pulled up near the Henderson fence. Old Mazda. Dented fender. A woman got out of the driver’s side. Forty, maybe. Tired face. She opened the trunk. Inside were two cans of white paint, a roller, a tray, and a pack of brushes. Still in the plastic. She left the trunk open and leaned against the car, arms crossed. Waiting.

At seven-fifty, the three boys came around the corner on foot. No backpack today. They were wearing old clothes — paint clothes. One of them had a baseball cap on backward. They walked to the car. The woman said something to the one in front. He nodded. She pointed at the fence. He nodded again.

She got back in the car and drove away.

The boys looked at the fence. They looked at each other. One of them opened a paint can. They worked.

Ms. Yuen watched them. They were not good at painting. The roller left streaks. One of them dripped white paint on the grass and tried to wipe it with his shoe, which made it worse.

But they worked. For two hours, they worked. They covered the red completely. They did the whole section of fence, not just the graffiti, because the new white didn’t match the old white exactly and the only way to fix that was to do the entire panel. They figured that out themselves, after the first coat dried patchy. Good. Learning.

At nine-forty, they finished. The paint was uneven in places. The brushwork at the top was sloppy. But the fence was white. The letters were gone.

One of the boys took out his phone. He took a picture. He put the phone away. They gathered the paint cans, the roller, the tray and the brushes. They put everything in a pile by the curb. They stood there for a moment, looking at the fence. Then they walked away. South, toward the park.

At ten, Aldric came out of his house. He walked past the fence. He stopped. He looked at it. He touched the paint — carefully, with one finger, at the edge where it was already dry. He nodded once. Small. Satisfied.

Then he walked to the pile of supplies by the curb. He took the roller and the brushes. Carried them back to his house. Ms. Yuen assumed he would clean them. The boys hadn’t cared to do that. Aldric would care.

At noon, Sandra came by. Ms. Yuen heard her through the open window.

“Did you see? Someone fixed the fence!”

“I saw,” Ms. Yuen said.

“Do you know who did it?”

“No.”

“Gerald thinks it was a city crew. But why would the city paint a private fence? I think it was Aldric. Did you see him go over there?”

“I didn’t see anything,” Ms. Yuen said.

Sandra looked at her for a moment. Sandra always looked at Ms. Yuen like she was trying to read a book in a language she didn’t speak. Then she smiled and moved on down the sidewalk, already pulling out her phone.

Ms. Yuen closed the window.

She thought about what she’d seen. She didn’t know what he’d said to them. She would never ask. It wasn’t her business, and more than that — she didn’t need to know. She had seen enough hands in her life to know what his did. They didn’t threaten. They didn’t plead. They offered something and let the other person pick it up.

Her husband had been like that, in his best years. Before the illness took the patience out of him. A man who could sit in a room and make other people want to be better without saying a word about being better.

Aldric was not her husband. He was younger, sharper, and there was something behind the patience that her husband had never had. Something that knew exactly what would happen if the patience ran out. But the skill was the same. The effect was the same.

Ms. Yuen made another cup of tea and sat back at her window. 

That evening, on her walk, she passed Aldric’s house. He was on the porch. The chair. The coffee. The lamp.

“Good evening, Ms. Yuen.”

“Good evening.”

She stopped.

“The fence looks nice,” she said.

“Does it? I hadn’t noticed.” He smiled.

“You notice everything.”

The smile stayed. He held the look a moment longer than politeness required.

“You give me too much credit,” he said.

“No,” Ms. Yuen said. “I don’t.”

She walked on. The evening was warm. The oak at the corner of Dave’s lot had its wood thrush again, singing the last notes of the day. Her knee was better today. 

The three boys had learned something yesterday. She didn’t know what. But she was fairly sure it would stay.
