# Chapter 31: The Faucet

*Saturday, the 4th of January 2025*

The leak had been there since November. Maria had noticed it the morning after she painted the fence, when she ran the cold tap to rinse her brushes and water came up around the base of the spout at the same time it came out the end. Not a stream. A weeping. The chrome darkened, a film spread under the collar, a small bead built up at the back where the metal met the deck. When she shut the tap off the weeping stopped. When she opened it again, cold or hot, either handle, even a quarter turn, the weeping came back. Every time. She had wiped it with a paper towel. Five minutes later, when she ran the tap to fill the kettle, the ring was back.

For six weeks she had kept a dishtowel folded on the edge of the sink and wiped the base of the spout dry every time she shut the water off. Wash a cup, wipe. Fill the kettle, wipe. Rinse a plate, wipe. The towel got wet through and she swapped it for a dry one. That was the system.

It was Saturday morning, the fourth of January, and the system had stopped being acceptable some time between Christmas and now. She wasn't sure when exactly. Maybe when she'd opened the wooden box on her porch and read the card. *For the next thing you decide to fix.* She had read it three times standing in her doorway in the cold. She had carried the box inside. She had set the screwdriver case on the kitchen counter where she could see it. She had not opened the case for ten days.

This morning she opened it.

The screwdrivers lay in a row in cut foam, eight of them, the handles the color of pale wood with a darker grain running through them. She picked up the smallest Phillips. The grip fit. It fit like a well-sized shoe, one you stopped noticing. She turned it in her hand, put it back in the foam and looked at the faucet.

She opened her laptop on the kitchen table and typed *kitchen faucet leaking at the base*. She watched the search results appear and chose the first video, because choosing the first video was a decision and she was going to make decisions today.

The man in the video had a beard, a baseball cap and a workshop behind him that suggested he had spent his entire life learning how to fix things. *Hey guys, today we're gonna show you how to replace the O-rings on a single-handle pull-out kitchen faucet, super easy job.* He held up something that looked nothing like anything on her sink.

She paused the video and looked at her faucet. It was a two-handle bridge type, brass under the chrome, fixed spout in the middle, no swivel, hot and cold to either side. It had been already installed in 1998 when she and Carlos bought the house and had never been replaced because there had been no reason to replace it. It had always worked.

She typed *two handle bridge faucet leaking at base of spout fixed spout.*

The third video was a woman in a sweatshirt who said the problem was almost always the gasket under the spout collar. *On these old fixed-spout types you've got a knurled retaining nut right at the base — that ring with the notches around it — and there's a flat rubber gasket inside that seals the spout down to the body. Rubber dries out, water starts weeping at the base. Twenty-year-old faucet, that's your guy nine times out of ten.* She held up a small black ring of rubber, flat as a coin. *Costs about a dollar. Whole job is fifteen minutes if you've got the right tools.*

Maria watched the whole video. She watched it again with a notebook open beside the laptop. She wrote: *1. shut water under sink. 2. unscrew the notched ring at base of spout (the woman called it a slip nut). 3. lift spout straight up. 4. replace gasket. 5. reverse.*

The woman in the video had used a flat tool with a curved hook on the end, which she called a spanner wrench. *If you don't have one, channel locks with a rag wrapped around the jaws works fine. Don't use regular pliers — you'll just chew up the chrome.*

Maria wrote: *channel locks. rag.*

She went under the sink.

There were two small valves down there, one on each pipe, but they had no handles. Just brass bodies set into the line, each with a recessed Phillips slot in the end where a knob should have been. She had never looked at them before. She wondered if they had ever had knobs, or if the house had simply been built this way. A builder's economy. A thing nobody was supposed to need to touch.

She came back up and took the second Phillips out of the foam, the one whose tip looked right for the slot. She went back under the sink, set the tip into the slot on the hot line and turned. The screwdriver fit. The valve turned a quarter, a half, three-quarters, and then it stopped, firm. She did the same on the cold line. She came up and tested the tap.

A small dribble, then nothing.

She stood at the sink for a moment with the screwdriver in her hand. She set it down on the counter beside the case. The tool had done its job.

The slip nut at the base of the spout was a chrome ring about an inch and a half across, with shallow notches cut into the rim, eight of them, evenly spaced. The notches were for the spanner wrench she did not have.

The pliers from the junk drawer were not the right kind. She knew that now. The woman in the video had been specific. But the hardware store was a twenty-minute drive each way and the pliers were here, and she thought maybe with a folded paper towel around the jaws she could just *try a little*.

She tried. She got the pliers onto the ring, paper towel between metal and chrome, squeezed, and turned. The pliers slipped. The paper towel kept them off the chrome, but they slid around the rim without catching, because the rim was round and the jaws were straight and there was nothing for them to bite. She tried again. Same result.

She stopped after the third try. The chrome was unmarked. Her hand was warm from squeezing. She hadn't made anything worse, but she had not moved the nut.

*I should call Jesús.*

She did not call Jesús.

She put the pliers down, put her coat on, took her keys, and walked to the car. She drove to the hardware store on the corner of Route 9 and Elm.

The hardware store on a Saturday morning in early January was almost empty. An older man at the register nodded at her without speaking. The fluorescent lights buzzed. She walked past the paint section, the lawn and garden, past the bird feeders, and stopped in the plumbing aisle in front of a wall of small parts in plastic bags.

A man in a green apron came around the end of the aisle. He was maybe thirty. He had the patient look of a man whose job involved explaining things to people who did not know what they needed.

“You looking for something specific?”

“A gasket.” She said it the way the woman in the video had said it. “For the base of a kitchen faucet spout. Two-handle, bridge type, fixed spout. There's a slip nut with notches around the rim, and a flat rubber gasket inside. The gasket is — ” she paused, “— probably original to the house.”

He nodded slowly, a nod that said the description had done most of his work for him. “You take it out yet?”

“No. I couldn't get the nut loose. I only had pliers.”

“Yeah, don't do that. You'd wreck the chrome.” He turned and walked two aisles over and she followed. “You want channel locks. Ten-inch'll do — that's the household size. Wrap the jaws in a rag and you can get a grip on those notches without marking anything up.” He took a pair off a peg. They were larger than she expected and heavier than they looked. The jaws opened wide enough to swallow her wrist.

“And the gasket?”

“Won't know the size till you've got it out. They're not all the same.” He thought for a second. “Take the assortment pack. Six gaskets, a couple different thicknesses. Two-fifty. One of them'll fit and you have a replacement ready, if you need another one somewhere else.”

He led her back to the plumbing wall and took down a small clamshell with flat black rubber rings in different sizes. He handed it to her and took down a roll of white tape from the next shelf.

“Teflon tape,” he said. “For the threads when you put it back. Three wraps, clockwise.”

“I saw that in the video.”

“Then you know.” He nodded at her at the register the same way the older man had nodded when she'd come in.

She paid and drove home.

She folded a clean dishtowel into a strip and wrapped it twice around the slip nut. She opened the channel locks wide and set the jaws over the towel. She squeezed the handles together and turned, counterclockwise, slow.

Nothing. She squeezed harder and set her shoulder into it. A thin metal squeak came out of the joint, the protest of something seized with twenty-eight years of mineral crust, and the nut moved.

A quarter turn, half, the dry small sound easing as the threads broke clean. She loosened her grip and turned again and after that it spun free. She backed it off with her fingers the rest of the way and slid it up the body of the spout.

She lifted the spout straight up.

It came out of the deck with a small wet release, the brass nipple underneath wet and dull. Sitting in the recess at the top of the body was a flat black ring of rubber. Or what had been a flat black ring of rubber. It was hard and had the color of old asphalt. When she put her thumbnail under the edge it did not lift. It cracked.

It came out in pieces. She picked up the first half-moon. But as she lifted the second part, she felt it go. A soft tick against brass, and when she looked down the piece was gone, down inside the body of the faucet, in the brass throat where the water came up. She bent her face close. The hole was dark.

She straightened up and went back to the recess. She coaxed out the rest with the corner of a screwdriver, two more crescents and a scatter of dry crumbs, and laid them on the counter. What had been a gasket was now a small pile of dry rubber.

She thought about leaving the piece in the throat. A piece of dead rubber the size of her thumbnail, somewhere down the line. Maybe it would flush through. Maybe it would lodge somewhere and split the stream into a hiss next week. She did not know enough to know which.

She went to the junk drawer and found the small flashlight she kept there for power outages. She went to the bathroom and took the tweezers off the shelf above the sink, the long thin ones she used for splinters.

She came back to the kitchen, flashlight in her left hand and aimed it down into the throat of the faucet body. The brass walls lit up, ringed with limescale, and at the bottom, against one side, the dark crescent of rubber lay flat on the metal. She moved the tweezers in slowly, as carefully as she had once worked a splinter from Jesus's thumbnail at four, when he would not hold still. The tips touched. They closed on the edge of the rubber. She lifted.

The piece came up clean. She laid it on the counter with the others.

She looked into the throat again with the flashlight. Empty. The brass walls, the dark of the line below. Nothing visible.

She had no way of knowing what size the gasket had been, not from the crumbs. But she had six sizes in the clamshell, and one of them had to be close enough.

She got a small bowl and filled it with white vinegar from the cabinet under the sink. She dropped the brass nipple from the bottom of the spout in and left it there. She took a Q-tip and a paper towel and worked at the recess where the gasket had sat, at the threads on the nipple where the slip nut went, at every surface the rubber had touched. The white crust came off in small flakes. Underneath, the brass was darker than she had expected, the color of old coins.

After ten minutes she lifted the spout out of the bowl and rinsed it. The limescale was gone. The brass colloring as good as new.

She held the clamshell up against the recess and picked the gasket closest in diameter, slightly thicker than the cracked pieces when laid flat. She seated it in the recess. She pressed it down with her fingertip until it sat flush.

She lowered the spout back into the deck, straight down, until the nipple was seated and the chrome flange rested against the body. She wrapped a piece of Teflon tape around the exposed threads, three times, clockwise, because that was what the man at the store had said and what the woman in the video had said. She threaded the slip nut down by hand until it stopped, then set the channel locks back over the towel-wrapped ring and tightened it the last quarter turn, snug, not gorilla-tight, as the woman in the video had said.

She went under the sink with the Phillips and opened both valves.

She came up and looked at the faucet.

The base was dry.

She ran the cold tap, then the hot, then both together, hard, for a full minute. She turned them off. She put her palm flat on the counter beside the spout and held it there. The counter was dry. The base of the spout was dry. 

She sat down at the kitchen table.

The laptop was still open. The woman in the sweatshirt was paused mid-sentence, mouth open, holding the spanner wrench Maria had not bought. The screwdriver case was open beside it, one screwdriver missing from its slot. The channel locks were on the counter beside the old pliers.

She hadn't called Jesús. She hadn't called anyone.

She got up and made coffee. She drank it standing at the counter, looking at the faucet that was no longer weeping at the base.

She wanted to feel something larger than she was feeling. She wanted the moment to mean what she suspected it meant. But it was just a quiet Saturday morning, and the coffee was hot, and the kitchen was warm, and the chrome at the base of the spout was dry, and that was it. That was the whole thing.

She finished the coffee. She washed the cup. She put the screwdriver back in its slot in the foam, closed the case, and laid the channel locks on top. The pliers went back in the junk drawer.

On the way past the window she glanced down the street toward number four, past the dark windows of number six. Aldric's porch was empty. The candle in the front window was not lit. Whatever he did on the days after Christmas, he was still doing it, and she did not need to know what it was.

She went into the living room and took her knitting out of the basket and sat down on the couch. A cardigan for the customer in Portland. Three more rows before the sleeve increase. Her hands knew the pattern. She did not have to think about it.

She thought about the faucet.

She thought: *I did that.*

It wasn't a big thought. It didn't go anywhere. It just sat in her chest where the wiping had been this morning, and it took up about the same amount of room.

But it would not let her sit. After a few rows she set the knitting down and went back to the kitchen.

She had crossed it a thousand times under the brown scallop on the ceiling, where the upstairs tub had been bleeding into the plaster since September. She had looked at it the way she looked at weather, as a thing that happened to her. Now, with the faucet dry behind her, she looked at it the way she had looked at the faucet that morning. A thing with a cause. A cause with a fix.

The laptop was still open on the table. She searched the tub, the joint, and the seal under the tile. The same woman in the sweatshirt came up, bookmarked after the faucet without Maria quite deciding to. Old caulk, dried and split, letting water creep behind the wall. Take all of it out. The new bead is the easy part.

She wrote the list at the bottom of the same page. Silicone, kitchen and bath. A cheap gun. A plastic scraper. Tape. She was still buoyed enough to put her coat back on and drive the eleven minutes before the feeling could talk her out of it. She knew the store now. She did not need the man in the green apron to walk her down the aisles. She found the silicone, the gun, the scraper, and the blue tape. She paid and drove home with the bag on the seat beside her.

By the time she carried it inside, the lift had gone out of the day.

She laid the things on the kitchen table in the order she would use them. Scraper, razors, two tubes of silicone, the gun, the tape. She would start after lunch. She did not start after lunch. She made a sandwich. She picked up the cardigan. The light went gray at the windows and the tools stayed where she had put them, untouched.

She knew this about herself now, in a way she hadn't before this morning. Her hands needed the tools on the table a while before they believed the work was hers. The faucet had waited ten days on the counter before she opened the case. The tub would wait too. That was all right. She had done the one thing today. The other was bought and paid for and sitting on her own table, hers, waiting only for her hands to catch up to what she already knew.
