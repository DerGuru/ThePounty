# Chapter 13: The Absence

*Thursday, the 19th of September 2024*

Dave almost forgot about the mail.

It was Thursday evening, and Marcus was screaming because Lina had taken the remote, and Lina was screaming because Marcus had bitten her hand, and Rosa was in the kitchen saying something about consequences that neither of them were hearing. Dave was standing in the hallway with his work bag still over his shoulder, keys still in his hand, shoes still on. He had not moved past the threshold. The noise had caught him like a net.

"Lina, give him the remote."

"He *bit* me!"

"Marcus, we don't bite."

"She took it!"

"I know she took it. Lina, give it back. Marcus, apologize."

"But—"

"Now."

The remote changed hands. Marcus mumbled something that was technically an apology. Lina held up her hand to show teeth marks that were barely visible. 

Dave set his bag down and took off his shoes. Then he remembered. The mail.

He went back outside in his socks, crossed the lawn, and walked up Aldric's driveway. The house was dark. All of it. Not just the porch lamp, which Aldric always turned on at dusk whether he was sitting outside or not. The windows too. No kitchen light behind the curtain. No reading lamp in the front room. The house sat there looking at the street with blank eyes.

Dave opened the mailbox. Three envelopes and some flyers. No packages today. He tucked them under his arm and walked back.

The porch was empty. The two chairs were still there, and the small table between them. The coffee cup was gone, which meant Aldric had put it away before leaving. Of course he had.

It was strange how quiet the house looked. Not abandoned — nothing was wrong, nothing seemed out of place. Just off. Like a stage between shows. Everything set up, nobody performing.

Dave went back inside. He put Aldric's mail on the kitchen counter, next to yesterday's stack.

"What's that?" Rosa said. She was chopping onions with a speed that suggested she was still angry about the biting.

"Aldric's mail. He asked me to grab it while he's gone."

"Right." The knife kept moving. "When does he get back?"

"Saturday, he said."

Rosa nodded. The onions went into the pan. Oil hissed.

After the children were in bed and Dave was watching something he'd already forgotten the title of, Rosa stood at the upstairs window.

She had not meant to end up here. She had gone to check on Marcus, who sometimes kicked off his blanket in his sleep, and then she had passed the window on her way back to the bedroom and stopped.

Aldric's house was dark. She had seen it dark before — late at night, when the porch lamp was the last thing to go off, always at ten-fifteen. But she had never seen it dark like this. Empty dark. The kind of dark that meant no one was inside to turn anything on.

It shouldn't have mattered. People went on business trips. People left for two days. This was not unusual.

Rosa stood at the window and tried to name what felt different. Not about the house. About the street.

On Monday, when Aldric had still been here, Gerald's daughter had come over to return a casserole dish. Marcus had played in the front yard until dark. The Greens' dog had barked at seven, as it always did, and Mr. Green had called it inside. Mrs. Chen had waved at someone while getting the mail. Normal. All of it normal.

Today, Marcus had played in the backyard. The casserole dish was still on the counter because Rosa hadn't seen anyone to return it to. She had not spoken to a single neighbor. Not one. She had not even gone to the front porch.

She could not explain why those things were connected to Aldric's absence. They weren't, probably. It was a Thursday. People stayed inside on Thursdays. It was getting cooler in the evenings. There were reasons.

But the street felt like a room where someone had turned the music off. You didn't notice the music until it stopped. You didn't realize it had been holding something together until the pieces drifted apart.

She looked at his driveway. Empty. She looked at his porch. The two chairs, the small table. The newspaper holder by the door, empty. No shoes drying on the step. No coffee smell carrying in the morning air. No wave from across the lawn when she took the kids to school.

It was just two days. He would be back tomorrow.

Rosa went back to the bedroom. Dave was still watching something downstairs. She could hear the murmur of it through the floor. She lay down on her side of the bed and pulled the blanket up.

She thought about how quickly it had happened. Nearly three months. Aldric had been here nearly three months, and the street had rearranged itself around him without anyone deciding to let it. He hadn't asked for anything. He hadn't organized anything, except his housewarming party. And even then the neighbors had brought most of the food. He had been present. That was all. Present and warm and paying attention, and something about that combination had made everyone else more present too.

And now two days without him, and the street was just a street again. Houses next to houses. People inside their walls. Quiet.

Rosa closed her eyes.

Saturday morning, Dave looked at Aldric's mail with the others on the kitchen counter. The pile was small — two days' worth of mail for a man who didn't seem to get much.

He glanced at the return addresses out of habit, then stopped himself. That was Gerald's department, not his.

At seven-forty, backing out of his driveway, Dave looked at number four one more time. The house was the same. Neat. Quiet. Blinds closed. The lawn still green from the last time Aldric had mowed it, three days ago. Everything maintained. Everything in order.

He turned left at the end of the block. The same way Aldric had gone.

Rosa was folding laundry in the upstairs bedroom when she heard the car. It was just past seven, the light outside turning amber.

She did not run to the window, but she carried the shirt she was folding to the upstairs hallway, where the window faced the street, and looked out.

The green Volvo was pulling into the driveway at number four. It stopped. The engine went off. Aldric stepped out, opened the trunk, and took out a dark canvas bag. He closed the car and walked to his porch. Same steps. Same unhurried pace. He unlocked the front door and went inside.

Five seconds later, the porch light came on.

Rosa finished folding the shirt. She smoothed the collar flat and set it on the pile.

Within the hour, she knew, someone would walk past his house. Not on purpose. Just a walk. Just fresh air. And Aldric would be on the porch, or in the garden, and he would say hello, and they would say hello back, and the street would sound like itself again.

She took the laundry downstairs. Marcus was playing with blocks in the living room. The evening light came through the front window, low and golden.

"Marcus, you want to go say hi to Mr. Venn? We should bring his mail over."

Marcus looked up. "Can I ring the doorbell?"

"Yes."

"Two times?"

"One time."

"Okay." He was already on his feet.

Rosa gathered the stack of envelopes from the counter. Three days of mail. She carried them to the door and held them against her hip while Marcus put his shoes on. Left foot, right foot. Velcro, not laces. He was getting faster at it.

They walked across the lawn together. The street was waking up again, and Rosa did not think about why.

She just noticed that it was.
