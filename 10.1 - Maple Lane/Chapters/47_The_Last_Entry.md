# Chapter 47: The Last Entry

*Monday, the 28th of April 2025*

There had been a flash in a stranger's car on Friday night. Aldric Venn had been inside the car, and Gerald could not stop thinking about it.

He had the blue notebook open on the desk, turned to Friday's page. He had read this page on Saturday, with the gray car already gone from the curb at number six and not coming back. He had read it Sunday, when it still had not come back. He was reading it now, his coffee going cold at his elbow. The reading had stopped giving anything back several readings ago. He read it again anyway. It was what he did with a page that would not balance.

*10:58 p.m. Gray Hyundai arrives.*

*11:30 p.m. Venn comes out, down to the car. In on the passenger side.*

*11:50 p.m. Flash. One. No sound.*

*11:52 p.m. Passenger door. Venn out, into the dark. Trunk up, a small case, trunk down.*

*11:54 p.m. Driver door. Venn brings the watcher out into the road. Right arm held against his chest. Wrong.*

*12:00 a.m. Venn works at the arm. Can't see how. It comes back bound. A sling. The watcher stands and lets him do it.*

*12:02 a.m. Venn in the driver's seat now. Bent to the console by the wheel. Hand moving over something. Can't make it out.*

*12:03 a.m. Venn reaches across to the far side of the floor. Comes up with something small. Puts it inside his coat.*

*12:05 a.m. Venn eases the watcher back into the seat. Leans over him. Careful with him.*

*12:06 a.m. Venn puts out his hand. Driver hesitates. They shake.*

*12:07 a.m. Venn back on his own lawn. The car pulls off under the limit. Stops at the corner.*

*12:07 a.m. Venn on the porch. Raises a hand to the car as it goes.*

He read it through once more, top to bottom, as he had read a dozen times between. Every line was a thing he had seen with his own eyes from his own window and set down in his own hand while it was happening, in the order it had happened. Not one of them was a guess. And not one of them, read back, gave him anything but another question.

The first question forked, and both forks were bad. Had Venn known the man in the car? If he had, he had known for days. He had watched the gray car move from spot to spot, marked it as he marked everything on his street, and done nothing, and let it sit outside the empty house until Friday night. Gerald could not think why a man waits like that, not if Venn knew the man. But if he had not known the driver, then Venn had come out of his house at half past eleven and let himself into a stranger's car in the dark and sat down in it as though he had been asked, which no man does, and at the end of it had stood on the curb and waved the car off down the street like a guest he was sorry to lose. Either the man had waited for days and picked his night, or he had got into a stranger's car at midnight and come out of it waving.

The rest came smaller and were no smaller for it. What was the flash? What had Venn carried up out of the trunk in that case, and what was the small thing he had taken off the floor of the car and put away inside his coat? The case he could nearly explain. A man with an arm to bind wants a kit, and the trunk is where a careful man keeps one. The thing from the floor he could not explain at all, nor what Venn had been doing bent over the console with his hand working, in a car that was not his. And under all of them sat the question that held the rest up: who was the man, and what had he driven onto Maple Lane to do?

That was how the page read now. It settled nothing and raised more each time he went down it, as a total you were sure of breeds new lines the longer you hold your eye to it. He left it open and looked out at the street, which gave him an ordinary Monday morning, because that was all it had.

Aldric came out at seven with his mug and book and the cardigan Maria had made him. He sat in the right-hand chair, set the mug down and opened the book to the bookmark. Gerald watched him do it and waited for the tell, the flick of the eyes to the dark end of the street, the half-second pause in the hand. There was nothing. A man was reading on his porch in the cold. Whatever had been in him three days ago, standing at his rail and not looking at a car, had gone out of him, leaving behind the man it had been borrowing.

Gerald wrote it down. *Monday. 7:00. Venn on the porch, as ever. Nothing in him. Nothing to see.* The pen moved before he had decided to let it. A nothing on the morning after a Friday that would not add was no longer a nothing; it was one more line in the same open account, and he was in no position to leave a line out, not until he had the one that made the rest resolve.

The week put itself back together in small pieces, and Gerald, who had spent eleven months cataloging small pieces, cataloged these too. He could not do otherwise. Somewhere in the ordinary days was the piece that explained the day that had not been ordinary, and a man did not find it by deciding in advance which days were worth the pen.

On Tuesday, Rosa Ortega came out at midday with a dish held flat in both hands and a towel over the top of it, and went up the steps to number four. She did not knock and wait as a stranger would. She set it on the table by the chair, said something through the screen door that Gerald could not hear, and came back down. By the time she reached her own gate she was already calling for Marcus, who had gotten himself up the low branch of the maple again and could not get down. Nothing in it. A woman had taken a hot dish to a neighbor on a Tuesday. Gerald kept a page for the comings and goings at number four. He opened it and wrote her down. Innocent, all of it, and onto the page even so, because he had stopped trusting himself to know in the moment which small thing would turn out to be the one.

On Wednesday Dave Ortega sat on Aldric's steps for half an hour with a mug of his own, the two of them talking about nothing Gerald could hear, Dave laughing once, loud, as he always did. An oil stain on the knee of his jeans. A normal man on a normal step. The street had a hundred mornings like it stored up from before the gray car, and here was one more, laid down on top of the others as though the car had never sat at the end of the road at all.

And on Thursday, Maria Alvarez painted her windows.

She had run a blue tape along the muntins of the front windows and laid newspaper on the sills. She was working a narrow brush down the edge where the frame met the glass, the hard line, the one that took patience. White paint. The frames had been white already since before Easter. He had that in the black notebook, where he kept the things that were only the street being the street. This was the second coat. The one that went on after the first had cured, that you did not strictly need, that made the difference between a job you did again in two years and one that held for eight.

She worked alone. That was the part his eye stayed on. No truck at the curb. No son with a radio and a six-pack on the drive. Nobody telling her how. She dipped the brush, wiped it on the lip of the can and drew it down the frame in one slow pull. She also cut the edges in with the small brush turned sideways. When she finished a window she stepped back and looked at it, satisfied.

Sandra had heard that the divorce had gone through. Quietly, in the end. No fight left in it by the time it came. The house was Maria's now, the whole of it, in her name, the mortgage paid. The fence she had painted in November and the white window frames. All of it, hers now.

Gerald watched her tape and paint and step back, alone, the whole morning, and felt something he had no column for. Not the tight thing behind the sternum that had lived there since September. Its opposite, whatever that was called. He had spent the better part of a year certain that the man at number four was a danger wearing a neighbor's face. He had never once written down that the same man had stood at this woman's fence on a Saturday in November and taught her how to hold a brush, and that she was putting her own house in order with her own two hands and needing no one to come and do it for her, because of it.

On Friday he went out for the mail. The day was mild and the street had gone quiet, as it did mid-morning once the cars were gone. Aldric was out at the foot of his own drive, a few letters in his hand at his box. Ms. Yuen came along the sidewalk on her round, slow and carrying nothing, as she had gone morning and evening since before Gerald moved in across from her. She slowed as she drew level with number four.

Gerald, at his own box, caught it across the quiet street the way he caught everything, without meaning to.

“You came back,” Ms. Yuen said.

Aldric stopped what he was doing. Just for a moment, the smallest hitch in him, before he said something low and pleasant that Gerald did not get.

“That's all I asked of you,” she said, and the round carried her on past number four and down toward the Ortegas' house.

Gerald stood at his mailbox with three pieces of mail in his hand. *You came back.* Back from where. The man's car had been in its drive every morning he had looked, even on Saturday it had been back before noon. 

He carried it back up the walk with the mail. Whatever account she had just settled with the man, she had settled it in passing, on her way down the street. He had worked at his own for eleven months and still could not make balance. He set the mail on the hall table. Then he went up to the study and took the notebook out. He opened it, not to a fresh page this time but to the front, to June, to the first line he had ever written about Aldric Venn.

*June 25. Three boxes. Standard trailer, far too big for three boxes. Who moves in with three boxes?*

He read forward. Eleven months of it. The deliveries he had counted off in September. The two days the man had driven away and come back from as though he had only gone to the store. The name that returned nothing. The file that had been too clean, and the four hundred dollars he had paid a man named Pruitt to be told so. The hedge cut on a day it did not need cutting. The flash.

He had been right. That was the thing he kept arriving at, and it kept refusing to bring the ease that being right was supposed to bring. He had been right about all of it. The man was not what he said he was. The quiet was a built thing. Every word of it true, and none of it the danger he had spent a year bracing the street against, which did not come. 

He took the pen from his pocket. A page like this wanted a line at the bottom of it, the figure that totaled the rest and let a man rule off and turn to something else.

He did not have it. That was the whole of the trouble, and the pen knew it before he did. You could not write a closing line on an account that would not reconcile, and this one would not, because its largest entry, the Friday entry, was a row of things he had watched and not one thing he understood. He turned instead to a clean page, wrote the date, and set the questions under it, numbered, in his neat hand. There was a figure still outstanding. The auditor in him had it by the collar before the rest of him could pretend otherwise. You did not close a book with an entry still open, and one entry was still open, and it drove a cruiser.

It posted itself on a Saturday in the second week of May.

Gerald was at the window with his coffee, not watching, only standing where the light was good, when a car he did not know came up Maple Lane from the east. Not the cruiser. A small blue Honda a few years old, a dent in the rear door, the kind of car a person owned rather than drove for the township. It came at the speed an ordinary car came, and he had it logged before he knew he was logging it, because eleven months did not wash out of a man in two weeks.

It was Kim. Out of the uniform, in a sweatshirt, one hand loose at the top of the wheel. A day off. Her own car, her own time, going wherever a person goes on a Saturday with nothing owed to anyone.

Aldric was on the porch.

He looked up. He raised a hand.

Kim raised hers off the top of the wheel, an easy thing, no weight in it, none of the extra beat Gerald had underlined in the blue notebook five weeks before. And then she did the thing that closed the page. She did not slow. She did not look twice at number four to see what it might give up. She drove on, past the house, turned at the corner, and was gone about her Saturday.

Gerald stood at the window a while longer.

For five weeks the cruiser had come up this street because there was something on it that would not settle, a figure she could feel and could not write down. Now she had come up it in her own car, on her own day, and not stopped, because there was nothing left on the street to stop for. The account stood at zero. Nothing owed, in either direction. It was closed on her side, and on the street's, and on every side but the one that was his.

He went back to the desk and stood over the blue notebook, the worn cover, the rubber band he had put around it in January when it began to swell with clippings. He thought about what a man did with an account that would not reconcile. He did not put it away. You did not put away an open book. You kept it where your hand fell on it, and you left the last line blank, because a blank line was not the end of a thing. It was the place the answer went, when it finally arrived.

So he did not slide the notebook into the drawer beneath the tax folders, where finished things went. He set it on the desk, square to the edge, the pen laid across it. The blue one was nearly full. He would need another soon, as he had needed this one when the black one filled, and the black one when an emptier question had filled that. He had been right for eleven months, and being right had bought him nothing but the certainty that there was more to Aldric Venn than meets the eye. Whatever the man across the street was, Gerald did not have it yet. He would keep at it until he did.

He went downstairs.

Sandra was at the table with the paper and the second cup she always poured and he always came down too late to drink. She looked up when he sat. She had been looking up like that for the better part of a year, at a man who came to the table gray and somewhere else, and she did not look surprised to find him the same today.

“You didn't sleep,” she said. Not a question. After forty years she did not bother to make it one.

“Not much.”

“You never do, lately.” She said it without an edge, went back to her paper, and let him keep whatever he was keeping, as she had let him keep it all year.
