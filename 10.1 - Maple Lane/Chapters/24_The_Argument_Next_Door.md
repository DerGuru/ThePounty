# Chapter 24: The Argument Next Door

*Monday, the 18th of November 2024*

Monday evening.

Dave stood at the kitchen counter, sorting through the mail. Electric bill. Coupon mailer. Lina's report card. He opened it with his thumb and held it under the light. Three Bs, two Cs, and one D. The D was in math.

“It's not my fault,” Lina said. “Mrs. Patterson doesn't explain anything. She just puts it on the board and expects us to get it. And the textbook is from like 2019. And I sit next to Tyler, who talks the entire time.”

“Long division isn't the teacher's fault,” Rosa said.

“It's not *my* fault.”

“Screen time is done until your math is back up.”

“That's not fair.”

“Until further notice.”

Lina looked at Dave. He shrugged. “She'll figure it out.”

Rosa didn't answer. Lina's door closed with the kind of precision that was louder than any slamming would have been. The rest of the evening was silence.

Tuesday morning. 

Dave opened the dishwasher after the cycle finished. The glasses were cloudy. The plates had a white film on them, gritty when he ran his thumb across the surface. The rinse cycle had made a sound like something small and metal was loose inside, tumbling with the water.

He pulled the bottom rack out, knelt on the kitchen floor, and unscrewed the spray arm. Gray goo had build up inside the nozzles. He cleaned the filter with an old toothbrush, rinsed everything under the tap, and put it back together. He ran another cycle. He stood in the kitchen for forty-five minutes, listening.

The glasses came out cloudy. The film was still there. He called a repair service. Earliest appointment: Thursday.

Wednesday. 

Marcus threw up at kindergarten, so Rosa picked him up. He threw up again in the car and a third time on the couch while Rosa was still getting the bucket. Dave came home from work to the smell of Lysol and the sound of his wife not talking to him. He asked what was wrong. She said nothing. He asked again. She said nothing louder.

Thursday.

The repair guy came. He looked at the dishwasher for ten minutes and said the filter housing needed replacing. He'd order the part. A hundred and eighty dollars plus labor. Dave agreed and the guy left. The dishwasher sat there, open, useless. Rosa washed the dinner plates by hand and didn't look at him.

Friday. 

Marcus was better. Lina was still not talking to Rosa. Rosa was still not talking to Dave, but for different reasons. Dave was talking to everyone and getting nothing back. He felt like a man broadcasting into a room where all the radios were off.

By Saturday morning, the Ortega household had settled into a cold, efficient silence. The kind where everyone knew where the cereal was and nobody asked anyone to pass it.

Dave took the trash. It was mid-November, cool but dry, the kind of morning that felt good if you kept moving. He set the can at the curb and looked at his house from the outside. The lights were on. The curtains were open. It looked like a house where people lived. It didn't look like a house where four people were navigating around each other like furniture.

Aldric was on his porch. Coffee. The cardigan. Saturday morning position. He raised his mug when he saw Dave.

“Morning,” Dave said.

“Good morning, Dave. How's the week been?”

Dave laughed. It came out wrong. Too sharp, too fast. “Long.”

Aldric looked at him. That look he had sometimes. Not studying. Not analyzing. Just looking, like a doctor before asking where it hurts. Except Aldric didn't ask.

“Do you have a moment?” Aldric said. “I could use a hand with something.”

Dave didn't have a moment. He had dishes to wash by hand because the dishwasher was still broken and the repair guy's part hadn't arrived yet. He had a daughter who needed a math conversation. He had a wife who needed something he couldn't figure out.

“Sure,” he said. Because the alternative was going back inside, and inside was where all the silence lived.

Aldric's garage was open. Dave had been in it twice before. It was organized like everything else about Aldric. Tools on pegboard, labeled. Shelves with clear bins. A workbench with a vice and a lamp. The Volvo wasn't inside. It was in the driveway, which meant the garage was being used for something.

There was a shelf unit on the workbench. Not assembled yet. Flat-pack, but not the cheap kind. Solid wood, dovetail joints, the kind of thing you'd find in a catalog that didn't print prices.

“I bought this for the study,” Aldric said. “But I'm not certain about the configuration. The room is narrow. If I build it as the instructions suggest, the bottom shelf will be too low for the items I need to store.”

“What items?”

“Books, mostly. Some boxes.”

Dave looked at the pieces. The wood was good. Walnut, maybe. The joints were precise, pre-cut, ready to glue and clamp. “What's the ceiling height?”

“Eight feet, two inches.”

“And you want the bottom shelf higher?”

“Fourteen inches off the ground instead of six. For ventilation and cleaning access.”

Dave pulled out a piece. Turned it over. The pre-drilled holes were set for the standard configuration. Moving the bottom shelf would mean new holes. He said so.

“I have a drill press,” Aldric said. He gestured toward the corner of the workbench. There it was. A drill press. In a home garage. Because of course.

They worked for an hour. Dave measured. Aldric drilled. They fitted the test pieces together and Dave held them while Aldric checked the alignment with a level. They talked about shelf spacing, load-bearing, and whether walnut needed sealing or if the natural oil was sufficient.

Lina's math grade never came up. Neither did the dishwasher. Dave's hands were moving and his shoulders were dropping and his breathing had changed from the tight, shallow rhythm he'd been carrying all week to something slower.

At ten-thirty, Aldric made coffee. He brought it to the garage on a tray. Two mugs and a small plate of crackers.

“The dishwasher,” Dave said. He hadn't planned to say it. “Repair guy came Thursday. Said it needs a new filter housing. Hundred and eighty plus labor.”

“Which model?”

“Bosch. The 500 series.”

“The part is twenty-six dollars online. The 2021 uses a revised filter basket, eight millimeters deeper than the 2019. The part number has a dash-R suffix.” Aldric sipped his coffee. “It takes ten minutes to replace. I can show you if you'd like.”

Dave stared at him. “How do you know that?”

“I replaced mine in April.”

“You have a Bosch 500?”

“I have the 300. But the filter assembly is cross-compatible between the 300 and 500 series from 2020 onward. Different door springs, same internals.”

“That's... very specific knowledge.”

“I spent two hours on a forum before I ordered. I dislike returning things.”

Dave drank his coffee. He thought about asking how Aldric knew all that stuuf. Dishwasher repairs, woodworking, and the exact wine that went perfectly with a meal. He didn't ask. That was the thing about Aldric. The questions were always there, but the answers never mattered more than the moment you were in.

They finished the shelf by noon. It stood against the garage wall, temporary, waiting to be moved into the study. The bottom shelf was at fourteen inches. The alignment was perfect. Dave stepped back and looked at it.

“Looks good,” he said.

“It does. Thank you.”

“You didn't really need my help.”

Aldric tilted his head. “I needed a second pair of hands for the clamping. And a second opinion on the spacing.” A pause. “And the company.”

Dave looked at him. Aldric was cleaning the drill press, wiping it down with a cloth, putting the bits back in their case. His face was neutral. Just a man who'd said a thing that was true because it was true.

“I should get back,” Dave said. “Rosa's probably wondering.”

“Of course.” Aldric set the cloth down. “I'll send you the part number. Twenty-six dollars. You'll have it by Tuesday.”

“Yeah. That'd be good.”

Dave walked back to his house. Thirty feet of driveway and sidewalk. The air was still cool but it didn't bother him the same way. His hands smelled like walnut and wood glue.

He went inside. Marcus was on the couch watching something with dinosaurs. Lina was in her room. Rosa was at the kitchen table with her laptop and a cup of coffee that had stopped steaming.

“Where were you?” she said. Not angry. Tired.

“Aldric's. He needed help with a shelf.”

“He needed help?” She said it flat. Like the concept was amusing but she was too exhausted to laugh.

“Yeah. Also, the dishwasher. Aldric says the part's twenty-six bucks online. I can do it myself.”

“Of course he did.”

Dave sat down across from her. The kitchen table. The same table where they'd eaten dinner every night. The same table where they'd sat after Marcus was born and Rosa had cried for no reason and Dave had held her hand and said nothing because nothing was the right thing.

“It's been a bad week,” he said.

Rosa looked at him. Her eyes were red at the edges. Not crying. Just tired. The kind of tired that didn't come from lack of sleep.

“I know,” she said.

“I'm sorry about the dishwasher. I should have just fixed it from the start.”

“I don't care about the dishwasher, Dave.”

He waited. She waited. The dinosaur show played in the other room. Lina's music leaked from upstairs, thin and muffled.

“I care that the dishwasher's been broken all week and you agreed to a hundred and eighty dollars without asking me. I care that Lina's failing math and you said 'she'll figure it out.' I care that Marcus was sick and you came home and asked me what was wrong like I was supposed to have one answer.” She stopped. Breathed. “I'm not mad at you. I'm tired of being the one who has to say 'this is what's wrong' when you should be able to see it.”

Dave sat with that. He didn't defend himself. He didn't explain.

“Okay,” he said.

“Okay?”

“I'll cancel the repair guy. The part's twenty-six dollars. Aldric said he'd show me how to do it.” He paused. “And I'll talk to Lina. About math. Not the grade. The actual math.”

Rosa looked at him. Something in her face shifted. Not a lot. Not a smile. Just the tightness loosening by a degree.

“Thank you,” she said.

Dave reached across the table. Took her coffee mug. It was cold. He got up, poured it out, and made her a fresh one. He didn't say anything while he did it. He set it down and sat back across from her.

Rosa wrapped her hands around the mug. The steam rose between them.

That evening, after the kids were in bed, Dave sat on the back porch with a beer. The yard was dark. The neighbor's security light threw a pale wedge across the grass. From here he could see the back of Aldric's house, two lots over. The study window was lit. A warm glow behind curtains.

The shelf was probably in there by now. Ready for books and boxes and whatever else Aldric kept in his study.

Dave drank his beer. He thought about the morning. About the way Aldric had said “and the company” like it was the most important item on a list of three.

He finished his beer and went inside. Rosa was reading, already in bed. He brushed his teeth and got in beside her.

“Aldric says hi,” he said. Which wasn't true. Aldric hadn't said hi. But it was the kind of thing you said.

“Tell him thanks for the part number,” Rosa said without looking up from her book.

“I will.”

She turned a page. He turned off his lamp. The house was quiet but it was a different quiet now. Not the silence of people avoiding each other. The silence of people who'd said the hard thing and come out the other side.

Dave closed his eyes.

The part arrived Tuesday. Aldric brought it over himself in a small padded envelope, knocked at five-thirty, and apologized for the timing. Rosa let him in. Dave was out getting some groceries.

“I won't stay. I know it's dinner soon.”

“Stay for coffee. Dave will be back in twenty minutes.”

“Another time. Thank you.” He set the envelope on the kitchen table beside Rosa's laptop. There was a library book open beside it. *A Tangle of Knots*. A bookmark stuck out the top, glossy cardstock, four young faces in pink foil. The book was open to a page with a chocolate cake on it — *V’s Mystery Fudge Cake*.

Aldric's eyes touched the page. They moved on.

“They use torx,” he said. “If Dave doesn't have one, I have a set. Tell him to come over.”

“I'll tell him.”

“Good evening, Rosa.”

He let himself out.

Rosa stood at the table for a moment with her hand on the envelope. Then she went back to her work.
