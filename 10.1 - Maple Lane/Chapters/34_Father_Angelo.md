# Chapter 34: Father Angelo

*Saturday, the 25th of January 2025*

Saturday started slow. Dave had taken Lina and Marcus to the indoor pool at the Y. The one good thing about January, he said, was that you could pretend it was summer for two hours if you had enough chlorine. Rosa had the house to herself for the first time in three weeks. She made a second cup of coffee, stood at the kitchen window for a few minutes and watched the snow that had fallen overnight. The heat rising from the radiator below the sill.

She was supposed to be doing the laundry, but she didn't feel like it. Today was her day.

On the counter sat the last jar of the apricot preserves she had put up in August. The small gift-size jars, the ones that Marcus had labeled, copying her prepared letters to the jar. She had been meaning to bring it over to Aldric for a week. She had not yet thanked him for the snowstorm yet. And there was only one way she ever thanked people for things — with food.

She put on her coat and crossed the strip of frozen sidewalk between her driveway and his.

Father Angelo was already on Aldric's porch.

He stood at the foot of the steps, looking up at the front door. The collar was unmistakable even at this distance, and so was the rest of him: the heavy black coat, the gray scarf and the leather gloves held in one hand. Father Michael Angelo, in person, on a Saturday morning, two streets from his own parish. With a name like that, Rosa had always thought, you had one of only two roads ahead of you. The cassock or the brush. He had picked the cassock. The hours were unpredictable, but it came with a reliable income. She stopped at the edge of the driveway.

“Rosa.” He turned. “Good morning.”

“Father.”

“I'm paying a call. Your neighbor was very generous with the path up to St. Catherine's after the storm last month. I never properly thanked him.” He raised his gloved hand toward the door, which had not opened. “I should have phoned first. I'm beginning to suspect I should have.”

Then the door opened.

Aldric was in the slate-blue cardigan today, the one Maria had knit him. A kitchen towel was folded over his shoulder. There was a faint dusting of flour at one cuff. He looked at Angelo first, then at Rosa. The smile he gave each of them was the same smile, neither warmer nor cooler. That was, Rosa had noticed before, his manner.

“Father Angelo. Rosa. Good morning to you both.”

“I should have phoned,” Angelo said again.

“You shouldn't have needed to. Please come in. I have coffee.”

“I can come back another time —”

“Rosa is here as well.” He stepped aside. “Three of us together is exactly the right number for a Saturday morning. Come in.”

Rosa held up the jar. “I brought you something. I can leave it and —”

He took the jar and tilted it so the small careful writing on the side caught the porch light. *MARCUS, AGE 5 WHEN HE WROTE THIS.* “No, please come in.”

They came in.

Aldric's kitchen was warm. It smelled of coffee and something baking, bread or close to it. The radio was on, low, set to the classical station out of New Haven. He turned it off when they sat down, with a small apology for having had it on at all.

“You weren't expecting company,” Angelo said.

“I'm always expecting company. Just not at any particular time.” He set three cups on the table, a small pitcher of cream and a bowl with sugar. He poured and sat. The coffee was the color Rosa liked, which she had stopped being surprised by months ago.

“To business, then,” Angelo said. He cleared his throat. “I owe you a thank-you. The path to the parish hall was clear when I arrived on the seventeenth. Clearer than my own walk at home, and I live there. Mrs. Daugherty said you had been by mid-morning.”

“It needed doing.”

“It did not need doing by you.”

“Someone was going to do it. I was up. It was simpler that way.” Aldric stirred his coffee once, set the spoon down, and looked at Father Angelo with the quiet he sometimes used with the children. “You are welcome, Father. Please drink your coffee before it cools.”

Angelo laughed, a short surprised laugh, and drank.

Rosa drank too. Aldric had not asked her how she took her coffee. He had simply made it as she took it. Black, a splash of cream, no sugar. She had not had to say a word. She wondered, briefly, when that had started, and then decided not to chase the question down.

The conversation went on. Father Angelo spoke at some length about the heating system at the church, which had failed twice during Advent, was now reportedly stable, but which he did not entirely trust. He spoke about Mrs. Daugherty, who was eighty-six and refused to use the elevator the parish had installed two years ago specifically with her in mind. He mentioned that Henry, the organist, had bronchitis again, and that the substitute on Sunday would be Henry's nephew, who was technically competent but had a tendency to add ornamentations that the hymnal did not strictly authorize.

“He flourishes,” Angelo said, with the air of a man making a serious complaint.

“That sounds like a kindness to Henry,” Aldric said. “Family covering for family.”

“It is. It is. I won't say a word to him about the flourishes.” He turned his cup in its saucer.

“The cold must thin the pews,” Aldric said.

“It does. Mostly the same faces in January as in November, only fewer of them. Though we've held on to a newcomer this year. A young woman, started coming in early May. Sits at the back, alone. Polite, observant, doesn't say much. Reminds me a bit of you, actually.”

He took another sip of his coffee, set the cup down with both hands and tapped the saucer once with his thumb. “Speaking of things at the back of the church. The boiler quote came in. It is higher than the vestry will authorize. So now we have to decide whether to patch it for one more winter or commit to a replacement.”

“Did the quote include the labor for removing the old unit?”

Angelo blinked. “I'd have to check.”

“That's usually where contractors pad the number.”

“Is it. Hm.” He pulled a small pad from his coat pocket and made a note. His thumb rested a moment too long on the saucer before he picked up the pen. Rosa noticed it. He had been visiting too many people this winter, she thought, and sleeping too little. “Thank you. I'll have Marjorie call them on Monday.”

He turned the pad over and tucked the pen back behind it. “And then there's the Sunday school enrollment. Down for the third year in a row. The finance committee keeps asking whether we couldn't rent the parish hall on weeknights to cover the shortfall. I am, in principle, opposed.”

“Yoga in the church basement,” Aldric said.

“Yoga in the church basement. Or worse. Pilates. There is a difference, apparently, though I have never had it explained to me in a way that I retained.”

Rosa drank her coffee. She liked listening to Father Angelo. He had the voice of a man who had read aloud for a living for thirty years. Measured, warm, with a small dryness at the edges that you noticed only when he was being funny on purpose. He was being funny on purpose about the finance committee now. She laughed. Aldric almost smiled.

“You should come on Sunday,” Angelo said, mildly. He was looking at his coffee. “Both of you. No pressure intended.”

“I'll think about it,” Rosa said, which was what she always said, which Father Angelo always accepted with the same small nod.

Aldric was a moment longer. “Thank you, Father. I appreciate the invitation.”

That was all he said. He left it there, neither one way nor the other, and Angelo nodded the same nod he had given Rosa.

They had been sitting for almost an hour. Father Angelo had been talking about a situation in the parish. He did not name names — he never did, which was one of the reasons people on Maple Lane felt comfortable talking to him. There had been, he said, a quarrel between two men who had been friends for a long time. The thing one had done was not the worst thing in the world. The thing the other had done in response was not the worst thing either. But neither would budge. Now there was a coldness between them that was beginning to extend to their wives, to the men they sat with at the Knights of Columbus on Thursdays, and that was how these things spread if they were not closed up.

“I keep telling them,” Angelo said, “that forgiveness is a gift. It is given freely. It is not a transaction. It does not require the other person to deserve it, or to ask for it, or to give anything in return. That's what makes it grace.”

He drank.

Aldric set his cup down. He thought for a moment. Not long, but long enough for the moment to be visible. “I agree, Father.”

“You do?”

“I do. I only think that balance is something else. Balance is not a return on a gift. It's a precondition. Without it, the gift has nowhere to land.”

Father Angelo looked at his coffee. He did not drink. He looked at his coffee for what Rosa counted as four full seconds, which was not long unless you were sitting in a kitchen with three people and one of them had just said something the other was working out.

Then he smiled. “That is an interesting distinction.”

“Thank you, Father.”

“I have to think about that.”

“Please do. I would be curious to hear what you decide.”

Father Angelo laughed his short surprised laugh again. He drank. The conversation moved on. Back to the boiler, briefly, and then to whether Mrs. Daugherty's refusal to use the elevator should be respected as autonomy or addressed as a safety issue, which was a question Father Angelo had been wrestling with for some months, and on which Aldric had no opinion he was willing to commit to.

Angelo left a little after eleven. He thanked Aldric again for the path. He put on his gloves carefully, one finger at a time, said his goodbyes to them both, and walked down the steps along the sidewalk toward the corner where his car was parked.

Rosa stayed another minute. She helped Aldric clear the cups.

“He's a good man,” she said.

“Yes. He is.”

“He likes you.”

“That is generous of him.”

“He invites everyone to Sunday service. He's been inviting me since we moved in.”

“And?”

“I keep thinking I'll go someday. I never go.”

Aldric set the cups in the sink and ran water over them. His back stayed to her when he answered. “You don't need to go for him to keep inviting. The inviting is the point. The going is your business.”

She thought about that for a second. “That's a kind way of saying I should stop feeling guilty about it.”

“It's the same thing, yes.”

She laughed. She put her coat back on. He walked her to the door, which he always did, held it open, which he always did, and watched her cross the strip of frozen grass back to her own driveway. She rarely noticed that part. She noticed it today.

At home the laundry was still waiting. She started a load. She put the radio on and while she was folding the things Dave had left in the dryer overnight, she thought about what Aldric had said.

*Balance is not a return on a gift. It's a precondition. Without it, the gift has nowhere to land.*

She turned that one over in her head while she paired socks. It was not how she would have put it. It was not how Father Angelo would have put it either. She was almost certain that Father Angelo was, even now, driving back toward St. Catherine's with that sentence sitting on the dashboard in front of him.

She told Dave about it that night when he came home. Marcus had cried about the goggles. Lina had made a friend whose name he could not remember. He was warm, tired and pleased with his afternoon. He listened as he always listened after chlorine and a sandwich, with his eyes half-closed, his head against the back of the couch.

“He said balance is a precondition,” she told him. “Not a gift in return. A precondition.”

“For what?”

“For forgiveness. For grace. Father Angelo was talking about grace as a gift you give without expecting anything back, and Aldric said yes, but you need balance first, or the gift doesn't go anywhere.”

Dave thought about that. He was not as sleepy as he looked.

“I don't know exactly why, but I like that,” he said.

“Somehow I do too.”

“Did Father Angelo like it?”

“I think Father Angelo is still deciding.”

Dave laughed. She put her feet up next to his as the dryer started chiming, muffled by the utility room's door. She did not get up right away.
