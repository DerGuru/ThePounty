# Epilogue: The Group Photo

*Saturday, the 21st of June 2025*

The print had come soon after the cleanup. Tamara Flynn had pushed an envelope through his mail slot with a sticky note on the front that said *one for you too, since you cut the boards.* He had put it in the drawer of the hall table and left it there on purpose. A photograph from March, framed in March, would have been a snapshot, and he wanted it to be more than that. He had a date in mind, as he had a date in mind for most things, and he had let the print wait in the drawer until the date arrived, because patience exercised toward a correct end was not the same as delay, however similar the two looked from the outside.

The date was today. The morning of the longest day, a year to the day since he had turned the Volvo onto Maple Lane. So he took the envelope to the mantelpiece and opened it.

It was a good photograph, for what it was. Tamara had taken four and chosen well. The whole street stood in front of the new bench in the noon light of a Saturday in late March, the sun low behind them. None of them was arranged as a photographer would arrange them and all of them arranged as a street actually stands. Ms. Yuen in the center of the bench because that was where she belonged and everyone had known it without a word. Marcus in front with his painted face. Sandra mid-sentence, because Sandra was always mid-sentence. Gerald at the end of the back row with the expression of a man being photographed against his better judgment. Dave with his arm half around Rosa. Maria, who had learned to paint a fence that year, stood in the photograph as though she had as much right to the day as anyone, which she did.

And at the end of the back row, beside Hector, a man in a gray cardigan who had let himself be put there.

He looked at that man for a while. The smile had come a half-second late, as it always did, and the camera had caught the half-second, so that he stood among them looking not quite finished, a man assembling an expression he had not yet completed. Nobody else would notice it. He noticed it. He had built that smile over twenty years and it still arrived a beat behind the others, and a photograph was an honest thing, and there it was.

He set the print into the frame.

He had bought the frame before he owned a single photograph to put in it, the week he arrived, and stood it empty on the mantel because an empty frame on a mantel was the kind of detail a careful man left for others to explain to themselves. They had explained it to themselves all year. Maria had thought of someone he had lost. Gerald had thought of it as evidence of something and written it down. Sandra had called it negative space. Rosa, once, had said it might be for a photograph he wanted to take here, of something that had not happened yet.

Rosa had been right. He had filed that, when she said it, with a small private acknowledgment that of all of them she saw the most and suspected the least, which was its own kind of intelligence.

*I haven't found the right one yet.* He had said that to Maria at the housewarming, in front of everyone, and meant it precisely, and let them take it for grief. It had not been grief. It had been a statement of fact about a photograph that did not exist because the street it would contain did not yet exist, not as a thing he was inside of. You could not photograph a place you had only just arrived in. You had to wait until it had closed around you, until you were one of the figures in the noon light and not the man holding the camera. That took a year. 

He centered the frame on the mantel. He stepped back and checked it from the angle the room offered a person coming in the front door. He adjusted it. Better.

The street was in the frame now. All of it. He stood in his living room a moment longer than the task required and allowed himself to look at the finished thing, which was a permission he did not often grant himself, because looking at finished things was how men came to believe they had finished. He had not finished. But the frame was full, and it was full of the correct photograph, and he had not expected, eleven hundred miles ago, that the correct photograph would have him in it. He had not expected to be in the picture. He had not expected to like it.

He had liked it. That was the part he turned over now, carefully, as he turned over everything. He had come here to do a thing, which required that he be a neighbor. He had been a neighbor with the whole of his attention because half-attention was discourteous and he did not do discourteous things. But somewhere in the past year, the performance and the man had stopped being possible to separate. The smile still came late. But it came.

That was worth knowing about himself. He filed it. He would examine it properly in December, when he examined everything.

Then he went and got the other thing.

It was upstairs, in the closet, behind the blanket that smelled of lavender, in the flat case he had carried in first the day he arrived and not opened in front of anyone since. He did not open it now. He took from the top of it a single folder, slim, unlabeled, the kind of plain binding a person might glance at on a porch in the evening and take for a library book. He had read it on the porch on many evenings, in Maria's cardigan, while the street told itself he was a man who liked to read outdoors. He knew it close to by heart. He took it out all the same. Some things a man looked at directly before he began, even when he had them by memory, because memory smoothed the edges a beginning wanted kept sharp.

He stood in the front room with the folder in his hand and the photograph on the mantel and let the two of them be in the room together, because they were the two halves of the year and it was honest to hold them in the same glance. The street, framed. The name, in the folder. One finished. One not begun.

He had carried the name eleven hundred miles. A dying man had given it to him, in a room he did not revisit even in thought because revisiting served no function, and the dying man had said *look at her,* and then he had said *do what you think is right,* and Aldric had said *I'll do,* which was a promise, and a promise given was permanent. That was the oldest rule. It predated all the others. The others were architecture. That one was foundation.

He had found her in May, though he had been nearly sure since the winter, since a priest two streets over had mentioned a young woman at the back of his church who was polite and observant and said very little, and reminded him, the priest had said, a little of Aldric himself. Aldric had filed it with no change in his face and finished the conversation about a boiler, and gone to a service the following Sunday. Not for the service — for her. He had seen her, and been nearly sure. The months since had been confirmation. Confirmation was slow work and he had not rushed it. You did not rush a thing you intended to get right, and he intended to get this right, whatever right turned out to be.

He put everything back where it belonged, and picked up the car keys.

Outside, the morning was the kind June occasionally produced and people remembered all winter, clean light and a sky without complaint. He walked to the Volvo. Gerald's blinds were at their angle across the street, and Aldric lifted a hand to them without looking, because a man watched from a window deserved acknowledgment as much as a man met on a path, and Gerald deserved it more than most for his persistence, which Aldric respected as he respected anything done thoroughly and without reward. The blinds did not move. That was all right. The wave was not for an answer.

He drove out under the limit. Past the playground at the end, two swings and a slide and a climbing frame, the street ending in an invitation, as it had on the day he arrived. Left at the corner. Then the few minutes of ordinary roads that separated his street from hers, which were not many, because the spool of the whole long thread had wound down, in the end, to a distance a man could cover before his coffee went cold.

He parked well back, where a gray Volvo was no kind of event, and walked the rest of the way to the small park, where the farmers' market was held on Saturday mornings, early tomatoes and salad greens on trestle tables, a man selling bread from the back of a van. He bought nothing. He stood near the bread van, which gave a man a reason to stand, and he looked across the stalls, and he found her without effort because he had known where to find her for a month now.

She was at the vegetable stall. Mid-thirties. Unremarkable, which on her was its own kind of remarkable, dressed to be unseen and managing it, for everyone but him. She was buying tomatoes. She picked them up one at a time and she was not, he saw, choosing them the way the woman beside her chose them, by color, by give. She was watching the man behind the stall. She had caught him short-weighting the customer ahead of her, and she was deciding what to do about it, and she had not yet decided, and nothing in her face showed any of it.

He knew that stillness. He had worn it at a market stall himself, in the autumn, over twenty-five cents and a man named Ray. He had a rule for it. She, he suspected, did not. She had the capacity and the wiring and the eye, and no architecture around any of it, nothing to tell her where the line was, only the same machine he had been born with running without a fence around it.

*Look at her.*

He was looking. He had looked for a year. The dying man had not said what he was meant to see, and Aldric saw, now, what there was to see, which was a person built the way he was built standing at the edge of a small decision with no rule to catch her, and behind that small decision a life full of larger ones, coming toward her as they came toward everyone, except that she would meet them with his equipment and not theirs.

She paid for the tomatoes. She said something to the stallholder, quiet, pleasant, and the man's face did something complicated, and she moved on down the green to the fruit stall, where the first cherries had come in beside the last of the strawberries. He followed at the edge of the green, a slow drift that kept her in sight without ever closing on her. He watched her turn the cherries over with the same care she had given the scale, buying nothing, her hands busy so the rest of her could be still. He would not approach. Today was not for approaching, only for looking. He was in no hurry. Hurry was how you got things wrong.

He had found her. The search was over. That was one thing closed, and he allowed himself to mark it closed as he had allowed himself to look at the photograph, briefly, and then set down.

The other thing had not begun. He could feel the size of it, the way you felt the weight of a case before you lifted it, and he did not yet know what he would do. He did not yet know enough, and a decision made before knowledge was just a preference wearing a decision's coat.

He would learn her. He would understand her. And then he would do what he thought was right, whatever that proved to be, and he would hold the promise as he held all of them, completely, because a word given was permanent and this one had been given to the only man who had ever seen him clearly and not minded what he saw.

He walked back to the Volvo and drove home. He made tea. He sat in the chair by the south window, angled toward the street but not facing it, visible but not ostentatious, a man at home on a Saturday. Across the road a curtain moved and was still. Down the street a sprinkler started. Somewhere a dog that had barked at everything for a year barked at something now, without much hope of result, and he found that he still liked the dog.
