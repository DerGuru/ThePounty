# POV Tone Examples — Uncommon Courtesy

*Gemeinsame Szene: Aldric sitzt abends auf seiner Veranda und poliert Schuhe. Ein unbekanntes Auto fährt langsam die Straße entlang.*

*Zweck: Tonvergleich der einzelnen Stimmen. Alle Beispiele zeigen dieselbe Situation — gefiltert durch das Wertesystem des jeweiligen POV.*

---

## 1. Dave Ortega

*Beobachtung → Einordnung → Unsicherheit. Praktisch. Kurze Sätze. Denkt nicht zu weit.*

---

Dave was taking the trash out when he saw Aldric on the porch. Two shoes were lined up on a piece of newspaper, laces pulled out and coiled beside them like something from a museum display. Aldric was working a brush in small circles, the kind of circles that suggested he'd done this a thousand times and never once considered not doing it.

They were already clean. Dave could see that from here. The shoes were already clean, and Aldric was cleaning them anyway.

"Evening," Dave called.

Aldric looked up. Smiled. "Good evening, Dave. How are the kids?"

"Loud. As usual."

"A good kind of loud, I hope."

"That's still in debate." Dave set the trash can at the curb and wiped his hands on his jeans. He considered going back inside, but didn't. For some reason, he stayed.

A car turned onto Maple Lane. Slow. Too slow for someone who lived here. Dave watched it pass under the streetlight. It was a dark sedan with tinted windows. No turn signal. It rolled past Aldric's house, past the Hoopers', and turned right at the end of the block.

Dave looked at Aldric. Aldric was looking at the shoe in his hand. The brush hadn't stopped moving.

"Know them?" Dave said.

"Hm?"

"The car."

"I didn't notice a car." Aldric held the shoe up to the porch light, tilted it, found something invisible that displeased him, and went back to brushing. "Would you like some coffee? I made more than I need."

"Sure, why not." Dave said. He went up the porch steps and sat in the second chair. The coffee was already poured. Two cups were waiting. One for him and one for Aldric.

He didn't ask about the two cups. Aldric didn't explain the two cups. The coffee was good. And Aldric's shoes were very clean.

## 2. Rosa Ortega

*Beobachtung → Fürsorge → Instinkt. Warm, detailreich. Bemerkt was fehlt, nicht was da ist.*

---

Rosa was folding laundry by the upstairs window when she noticed Aldric. The lamp on his porch was on, the yellow one he'd bought three weeks after moving in, after asking her opinion on the color. She'd said warm. He'd said warm, thank you, and bought exactly that one.

He was polishing shoes. Not the ones he wore every day. The other pair. The ones she'd only seen twice, both times when he left early in the morning and came back after dark.

His hands moved with something that looked like care but wasn't. Care meant thinking about the object. This seemed different. More like maintenance. The way Dave cleaned his grill before winter. Not because he loved the grill, but because that was what you did.

She folded a towel. Then folded another.

A car passed below. She wouldn't have noticed it at all, but Aldric's hand stopped. Just for a breath. The brush paused mid-stroke, and then it moved again, same rhythm, same circles, like nothing had interrupted them.

Rosa set the towel down.

Aldric was smiling at nothing. The porch. The evening. The shoes that were already clean twenty minutes ago. He looked like a man enjoying his evening. He looked like a man with nothing in the world to worry about.

His shoulders hadn't moved. Not once. Not when the car passed, not when it had changed direction. They were set. Held in place, as if all he really wanted to do was turn around.

Rosa closed the curtain and went back to the laundry. She told herself it was nothing. Told herself that lots of people polished shoes at nine in the evening and lots of people noticed cars without looking up.

She folded the last towel. When it didn't sit right, she refolded it.

## 3. Gerald Hooper

*Beobachtung → Verdacht → Notiz. Listen. Inventarisierung. Denkt wie ein schlecht kalibrierter Detektiv.*

---

9:14 PM. Porch light on. Again.

Gerald stood at his study window with the blinds angled down — fifteen degrees, enough to see without being seen from the street. The notebook was on the desk. Open. Pen uncapped.

Venn was polishing shoes. Not the loafers. The other ones. Black. Leather. Hard sole. Gerald had seen them exactly twice before: March 12th, on Venn's morning departure 6:40 AM, return 11:22 PM. April 3rd, same pattern. He wrote those dates from memory. They were already in the notebook.

The shoes were derby-style. Cap-toe. Not expensive in a flashy way. Expensive in the way that people who know shoes recognize and nobody else does. They were the kind of shoes you wore when you wanted to walk a long time without anyone noticing your shoes.

He wrote: *Shoe care. Third time visible. Shoes worn only on long-absence days. Prep or maintenance?*

A car entered the street. Dark sedan. Gerald's pen stopped.

It came from the east end. Slow. Under twenty. There was no reason to go that slow unless you were looking for an address or already knew the address and were looking at something else.

Gerald watched Venn. Venn didn't look up. The brush kept moving. That was wrong. Everyone looks at a slow car on a quiet street. It's instinct. You look, you dismiss, you go back to what you were doing. Normal.

Venn didn't look.

The car turned right. And was gone again.

Gerald wrote: *Dark sedan. Possibly Camry or Accord. Tinted rear. No plate visible from this angle. Passed at 9:17. Speed: ~15mph. Venn did NOT look up. Noted.*

He underlined the last two words. Then underlined them again.

Below, Venn put down the shoe, picked up the cup beside him, and drank. He looked perfectly relaxed. He looked like a man who had never worried about anything in his life.

Gerald capped the pen. He was definitely not relaxed.

## 4. Sandra Hooper

*Aktiv, etwas aufgeregt, neugierig. Denkt in Verdächtigungen, die sich nicht bewahrheiten. Komik-Linse.*

---

Sandra was absolutely not spying. She was watering the begonias. The fact that the begonias were on the side of the house that faced Aldric's porch was just a coincidence.

He was polishing shoes. At nine at night. Outside. On his porch. Where anyone could see.

Normal people polished shoes inside. In a closet. Or on top of newspaper in a laundry room. Not outside under a lamp like it was some kind of performance. Unless it *was* a performance. Unless it was a signal.

Sandra had read an article about that once. Signals. People who left certain objects on their porch to communicate with— no. That was plant pots. Plant pots turned certain ways. Not shoes. Probably not shoes.

The brush made little circles. She could hear it from here. *Shff. Shff. Shff.* Rhythmic. Calming. Like a serial killer's playlist.

She was *not* spying.

A car drove past. Slow. Very slow. Sandra's thumb slipped off the hose nozzle and sprayed the wall. She recovered and wiped her hand on her shorts. She looked at Aldric. He hadn't moved. Hadn't flinched. Hadn't even glanced.

The car was gone.

"Gerald!" she hissed through the kitchen window. "Gerald, did you see that?"

Nothing.

"GERALD."

"What." His voice came from the study. Flat.

"A creepy car just drove past."

"I know."

"You *know*?"

"I'm working."

Sandra watered the begonias again. They were already drowning now, but she didn't care. Something was happening on this street and nobody was taking it seriously except her.

She made a mental note to casually bring it up at the book club. Tanya would know what a slow car meant. Tanya watched true crime.

## 5. Ms. Yuen

*Beobachtung → Muster → Geduld. Wenige Worte. Ruhig. Gewicht in jedem Satz.*

---

The young man was preparing.

Ms. Yuen had been walking this street for eleven years. She knew what preparation looked like. Her husband had done it differently — sharpening knives before a dinner he'd cook the next day, ironing shirts on Sundays for weeks that hadn't started yet — but the shape was the same. You always saw it in the hands first.

Aldric Venn sat on his porch with his shoes on newspaper. Good posture. Good light. Good hands. He moved the brush like her husband had moved the knife: without thinking. Muscle and habit and years.

Those were not everyday shoes.

A car passed. She was at the end of the block by then, coming back. It moved like someone was looking for something. She had seen that too, before. In other decades. In other countries.

Aldric's hands didn't stop. His shoulders didn't change. His breathing kept steady. That mouth was almost smiling, the way it always was.

His weight had shifted, though. The left foot pressed flat on the porch floor. The right one, which had been crossed over his knee, was now beside it. Both feet down. Both flat.

You would need to have been watching for six months to notice that. Ms. Yuen had been watching for six months.

She walked past his house. He looked up.

"Good evening, Ms. Yuen. How is the knee?"

"It's better, thank you," she said.

"I'm glad to hear that."

She nodded and walked on. She did not look back.

He was a dangerous man who had decided to be kind. That was not the same as a kind man. But it was close enough for Maple Lane. It was close enough for Ms. Yuen.

## 6. Officer Kim

*Beobachtung → Risikobewertung → Pflicht. Präzise, professionell. Denkt in Lücken und Abweichungen.*

---

Kim drove past Maple Lane at 9:18 on her way home. She told herself it was just the route home, even though it was a little detour. She told herself that she wasn't looking for anything, even though she was looking at everything. The street was quiet. The sprinklers were on at the Hoopers'. A dog was barking.

The subject — the *resident*, she corrected herself — was seated on his front porch, maintaining shoes. She saw two shoes, a newspaper, a brush, a tin (likely polish) and a ceramic mug on his left, steam forming above it. He had started recently or interrupted his maintenance for a cup of coffee, which was less likely. His body language was relaxed. His posture open. He was facing southwest, toward the street.

Kim noted all of this in the time it took to pass at twenty-five miles per hour. That was training. Not suspicion, she told herself. Training.

His porch light was a warm 2700K. She'd noticed it before. Inviting. Not concealing. A man who sat in shadow was hiding. A man who sat in good light wanted to be seen. Or wanted you to think he wanted to be seen.

Kim checked her mirror. Another vehicle had entered the street. A dark sedan, two hundred yards behind her. It was moving slow. Very much under the limit, which was not illegal, but not normal either.

She looked at the porch again in the mirror. Venn was watching her taillights. No — watching past her taillights. He was watching the sedan. Just for a moment and then he immediately went back to the shoes.

That moment was enough. It looked like a threat assessment. She'd just done the same thing in the same amount of time.

Kim pulled left at the end of the block, when her normal turn would have been right.

She drove around the block, which took four minutes. When she came back, the sedan was gone. Venn was still on the porch. Still polishing. He raised his hand when her headlights swept across the lawn.

A wave. Friendly. Normal.

She waved back and drove home. 

She still thought about it when she went to bed thirty minutes later.

A man polished shoes. A car drove by. A police officer took the long way home. Nothing.

## 7. Hired Muscle

*Professionell, sachlich, leicht gelangweilt. Unterschätzt. Denkt in Jobs und Aufwand.*

---

The target was sitting on his porch, doing something with shoes. Polishing, it looked like. At night. On the porch. In the open. What a loser.

The man in the dark sedan — he didn't use names on jobs as a rule, not even his own — shifted his weight and checked his mirror again. Empty street. Quiet neighborhood. Sprinklers three houses down. A woman walking a block away, elderly, not a concern.

He'd done twelve of these in nine years. Debt collection at a distance. Show up, make the point, leave. Sometimes the point was a conversation. Sometimes it was less of a conversation. What happened depended on the client's instructions and the target's attitude.

This one was supposed to be simple. Suburban. Single male. No visible security. No dog. The file said *former consultant* and gave an address. That was usually enough.

He rolled past at fifteen. Getting the layout. Counting exits. Front door, side door visible from this angle, probably a back door. A wooden fence, two feet, decorative. No cameras. No motion lights. Just the porch lamp and the guy with his shoes.

The target didn't look up.

That was— fine. People didn't always notice. In suburbs like this, cars came and went. Nothing unusual about a sedan on a residential street.

He turned right and parked two blocks over. He made a note on his phone: *Two stories. Middle lot. Low fence. Porch light. Target visible 2114. No visible weapon. No awareness.*

He locked the car and made his way back to the motel. Sixteen minutes on foot. Tomorrow he'd do a day pass. See the routine. Garbage day, mail schedule, when the neighbors were out. Standard prep. Three days, maybe four, then he would have a conversation with Mr. Venn.

Shouldn't take a week.

He ordered room service and didn't think about the man on the porch again.

He should have.

## 8. Aldric Venn (Prolog/Epilog only)

*Beobachtung → Bewertung → Regel. Kalt-präzise, konstruiert, systematisch. Intern anders als extern.*

---

The left shoe was done. The right one had a scuff on the instep that would take another minute. Acceptable. He adjusted the newspaper — the corner had folded under the sole, and asymmetry in the workspace bothered him.

9:14. Mrs. Yuen would pass in three minutes, give or take some seconds for the knee. Dave's garbage was not at the curb yet. It would be soon. Rosa's upstairs light was on. Laundry night. Gerald's study blinds were angled, with only thin slits of light between them, which meant Gerald was watching. Nothing new here. Gerald was always watching.

*Good.*

The car came from the east. A dark sedan with tinted rear windows. Fourteen, fifteen miles per hour. It wasn't lost — a lost driver slows and accelerates, scanning house numbers. This one maintained constant speed. That seemed deliberate. An assessment pass.

Aldric didn't look up. Looking up was information. Looking up said: *I noticed you. I consider you noteworthy.* Neither message was useful at this stage.

He set his right foot down, next to his left, redistributed his weight to the balls of both feet, and continued the brush circles without breaking rhythm. Under a second to stand from this position. Acceptable.

The sedan passed. It sounded like a four-cylinder, well-maintained. No exhaust rattle. Possibly a rental or recent purchase. Purring tires, even at this speed. That meant all-season, standard, not performance. A single silhouette was sitting on the driver seat, left hand on the wheel, right hand below the window line — phone, armrest or a concealed weapon.

*Amateur,* he thought. No, not that word. *Unaware* was the better choice. The man in the car thought he was observing, while he was being catalogued.

Aldric held the shoe to the light. Found the scuff and worked to remove it in four strokes.

Mrs. Yuen was approaching. Right on time.

"Good evening, Ms. Yuen. How is the knee?"

The smile was ready. The smile was always ready. That was what the rules were for.

## 9. Maria Alvarez

*Beobachtung → Selbstzweifel → stille Bewunderung. Unsicher, selbstkritisch. Sieht, was andere können, und misst sich daran.*

---

Maria was on her porch with a cup of chamomile that had gone cold twenty minutes ago. She'd meant to go back inside, heat it up, maybe start a new row on the cardigan she was knitting for a customer in Portland. She hadn't moved. Moving required deciding to move, and decisions had been hard lately.

Aldric was doing something on his porch. She could see him from here, two houses down, past the dark windows of number six. His porch lamp was on, that warm one he'd put up. A shape, hunched slightly forward, arms moving in small repeated motions. Polishing shoes, maybe. Or cleaning something. Whatever it was, he'd been at it for a while. The rhythm hadn't changed once. Like he knew exactly how long it would take and had already set aside the time. Like maintenance was a thing that lived in a schedule, not something you forgot about until company was coming and then panicked.

She used to polish Carlos's shoes. Sunday nights, in the bathroom, with the radio on. It was one of those things she'd done without thinking, part of the machinery of a shared life. She was good at it. She was good at all of it — the laundry, the ironing, the meals, the groceries. The house ran because she ran it. What she couldn't do was fix the house itself. The dripping faucet, the fence, the shelf that needed a drill and a wall plug. Those had been Carlos' jobs. There were a lot of things like that. And the list kept getting longer.

She watched the shape on the porch. Steady. Unhurried. No pausing, no getting up to check something, no second-guessing. Just the quiet certainty of a man who knew what he was doing and didn't need anyone else in the room to confirm it.

She envied that. She envied it so much it sat in her chest like a stone.

A car passed. Slow. She noticed because the street was usually dead by nine. Dark, tinted windows. She watched it roll past Aldric's house and thought nothing about it. Then she went back to watching Aldric and the not drinking her tea. She knew, she was supposed to be knitting, but couldn't bring herself to start.

Aldric was still polishing with the same steady rhythm. Keeping the same posture. The car hadn't existed for him. Or if it had, it hadn't mattered. Either way, he hadn't flinched, hadn't looked, hadn't changed what he was doing by a single degree. She couldn't imagine that. Being so settled inside yourself that the world could pass by and you didn't even look up.

Five months ago, Carlos had left and everything had changed. The house hadn't changed. The street hadn't changed. She had changed. Or maybe she hadn't changed at all, and that was the problem — she was still the same woman, just without the person who made it look like she was doing fine.

Maria picked up the cup. It was still cold. She went inside to heat it up. She did not start the cardigan. She sat at the kitchen table instead and looked at the wall where the wedding photo used to hang. The nail was still there. She should take it out. She should patch the hole. She should learn how to patch holes.

Tomorrow. Maybe tomorrow.
