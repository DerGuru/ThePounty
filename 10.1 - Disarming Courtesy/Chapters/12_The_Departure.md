# Chapter 12: The Departure

*Thursday, the 19th of September 2024*

The kettle clicked off at five-forty. Ms. Yuen poured the water over the leaves and set the timer on the counter. Three minutes. She carried the cup to the front window. She stood there in the gray light waiting for the tea and the morning to arrive together.

Maple Lane was quiet. The streetlamps were still on, their orange glow thinning as the sky lightened behind the rooftops. No cars. No sprinklers. The Ortegas' porch light was off, which meant Rosa had remembered to switch it this time. The Hoopers' newspaper sat on their driveway in its plastic sleeve, white against the dark concrete. No one was awake. Ms. Yuen was always the first one awake.

She liked this hour. The street was her's for a while before it belonged to everyone else. She knew the sounds: the Chens' cat crossing the road at exactly this time, the click of the automatic sprinkler at number twelve starting its cycle. She knew what the street looked like when it was just itself, before the people filled it with noise and intention.

The timer beeped. She went back to the kitchen, removed the strainer and brought the cup to the window again. The tea was still too hot, so she held it under her nose and let the steam rise.

She inhaled deeply, letting the aroma fill her senses. A door opened across the street.

Ms. Yuen did not move. She watched over the rim of her cup.

Aldric Venn came out of number four. He pulled the door shut behind him and tested the handle once. He was carrying a small bag. Dark canvas, no wheels, the kind that held two days of clothing and nothing more. He wore a jacket she had not seen before. Dark, fitted, no collar. Not the cardigan. Not the shirt he wore to mow the lawn. This was a different man's clothing on the same man's body.

He walked to his car. His steps were even and unhurried, but there was nothing casual in them. Each one landed where it meant to land. His head did not turn to look at the street or at the neighbor's garden. He looked at the car. He reached the car. He opened the trunk and placed the bag inside with one hand. Closed it.

Ms. Yuen had seen men move like that before. Not often. Twice in her life that she could name, and once more that she would not. Her husband had moved like that on mornings she was not supposed to remember. Before he retired. Before the knee and the garden and the slow years.

It was the absence of waste. No extra movement. No adjustment, no hesitation, no looking back to check. Every action completed on the first attempt, in the order it was needed. A person did not learn that by practicing. A person learned that by needing it to be true.

Aldric walked around to the driver's side. His keys were already in his hand. He had not taken them out. They had been there since the door.

Dave Ortega's garage door opened.

The sound carried in the still morning, the mechanical rattle cutting across the quiet street. Dave appeared in his driveway in gym shorts and running shoes, phone strapped to his arm. He saw Aldric and raised a hand.

“Hey. Early start.”

Ms. Yuen watched the shift. It was visible, if you knew where to look. Aldric's shoulders moved back. His chin lifted. The line of his mouth changed. He turned, and when he faced Dave, he was the man from the porch again. The one who asked about the children and brought cookies at Christmas and waved at every passing car.

“Good morning, Dave.” The smile was there. Full, warm, present. “Just a short trip. Business. I should be back by Saturday.”

“Need anything while you're gone? I can grab the mail.”

“That would be very kind. Thank you.” Aldric opened the car door. “Give my best to Rosa and the children.”

“Will do. Safe travels.”

“Thank you.”

He got in. The door closed. The engine started, quiet and low. The headlights came on. The green Volvo pulled out of the driveway, turned left at the end of the street, and was gone.

Dave stood in his driveway for a moment. He looked at the empty space where the car had been. Then he put his earbuds in, stretched his calves against the curb and started running toward the park.

Ms. Yuen took a sip of her tea. It had cooled to the right temperature.

She thought about the jacket. The bag. The way his body had been one thing and then, when Dave appeared, had become another thing entirely. Not false. Not performed. More like a translation. The same meaning in a different language, spoken fluently enough that most people would not hear the accent.

Most people.

She finished her tea standing at the window. The street filled slowly. The Chens' cat came back across the road. Gerald's newspaper stayed on the driveway until seven-fifteen, when Gerald appeared in his robe and slippers to collect it. The sprinkler at number twelve finished its cycle. The sky went from gray to blue.

At six-twenty, she washed her cup, dried it and put it back in the cupboard. She walked to the hallway closet where she kept her walking shoes and put them on, one at a time, sitting on the bench by the door. Her knee was better today. That was something.

She opened the front door and stepped outside into the cool morning. The air smelled like dew and jasmine. The street was ordinary. Two joggers she did not recognize passed at the far end. A mockingbird was repeating itself in the Hoopers' magnolia.

Ms. Yuen began her walk. She did not look at number four as she passed it. The driveway was empty. The porch was dark. The house looked like a house where no one lived, which was not unusual for six-thirty in the morning, except that she had seen him leave, and she knew what leaving looked like when it was not just leaving.

Her husband had come back every time. Thirty-one years, every time. She had never asked where he went. He had never explained. They had built an entire marriage in the space around that silence, and it had been a good marriage. A true one.

She reached the end of the block and turned right toward the park. Her knee held. The morning was cool and clear, and she walked at her pace, which was slower than it used to be and faster than people expected.

Aldric Venn would come back. She was reasonably certain of that. Men who packed that lightly planned to come back.

It was the ones who packed nothing at all that you worried about.
