# Chapter 25: Gerald's Discovery

*Friday, the 29th of November 2024*

It started with a name.

Gerald wasn't looking. He'd stopped looking. Two weeks. Fourteen days of normal. The drawer stayed closed. The blinds stayed level. He slept through the night and didn't think about license plates or shoe polish or the exact time Venn's porch light came on.

Then Sandra's laptop froze.

It was Friday evening. She was in the kitchen, trying to download a pattern for her book club's holiday craft exchange. The screen locked. She called Gerald. He came, closed the frozen tabs, cleared the cache, restarted the browser. Standard. Routine. He was about to hand it back when he saw the search bar.

Empty. Cursor blinking.

He typed nothing. He closed the laptop and slid it across the counter.

That night, at eleven, he was in his study. His own computer. The screen reflected in his glasses. He told himself he was checking email. He checked email. Then he opened a new tab.

He typed: *Aldric Venn*

Google returned four results. A white pages listing with no associated address history. A mention in the Greenfield Township Newsletter from September. The HOA membership roster, posted publicly by mistake in July and taken down within a day. A duplicate of the white pages listing on a mirror site.

Four results.

Gerald leaned back. He took off his glasses and rubbed the bridge of his nose.

Four results for a forty-three-year-old man. In 2024. That was not possible. Not for someone who had lived a normal life. Not for someone who had worked, rented, bought groceries, owned a car, paid taxes, existed in the world for four decades.

He put his glasses back on. Opened LinkedIn. Searched the name directly.

Nothing.

Facebook.

Nothing.

Four results. Nothing else. No college. No employer. No alumni association. No marathon finisher list. No conference attendee list. No comment on a newspaper article. No old Yelp review. No forgotten MySpace page from 2006.

Gerald opened a new tab. Property records. The county assessor's website was public. He'd used it before, years ago, when the Weavers' house changed hands and he wanted to know what it sold for.

He searched by address. 4 Maple Lane.

The record came up. Owner: Aldric J. Venn. Purchase date: May 2024. Purchase price: $412,000. Mortgage: none. Paid in full.

Gerald wrote that down. On a receipt from the kitchen. Not the notebook. Not yet.

Previous owner: Blanchard Family Trust. Held since 1998. Before that: three other names going back to 1971.

He searched by name. Aldric Venn. The assessor's database showed one property. 4 Maple Lane. No previous addresses. No prior ownership in the county.

He tried the state database. Broader. Same result.

He tried the DMV lookup. The public version, the one that showed registration status. Venn's Volvo was registered in June 2024. No prior vehicle registrations under that name.

Gerald sat very still.

No prior vehicle. No prior address. No prior anything. The name Aldric Venn appeared in public records starting in May 2024 and nowhere before.

He checked the clock. 11:47.

He tried variations. A. Venn. Aldric without a last name. Venn without a first. Every combination produced noise. Other people. Other states. Nothing that connected to the man across the street.

He tried the voter registration database. Not registered. Not in this county. Not in this state. Not in any state that had a public lookup.

No voter registration. For a man who attended HOA meetings and talked about community engagement and knew the soil composition of his own street.

Gerald pulled open the drawer.

The notebook was where he'd left it. Black cover. Worn corners. Seventeen pages of observations that had felt embarrassing two weeks ago. That had felt paranoid. That had felt like the product of a bored retiree with too much time and too little to do.

He opened it to page eighteen.

He wrote the date. November 29, 2024.

He wrote: *The name Aldric Venn does not exist before May 2024.*

He underlined it. Stared at it.

He wrote: *No LinkedIn. No Facebook. No prior address. No voter registration. No vehicle history. No employment record. No digital footprint of any kind before this year.*

He put the pen down. Picked it up again.

*A man with no past moved to Maple Lane five months ago. He paid cash for a house. He owns a car registered to a name that didn't exist a year ago. He left for two days in September and came back as if he had never gone. He researches local history in the public library.*

*I was not wrong.*

Gerald closed the notebook. Looked at it. Seventeen pages of observations, and then this. The seventeenth page was the most embarrassing thing he'd ever written. The eighteenth was the most frightening.

He opened the drawer again. Reached past the notebook. Under the pen tray, under the envelopes, there was a second notebook. New. Blue cover. He'd bought it three months ago for no reason he could explain to Sandra. He'd never written in it.

He put the black notebook back. Took out the blue one.

He opened it to the first page. Clean. Unmarked. He clicked the pen.

He wrote: *VENN — Book Two*

Below that: *What I know. What I don't. What I need to find out.*

He made three columns. He filled the first one in twelve minutes. It was long. Every observation from the black notebook, compressed into bullet points. Times, dates, patterns, details.

The second column was longer.

The third column had one entry: *Who was he before?*

At 12:30, Gerald turned off the desk lamp. He put the blue notebook in the drawer. Not on top of the black one. Behind it. Under the envelopes. Where Sandra wouldn't find it while looking for stamps.

He went to bed. Sandra was asleep. The house was dark. Across the street, Venn's porch light was off. That was normal. It went off at 10:15 most nights. Gerald knew that.

He lay in the dark and looked at the ceiling.

A history nerd. That's what he'd told Sandra. That's what he'd told himself. A history nerd with perfect posture, no LinkedIn, no past, a cash-purchased house, and shoes he polished before days he left early and came back late.

Gerald closed his eyes.

He did not sleep. Somewhere under the envelopes the blue notebook held a single question with no answer, and it would still be there in the morning.
