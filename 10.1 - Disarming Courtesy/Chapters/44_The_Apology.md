# Chapter 44: The Apology

*Sunday, the 20th of April 2025*

The little kids got the eggs and Lina got to watch, which was the deal when you turned ten and your brother was six.

She did not really mind. The park at the end of Maple Lane had been roped off with plastic ribbon since Friday. Mrs. Henderson and Sandra had hidden the eggs that morning before church, which meant half of them sat in places a six-year-old would never look and the other half sat in the open grass like the people who hid them had run out of ideas. Marcus had a basket already too heavy to swing. He ran from one plastic egg to the next with his church shoes still on. Every time he found one he held it up and yelled “LINA” like she might have missed it.

“I see it,” she called back. “Good one.”

It was warm for April. The grass was that bright new green that only lasted a couple of weeks before it went regular green. Out in the middle of it someone had stood a big plush Easter rabbit, almost as tall as Marcus, tipping a little further to one side as the morning went on. Somebody's speaker was playing the kind of music parents thought was fun. There were folding tables with deviled eggs and a coffee thing Sandra had brought, set up by the benches at the basketball court. Most of the street was there, standing around in their good clothes, holding paper cups and talking about nothing.
Mr. Venn had come with them. Mom had asked him at the mailbox on Thursday and he had said he would be honored, which was how Mr. Venn said yes. He had a basket too, which was funny, because he didn't have any kids. He carried it for Marcus's overflow. Marcus thought this was the best job anyone had ever been given.

Lina drifted. That was the good part of being ten. You could drift.

She was near the coffee table, not getting coffee, when she noticed Mr. Venn talking to Mr. Chen by the fence. Nothing strange about that. But Mr. Chen had his head tipped like grown-ups do when someone surprises them, and Mr. Venn had his hand a little bit out. Lina caught three words on the warm air before the speaker swallowed them again.

“—sorry for the—”

Then Mr. Chen was shaking his head and saying no, no, it was fine, like you do when someone apologizes for something you didn't even know was a thing.

Lina filed it away. She was good at filing things away. Mr. Venn had taught her that without meaning to, back in the fall, when he fixed the Aiden problem by talking to people in a hallway.

She found Emma for a while and they ate deviled eggs and talked about whether Mrs. Hendricks was going to make them do the state report over spring break. Then Emma's mom called her, and Lina was drifting again, and she saw Mr. Venn over by the basketball court, talking to Mrs. Solis with the twins hanging off her like backpacks.

He had his hand out the same little bit. His face was doing the serious thing.

Mrs. Solis laughed, the laugh grown-ups use when they don't understand the question. She said something. Mr. Venn nodded, like she had given him exactly the answer he expected, and he moved on.

To Maria. Lina watched him say something to Maria, low, just to her. Maria did not laugh. Maria looked at him for a second too long and then looked down at her cup, and Mr. Venn touched the top of the fence post once with two fingers, like he was checking it was still there, and went on.

That was three. Lina did not have Gerald's word for it, but she knew three of anything was the start of something. Three meant a pattern. Three eggs in the same bush meant Marcus had found the lazy hider's spot.

She went and found him.

He was over by the bushes at the edge of the grass, where it grew longer because the mower couldn't get in close. He had set Marcus's heavy basket down and was crouched, putting a cracked egg back together so the candy wouldn't fall out, his church coat pulled tight across his back.

“Mr. Venn.”

“Lina.” He didn't look up right away. He got the egg closed, set it in the basket, and then he looked up, and the smile came on like it always did. “Are you having a good Easter?”

“Why do you keep saying sorry to everyone?”

His hands went still on his knees. Just for a second. She knew that second. She had seen it in the school parking lot in October, the half-breath before something underneath came up to the surface. But this time nothing hard came up. He stayed crouched, so his face was level with hers, which most grown-ups never bothered to do.

“That's a good question,” he said. He said it like he meant it was a good question, not like a grown-up getting out of one. “You were watching.”

“I watch stuff.”

“You do. You always have.” He looked out at the park for a moment, at Marcus charging a bush, at the tables, at the whole bright warm street of people in their good clothes. “I find that when I've lived somewhere a while, and people have been kind to me, it's polite to thank them for it. And sometimes to say sorry in advance, for any trouble I might be, before I get the chance to be any.”

“You're not trouble,” Lina said. “You made Aiden stop. You made school okay again.”

Something moved in his face that she did not have a name for. It was gone fast.

“That's very kind of you to say.” He picked up Marcus's basket and stood, unfolding all the way up, no hand on his knee. “Will you keep an eye on your brother's haul? He's about to lose the back third of it in the grass and he'll be heartbroken at lunch.”

It was a real question and it was also not an answer, and Lina was ten, which was old enough to know the difference but young enough to not stop. She took the basket.

“You didn't say what you're sorry for,” she said.

“No,” Mr. Venn agreed. “I didn't.” And he smiled at her, warm, like she had won something, and that was the end of it.

Ms. Yuen came along the path then.

She always took her walk at the same time, and the egg hunt hadn't changed it. She came down from the east end with her coat on even though it was warm, her hands behind her back, slow and straight. She did not come for coffee. She came past, like she came past everything, like she was checking the street was still where she left it.

Mr. Venn stepped over to her. Lina was close enough this time. She had the basket and she wasn't going anywhere.

“Ms. Yuen. Happy Easter.” He did the little bow that wasn't really a bow. “I wanted to tell you I'm sorry for any disturbance I may—”

“No.”

Ms. Yuen said it quietly. She was looking up at him the way she looked at everyone, the long look, the one most people looked away from. Mr. Venn did not look away.

“Not to me,” Ms. Yuen said. “I've seen it before. You do what you need to do.” She looked at him a moment longer. “Then mind you come back.”

Mr. Venn didn't say anything. The smile stayed where it was, but the rest of him went very still, stiller than the parking lot, stiller than Lina had ever seen a person stand at a party in the sun. He bowed his head, slower this time, more real.

“Yes,” he said. “Thank you.”

Ms. Yuen nodded once, the nod that ended things, and walked on toward the rabbit on the grass where Marcus was. She said something to Marcus that made him laugh. Already the strange part was folding itself away.

But Lina stood there with her brother's basket of eggs, in the warm grass, in her good Easter dress, and she felt cold.

She didn't understand what Ms. Yuen meant. *Come back* from where. Mr. Venn lived right there. Number four. You could see his porch from here. He wasn't going anywhere.

“Are you moving away?” she asked. “Or going somewhere?”

He looked at her, and the warmth was already back in his face, the regular smile, as if the stillness from a moment ago had never been there at all. He crouched down to her again. The answer came right away, with nothing held behind it.

“No,” he said. “I have no intention of leaving Maple Lane.”

She believed him. He said it plain and true, every word. It did not help at all, and she could not have said why it didn't.

And the sorry was wrong. That was the part she kept circling, the way her tongue kept finding a loose tooth. *Sorry* was what Aiden had to say after Mr. Venn made his dad get small. *Sorry* was the thing that came at the end, after the bad part, when the bad part was over. Mr. Venn was saying it without having done anything wrong. And he was saying it on a sunny Sunday with eggs in the grass, to people who hadn't done anything and didn't know anything. And he smiled while he did it.

She looked back over at him. He was holding the basket for Marcus, nodding at something Dad was saying, the nicest man on the street. He was still smiling.

In October the scary thing had been his smile going away. Today he would not stop smiling, and that scared her too, and she could not work out how it could be both. Lina did not have the word for it.

“LINA!” Marcus came pelting across the grass, basket banging his knees, an egg in each fist. “I got the gold one! Mrs. Henderson said the gold one's the best one!”

“That's great, buddy.” She crouched down and helped him fit the gold egg in with the others, where it wouldn't fall. “That's really great. Don't lose it.”

She kept his eggs for him the rest of the morning. It was the job she'd been given. But she kept checking where Mr. Venn was, over by the tables, the same way she checked on Marcus in a crowd. Not scared, exactly. She just couldn't stop making sure he was still there.
